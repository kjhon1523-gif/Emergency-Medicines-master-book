0 Introduction: The Breath of Life

‚ö†Ô∏è TIME IS OXYGEN: For every minute of hypoxia, brain cells die. Respiratory emergencies require immediate recognition and intervention. Never delay treatment while waiting for investigations.
üß† Core Concept: Respiratory emergencies involve two main pathologies:
1. Airway Obstruction (asthma, anaphylaxis, foreign body)
2. Gas Exchange Impairment (pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary edema)
This module focuses on obstructive emergencies and their pharmacological management.

1 Severe Asthma & Status Asthmaticus

Status Asthmaticus: A severe, life-threatening asthma exacerbation that fails to respond to conventional therapy with beta-agonists and systemic corticosteroids. Characterized by progressive respiratory distress leading to respiratory failure.

1.2 Asthma Management Algorithm

STEP 1: ASSESS & OXYGENATE
‚Ä¢ High-flow oxygen (target SpO‚ÇÇ 94-98%)
‚Ä¢ Assess severity: Speech, work of breathing, auscultation
‚Ä¢ Peak flow if possible
‚Ä¢ ABG if severe

‚Ä¢ Salbutamol 5 mg nebulized + Ipratropium 0.5 mg (back-to-back)

‚Ä¢ Prepare for intubation if deteriorating

2 Core Drug 1: Adrenaline (Asthma/Anaphylaxis Focus)

Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Alpha-1 (Œ±‚ÇÅ): Vasoconstriction ‚Üí reduces mucosal edema, increases BP
‚Ä¢ Beta-1 (Œ≤‚ÇÅ): Increases heart rate and contractility

‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema

‚Ä¢ TCAs, MAOIs: Potentiate effects ‚Üí hypertensive crisis

‚Ä¢ Ergot alkaloids: Extreme vasoconstriction

3 Core Drug 2: Aminophylline

5 Core Drug 4: Dexamethasone

9 Anaphylaxis Management Protocol

9.1 Step-by-Step Anaphylaxis Management

STEP 1: RECOGNITION (Seconds)
‚Ä¢ Sudden onset (minutes to hours)
‚Ä¢ Skin/mucosal: Hives, itching, flushing, angioedema
‚Ä¢ Respiratory: Stridor, wheeze, dyspnea, hypoxemia
‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia, collapse
‚Ä¢ Gastrointestinal: Cramping, vomiting, diarrhea

STEP 2: IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (0-2 minutes)
‚Ä¢ Remove trigger if possible (stop IV medication, remove sting)
‚Ä¢ Call for HELP (crash team, senior support)
‚Ä¢ Position: Supine (or left lateral if pregnant)
‚Ä¢ High-flow oxygen: 15 L/min via non-rebreather mask
‚Ä¢ Assess airway: Prepare for intubation if stridor present

STEP 3: ADRENALINE (Within 5 minutes)
‚Ä¢ IM Adrenaline: 0.3-0.5 mg (1:1000) in anterolateral thigh
‚Ä¢ Repeat every 5-10 minutes if no improvement
‚Ä¢ IV Adrenaline: Only if refractory (0.1 mg of 1:10,000 over 5-10 min)

STEP 4: FLUID RESUSCITATION (Concurrent)
‚Ä¢ Wide-bore IV access (x2 if possible)
‚Ä¢ Normal saline: 500-1000 mL bolus (child: 20 mL/kg)
‚Ä¢ Repeat if hypotensive
‚Ä¢ Consider colloid if massive fluid requirement

STEP 5: SECOND-LINE MEDICATIONS (After adrenaline)
‚Ä¢ Chlorphenamine: 10 mg IV/IM (child: 0.2 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Hydrocortisone: 200 mg IV (child: 2-4 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Salbutamol neb: If bronchospasm prominent

STEP 6: REFRACTORY CASES (ICU management)
‚Ä¢ Adrenaline infusion: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Glucagon: 1-2 mg IV if on beta-blockers
‚Ä¢ Vasopressin: Consider if catecholamine-resistant
‚Ä¢ Methylene blue: For refractory vasodilation (rare)
‚Ä¢ ECMO: Consider for catastrophic cases

10 Asthma vs COPD: Emergency Management Differences

Key Difference: Asthma is reversible airway inflammation; COPD is progressive, not fully reversible airflow limitation. Emergency management overlaps but has important differences.
