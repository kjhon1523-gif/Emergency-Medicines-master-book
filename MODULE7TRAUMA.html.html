1.2 Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)

ü©∏ MASSIVE TRANSFUSION PROTOCOL (1:1:1 RATIO):
‚Ä¢ Definition: >10 units PRBCs in 24h OR >4 units in 1h OR replacement of entire blood volume
‚Ä¢ Ratio: 1 unit PRBCs : 1 unit FFP : 1 unit platelets (or 1 pack = 6 pooled units)

‚Ä¢ Use balanced solutions (PlasmaLyte, Hartmann's) if available

2 Core Drug 1: Tranexamic Acid (TXA)

TRANEXAMIC ACID (TXA) - ANTIFIBRINOLYTIC FOR TRAUMA & HEMORRHAGE

Drug class: Synthetic lysine analogue, antifibrinolytic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitively inhibits plasminogen activation to plasmin
‚Ä¢ At higher doses, non-competitively inhibits plasmin
‚Ä¢ Reduces breakdown of fibrin clots ‚Üí stabilizes clot formation
‚Ä¢ Reduces bleeding without increasing thrombotic risk at trauma doses
‚Ä¢ Also has anti-inflammatory properties (reduces complement activation)
‚Ä¢ Minimal effect on coagulation parameters (PT/INR, aPTT)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Trauma with significant hemorrhage or risk of significant hemorrhage
‚Ä¢ Post-partum hemorrhage (obstetric emergencies)
‚Ä¢ Major surgical bleeding (cardiac, orthopaedic, spinal surgery)
‚Ä¢ Hereditary angioedema (prophylaxis and treatment)
‚Ä¢ Menorrhagia (oral formulation)
‚Ä¢ Upper GI bleeding (emerging evidence)
‚Ä¢ Epistaxis refractory to packing
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Active thrombosis (DVT, PE, stroke within 30 days)
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to tranexamic acid
‚Ä¢ Subarachnoid hemorrhage (increased risk of cerebral ischemia)
‚Ä¢ Color vision disturbances (rare side effect, avoid if history)
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) - requires dose reduction
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use of factor IX complex or anti-inhibitor coagulant complex
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Trauma hemorrhage (CRASH-2 protocol): 1 g IV over 10 min, then 1 g IV over 8 hours
‚Ä¢ Massive hemorrhage: 1 g IV bolus, consider additional 1 g after 1 hour if ongoing bleed
‚Ä¢ Surgical bleeding: 10-20 mg/kg IV pre-incision, then 1 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Post-partum hemorrhage: 1 g IV over 10 min, may repeat after 30 min if needed
‚Ä¢ Oral (menorrhagia): 1-1.5 g TID-QID during menses
‚Ä¢ Max daily dose: 4-6 g (trauma/surgical), 4 g (other indications)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Trauma/surgical: 15 mg/kg IV load, then 2 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Alternative: 10 mg/kg IV q6-8h
‚Ä¢ Neonates/infants: Limited data - 10 mg/kg IV over 10 min
‚Ä¢ Cardiac surgery: Load 30 mg/kg, then 16 mg/kg/hr, then 2 mg/kg/hr
‚Ä¢ Max pediatric dose: Adult dosing if weight >50 kg
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred in emergencies), oral, topical
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 100 mg/mL (10 mL = 1 g), 500 mg/5 mL vials
‚Ä¢ For IV bolus: Give undiluted over 10 minutes (at least)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Dilute in 50-100 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 8 hours
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most IV solutions, NOT with penicillin, blood products
‚Ä¢ Oral: 500 mg tablets, crush and give via NG if needed
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (especially oral)
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Visual disturbances (rare, reversible)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension with rapid IV administration
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions
‚Ä¢ Fatigue, malaise
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Thromboembolic events (DVT, PE, stroke) - risk increases with higher doses/longer use
‚Ä¢ Seizures (especially with high doses in cardiac surgery patients)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Color vision abnormalities, retinal changes
‚Ä¢ Acute renal cortical necrosis (rare, with ureteral obstruction)
‚Ä¢ Intracranial thrombosis (avoid in subarachnoid hemorrhage)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Anticoagulants/antiplatelets: May reduce effectiveness of TXA
‚Ä¢ Factor IX complex: Increased thrombosis risk - contraindicated
‚Ä¢ Ticlopidine: Increased thrombosis risk
‚Ä¢ Hormonal contraceptives: Increased thrombosis risk (theoretical)
‚Ä¢ Tissue plasminogen activators (tPA): Antagonizes effect
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "3 HOUR RULE": Give within 3 hours of injury - mortality benefit up to 3h, harmful after?
‚Ä¢ CRASH-2 trial: 1g load + 1g over 8h reduced mortality 1.5% (NNT=67)
‚Ä¢ MATTERs study: Combat trauma with MTP - 6.5% absolute mortality reduction
‚Ä¢ SBP <90 or HR >110: Clinical signs of significant hemorrhage - give TXA
‚Ä¢ Not for minor bleeding: Use only when significant hemorrhage suspected
‚Ä¢ Avoid in head injury alone: CRASH-3 showed benefit only if GCS >8 or no significant ICH
‚Ä¢ Cost-effective: ~$5 per treatment - one of most cost-effective interventions
‚Ä¢ Pre-hospital use: Safe and effective when given by paramedics
ü©∏ TXA IN TRAUMA - CRITICAL TIMING:
‚Ä¢ WITHIN 1 HOUR: Maximum benefit (32% mortality reduction)
‚Ä¢ 1-3 HOURS: Still beneficial (mortality reduction)
‚Ä¢ >3 HOURS: May be harmful (increased mortality in CRASH-2)
‚Ä¢ DOSING: 1g IV over 10 min + 1g over 8 hours
‚Ä¢ WHEN: SBP <90 OR HR >110 OR significant hemorrhage suspected

3 Core Drug 2: Vitamin K (Injectable)

VITAMIN K (PHYTOMENADIONE) - ANTICOAGULATION REVERSAL

Drug class: Fat-soluble vitamin, cofactor for clotting factors
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Essential cofactor for Œ≥-glutamyl carboxylase enzyme
‚Ä¢ Converts inactive clotting factors II, VII, IX, X to active forms
‚Ä¢ Also activates proteins C, S, and Z (natural anticoagulants)
‚Ä¢ Does NOT reverse heparin or direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs)
‚Ä¢ IV form works within hours, oral takes 24+ hours
‚Ä¢ Fat-soluble - requires bile salts for oral absorption
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin/coumarin overdose or excessive anticoagulation
‚Ä¢ Reversal of vitamin K antagonists before urgent surgery/procedures
‚Ä¢ Vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB) in neonates
‚Ä¢ Malabsorption syndromes (IBD, cystic fibrosis, short bowel)
‚Ä¢ Antibiotic-associated hypoprothrombinemia
‚Ä¢ Liver disease with prolonged INR (partial correction)
‚Ä¢ Rodenticide poisoning (superwarfarin exposure)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to vitamin K preparations
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic failure (may not respond)
‚Ä¢ Not for heparin reversal (use protamine)
‚Ä¢ Not for DOAC reversal (use specific antidotes)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Recent thrombotic event (risk of thrombosis with reversal)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin reversal (no bleeding): 1-2.5 mg PO, repeat in 24h if needed
‚Ä¢ Warfarin reversal (minor bleeding): 2.5-5 mg PO, may repeat in 24h
‚Ä¢ Warfarin reversal (major bleeding): 5-10 mg IV slow + 4-factor PCC
‚Ä¢ INR >10 no bleeding: 2.5-5 mg PO, repeat as needed
‚Ä¢ Pre-procedure: 1-2.5 mg PO for 3 days prior, check INR day of
‚Ä¢ Rodenticide poisoning: 10-25 mg PO daily for weeks to months
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonatal VKDB prophylaxis: 0.5-1 mg IM at birth (single dose)
‚Ä¢ Neonatal VKDB treatment: 1-2 mg IV (severe cases may need FFP)
‚Ä¢ Warfarin reversal: 0.025-0.1 mg/kg IV/PO
‚Ä¢ Vitamin K deficiency: 1-2 mg IM/SC weekly, or 2.5-5 mg PO twice weekly
‚Ä¢ Cystic fibrosis/malabsorption: 2.5-5 mg PO daily or 1-2 mg IM weekly
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (slow infusion), IM, SC, PO
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 1 mg/mL (1 mL ampoules), 10 mg/mL (multidose vials)
‚Ä¢ For IV: MUST dilute and give slowly - 1 mg/min max rate
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Mix with 50 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 20-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ IM/SC: Preferred over IV when possible (less anaphylactoid risk)
‚Ä¢ Oral: 5 mg tablets, liquid formulations available
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Flushing, dizziness (especially with rapid IV)
‚Ä¢ Altered taste (with IV administration)
‚Ä¢ Pain/swelling at injection site (IM/SC)
‚Ä¢ Hyperbilirubinemia in neonates (especially with high doses)
‚Ä¢ Skin reactions (rash, urticaria)
‚Ä¢ Gastrointestinal upset (oral)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylactoid reactions (with rapid IV administration)
‚Ä¢ Death (rare, with rapid IV undiluted)
‚Ä¢ Hyperbilirubinemia/kernicterus in neonates
‚Ä¢ Thrombosis (with rapid reversal of anticoagulation)
‚Ä¢ Hemolytic anemia in G6PD deficiency (theoretical)
‚Ä¢ "Purple glove syndrome" - skin discoloration at injection site
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Antagonizes effect (obvious)
‚Ä¢ Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum abx reduce vitamin K production by gut flora
‚Ä¢ Orlistat, cholestyramine: Reduce absorption of oral vitamin K
‚Ä¢ Phenytoin, barbiturates: Increase metabolism via CYP induction
‚Ä¢ Vitamin E: High doses may antagonize vitamin K
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "IV = Slow or No": Never give IV push - dilute and infuse over 20-60 min
‚Ä¢ Major bleeding + warfarin: Give 4-factor PCC (immediate) + Vitamin K 5-10 mg IV (sustained)
‚Ä¢ Onset of action: IV: 1-2 hours, PO: 6-12 hours, full effect 24-48 hours
‚Ä¢ Duration: Single dose lasts 2-5 days depending on liver function
‚Ä¢ Liver disease: May have partial response - often need FFP as well
‚Ä¢ Resistance: Superwarfarins (rodenticides) may need months of treatment
‚Ä¢ Neonates: IM vitamin K at birth prevents hemorrhagic disease (standard care)
‚Ä¢ Antidote for vitamin K antagonists ONLY - not heparin, not DOACs

4 Core Drug 3: Morphine

MORPHINE - GOLD STANDARD OPIOID FOR MODERATE-SEVERE PAIN

Drug class: Natural opium alkaloid, mu-opioid receptor agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Primarily agonizes mu-opioid receptors in CNS and periphery
‚Ä¢ Also has delta and kappa opioid receptor activity
‚Ä¢ Inhibits pain transmission in dorsal horn of spinal cord
‚Ä¢ Activates descending inhibitory pathways from brainstem
‚Ä¢ Alters emotional response to pain (limbic system)
‚Ä¢ Causes peripheral vasodilation (histamine release)
‚Ä¢ Metabolized to active metabolites (M6G - more potent, M3G - neuroexcitatory)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Moderate to severe acute pain (trauma, post-op, MI, sickle cell crisis)
‚Ä¢ Chronic cancer pain (oral formulations)
‚Ä¢ Dyspnea in palliative care (reduces air hunger)
‚Ä¢ Sedation in mechanically ventilated patients (ICU)
‚Ä¢ Pre-medication before anesthesia
‚Ä¢ Acute pulmonary edema (reduces preload and anxiety)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to morphine or opioids
‚Ä¢ Acute or severe asthma/COPD (risk of respiratory depression)
‚Ä¢ Paralytic ileus, GI obstruction
‚Ä¢ Increased intracranial pressure (risk of hypercapnia ‚Üí increased ICP)
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment (accumulation of metabolites)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent MAOI use or within 14 days (risk of serotonin syndrome)
‚Ä¢ Acute pancreatitis (may cause spasm of sphincter of Oddi)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ IV acute pain: 2-5 mg IV q5-15min titrated to pain relief
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: 1-5 mg/hr after loading dose, titrate to effect
‚Ä¢ IM/SC: 5-15 mg q4h (less preferred due to variable absorption)
‚Ä¢ MI/pulmonary edema: 2-4 mg IV, repeat q5-15min
‚Ä¢ Oral immediate-release: 10-30 mg q4h
‚Ä¢ Oral controlled-release: Not for acute pain initiation
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ IV/IM/SC: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg q2-4h (max initial dose 4-5 mg)
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: 0.01-0.04 mg/kg/hr after loading dose
‚Ä¢ Oral: 0.2-0.5 mg/kg q4-6h
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 0.05 mg/kg IV/IM q4-8h (prolonged half-life)
‚Ä¢ PCA (patient-controlled analgesia): 0.01-0.02 mg/kg q6-10min + background
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred in emergencies), IM, SC, PO, PR, epidural, intrathecal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 1 mg/mL, 2 mg/mL, 4 mg/mL, 5 mg/mL, 10 mg/mL, 15 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Dilute to at least 1 mg/mL, give over 4-5 minutes
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Typical concentration 1 mg/mL in NS or D5W
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most solutions, NOT with alkaline solutions (precipitate)
‚Ä¢ Stability: 24 hours at room temperature when diluted
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Sedation, drowsiness, confusion (especially elderly)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (30% of patients)
‚Ä¢ Constipation (almost universal with chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Pruritus (especially facial, not allergy - histamine release)
‚Ä¢ Urinary retention
‚Ä¢ Dry mouth, sweating
‚Ä¢ Miosis (pinpoint pupils - pathognomonic but tolerance develops)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression (dose-related, watch for <8 breaths/min)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (especially with hypovolemia or rapid administration)
‚Ä¢ Severe bronchospasm (histamine release in susceptible)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (with high doses or accumulation of M3G metabolite in renal failure)
‚Ä¢ Hyperalgesia (with chronic high-dose use)
‚Ä¢ Adrenal insufficiency (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Serotonin syndrome (especially with other serotonergic drugs)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other CNS depressants: Synergistic respiratory depression (benzos, alcohol, barbiturates)
‚Ä¢ MAOIs: Serotonin syndrome risk - contraindicated
‚Ä¢ SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs: Increased serotonin syndrome risk
‚Ä¢ Anticholinergics: Increased constipation/urinary retention risk
‚Ä¢ Mixed agonist-antagonists (pentazocine): Precipitate withdrawal
‚Ä¢ Protease inhibitors (ritonavir): Increased morphine levels
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Start low, go slow" especially in opioid-na√Øve, elderly, renal impairment
‚Ä¢ Renal failure warning: Metabolites accumulate ‚Üí neuroexcitation, seizures, myoclonus
‚Ä¢ Histamine release: Causes flushing, itching, hypotension - NOT anaphylaxis
‚Ä¢ Equianalgesic: 10 mg IV morphine = 30 mg PO morphine (3:1 ratio)
‚Ä¢ Naloxone ready: Have reversal agent available (0.4 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ Pain is the 5th vital sign: Assess before and after administration (0-10 scale)
‚Ä¢ MI pain: Morphine reduces anxiety, preload, oxygen demand - still first line
‚Ä¢ PCA: Patient-controlled analgesia gives better pain control with less total dose

5 Core Drug 4: Fentanyl

FENTANYL - POTENT, RAPID-ONSET SYNTHETIC OPIOID

Drug class: Synthetic phenylpiperidine, mu-opioid receptor agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Highly selective mu-opioid receptor agonist (100x more potent than morphine)
‚Ä¢ Lipid soluble - rapid CNS penetration (onset 1-2 minutes IV)
‚Ä¢ Minimal histamine release ‚Üí less hypotension than morphine
‚Ä¢ Short context-sensitive half-time (accumulates less with infusion)
‚Ä¢ Metabolized by CYP3A4 to inactive metabolites (mostly)
‚Ä¢ Transdermal and transmucosal formulations have different pharmacokinetics
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Severe acute pain (trauma, burns, post-op)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation and analgesia (PSA)
‚Ä¢ Rapid sequence intubation analgesia component
‚Ä¢ Chronic cancer pain (transdermal patches)
‚Ä¢ Breakthrough cancer pain (oral transmucosal)
‚Ä¢ Pre-medication before painful procedures
‚Ä¢ Cardiac surgery/ICU analgesia (hemodynamically stable)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to fentanyl
‚Ä¢ Opioid-na√Øve patients for transdermal use (high overdose risk)
‚Ä¢ Acute or severe asthma/COPD (risk of respiratory depression)
‚Ä¢ Paralytic ileus
‚Ä¢ Increased intracranial pressure (risk of hypercapnia)
‚Ä¢ Hepatic impairment (may prolong effect)
‚Ä¢ MAOI use within 14 days

‚Ä¢ IV infusion: 25-100 mcg/hr after loading dose

‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (less than morphine)

‚Ä¢ CYP3A4 inducers: Decreased levels (rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin)

6 Core Drug 5: Ketorolac

Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Reversible inhibition of cyclooxygenase (COX-1 and COX-2) enzymes

‚Ä¢ Renal colic gold standard: More effective than opioids for ureteric stone pain
‚Ä¢ IV vs IM: IV preferred - faster onset, less painful, same efficacy

7 Core Drug 6: Tetanus Immunization

TETANUS TOXOID & IMMUNOGLOBULIN - WOUND PROPHYLAXIS

Drug class: Vaccine (toxoid) and passive immunization (immunoglobulin)
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Tetanus toxoid (TT/Td/Tdap): Inactivated toxin stimulates active immunity
‚Ä¢ Tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG): Pre-formed antibodies provide immediate passive immunity
‚Ä¢ Neutralizes circulating tetanus toxin before it binds to neurons
‚Ä¢ Does NOT reverse bound toxin (once bound, only supportive care)
‚Ä¢ Tetanus toxoid provides long-term protection (10 years booster)
‚Ä¢ TIG provides immediate but temporary protection (weeks)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Tetanus toxoid: Primary immunization, booster every 10 years, wound prophylaxis
‚Ä¢ Tetanus immunoglobulin: Passive immunization for high-risk wounds in inadequately immunized
‚Ä¢ Wound management: Based on wound type and vaccination history
‚Ä¢ Neonatal tetanus prevention: Maternal immunization during pregnancy
‚Ä¢ Tetanus treatment: TIG as part of management (doesn't reverse symptoms)
‚Ä¢ Post-exposure prophylaxis: For contaminated wounds in unvaccinated/unknown status
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Absolute: Severe allergic reaction to previous dose
‚Ä¢ Toxoid contraindications: Encephalopathy within 7 days of previous dose
‚Ä¢ Precautions: Moderate/severe acute illness (delay until recovered)
‚Ä¢ Guillain-Barr√© syndrome: Within 6 weeks of previous tetanus vaccine
‚Ä¢ Immunoglobulin precautions: IgA deficiency (risk of anaphylaxis)
‚Ä¢ Live vaccines: MMR/varicella should be given same day or separated by 4 weeks
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Tetanus toxoid (Td/Tdap): 0.5 mL IM single dose
‚Ä¢ Tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG): 250-500 units IM single dose
‚Ä¢ Wound management: Follow CDC algorithm (clean vs dirty wound, vaccination history)
‚Ä¢ Primary series: 3 doses: 0, 1-2 months, 6-12 months
‚Ä¢ Booster: Every 10 years (Td) or once as adult with Tdap, then Td
‚Ä¢ Tetanus treatment: 3000-6000 units IM (part in wound edges)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ DTaP (children <7 years): 0.5 mL IM at 2, 4, 6, 15-18 months, 4-6 years
‚Ä¢ Tdap (‚â•7 years): 0.5 mL IM single dose (booster at 11-12 years)
‚Ä¢ TIG: 4 units/kg IM (max 250 units)
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Maternal immunization during pregnancy protects infant
‚Ä¢ Primary series: Complete if unknown/incomplete status
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IM only (deltoid or vastus lateralis)
‚Ä¢ Toxoid preparation: 0.5 mL prefilled syringe or vial
‚Ä¢ TIG preparation: 250 units/mL vial
‚Ä¢ Administration site: Different limbs for TIG and toxoid if both needed
‚Ä¢ Needle size: 22-25 gauge, 1-1.5 inch for deltoid (based on size)
‚Ä¢ Storage: Refrigerate (2-8¬∞C), do not freeze
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Injection site: Pain, redness, swelling (80% of recipients)
‚Ä¢ Fever, malaise, headache (10-15%)
‚Ä¢ Arthralgia, myalgia
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
‚Ä¢ Lymphadenopathy (regional)
‚Ä¢ Mild allergic reactions (urticaria)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare: 1 in 1,000,000 doses)
‚Ä¢ Brachial neuritis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Guillain-Barr√© syndrome (rare association)
‚Ä¢ Arthus reaction (severe local reaction in over-immunized)
‚Ä¢ Syncope (especially adolescents)
‚Ä¢ Extensive limb swelling (more common with boosters)
‚Ä¢ Encephalopathy (extremely rare)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Immunosuppressants: Reduced immune response (chemotherapy, steroids, biologics)
‚Ä¢ Other vaccines: Can be given simultaneously at different sites
‚Ä¢ Blood products/immunoglobulins: May interfere with live vaccines (not tetanus)
‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Increased bleeding risk at injection site (check INR)
‚Ä¢ Aspirin/NSAIDs: Not contraindicated but may mask fever
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Dirty wound + unknown vaccine = TIG + Toxoid"
‚Ä¢ Tdap vs Td: Tdap has pertussis component - give once as adult, then Td boosters
‚Ä¢ Wound classification: Clean minor vs contaminated/tetanus-prone wounds
‚Ä¢ History unknown/3 doses: Treat as unimmunized (give toxoid ¬± TIG)
‚Ä¢ Last dose <5 years: No toxoid needed for clean minor wounds

‚Ä¢ Cost-effectiveness: $30 for TIG vs $3 million for tetanus treatment

Tetanus Prophylaxis Algorithm

üìã TETANUS WOUND MANAGEMENT (CDC GUIDELINES):

STEP 1: Classify wound
‚Ä¢ Clean, minor: Little contamination, >6h old
‚Ä¢ Tetanus-prone: >6h old, >1cm deep, puncture, avulsion, crush, burn, frostbite, contaminated

STEP 2: Check vaccine history
‚Ä¢ Unknown or <3 doses: Consider unvaccinated
‚Ä¢ 3 doses: Consider vaccinated but check timing

STEP 3: Decide on Tetanus Toxoid (Td/Tdap)
‚Ä¢ Clean minor: Give if >10 years since last dose
‚Ä¢ Tetanus-prone: Give if >5 years since last dose
‚Ä¢ Unknown/<3 doses: Always give

STEP 4: Decide on Tetanus Immunoglobulin (TIG)
‚Ä¢ Clean minor: Never needed
‚Ä¢ Tetanus-prone: Give if unknown/<3 doses or if HIV/immunocompromised
‚Ä¢ Give TIG and toxoid in different limbs with different syringes

Massive Hemorrhage Protocol (MHP) Checklist

  1. Activate MHP early (>4 units predicted in 1h or ongoing shock)
  2. TXA 1g IV if within 3h of injury
  3. Blood products in 1:1:1 ratio (PRBC:FFP:platelets)
  4. Consider cryoprecipitate/fibrinogen if fibrinogen <1.5 g/L
  5. Calcium chloride 1g IV if massive transfusion (citrate toxicity)
  6. Avoid excessive crystalloid (<2L initially)
  7. Permissive hypotension (SBP 80-90 mmHg) if no head injury
  8. Damage control surgery if ongoing bleed
  9. Rewarm patient (avoid hypothermia)
  10. Monitor labs q30-60min (Hb, INR, fibrinogen, Ca¬≤‚Å∫, pH)
