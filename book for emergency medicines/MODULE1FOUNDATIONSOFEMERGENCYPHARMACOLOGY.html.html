1 Definition of Emergency Medicine

Emergency Medicine: The medical specialty concerned with the care of illnesses or injuries requiring immediate medical attention. Emergency physicians, also known as emergency medicine doctors, specialize in providing care for unscheduled and undifferentiated patients of all ages.

Core Principles of Emergency Medicine

üß† Clinical Pearl: In emergency medicine, we often have to make critical decisions with incomplete information. The priority is always "First, do no harm" while stabilizing life-threatening conditions.

Key Characteristics:

  • Time-Critical Nature: Interventions must occur within specific time frames ("golden hour," "platinum 10 minutes")
  • Undifferentiated Patients: Patients present without prior diagnosis; rapid assessment is crucial
  • Resource-Limited Environment: Often working with incomplete information or limited resources
  • Multisystem Approach: Must consider multiple organ systems simultaneously
  • Continuum of Care: Focus on stabilization before transfer to definitive care
  • Team-Based Care: Collaboration with nurses, paramedics, and specialists
  • High-Stakes Decision Making: Decisions can mean life or death

The Emergency Medicine Mindset:

  1. ABCDE Approach: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure
  2. Treat First What Kills First: Prioritize immediate life threats
  3. Rule Out Worst-Case Scenario: Consider the most dangerous possibilities first
  4. Reassess Continuously: Patients can deteriorate rapidly

2 Difference Between Key Pharmacological Terms

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Understanding: These distinctions are not just academic‚Äîthey have direct implications for patient safety, documentation, and legal responsibility.

2.1 Indication vs Contraindication

Term Definition Example in Emergency Medicine Clinical Significance
Indication A valid reason to use a particular test, medication, procedure, or surgery Adrenaline: Cardiac arrest, anaphylaxis, severe asthma Justifies the use of a treatment; evidence-based application
Contraindication A specific situation in which a drug, procedure, or surgery should NOT be used Streptokinase: Active internal bleeding, recent stroke Prevents harm; using despite contraindication may be malpractice
Absolute Contraindication A situation that makes a treatment completely inadvisable ACE inhibitors: Pregnancy (especially 2nd & 3rd trimester) Never use in this situation
Relative Contraindication A situation where caution is advised but treatment may proceed if benefits outweigh risks Contrast dye: Mild allergy with pretreatment possible Requires careful risk-benefit analysis

2.2 Side Effect vs Adverse Effect vs ADR

üß† Clinical Pearl: All drugs have side effects. The key is distinguishing between expected, manageable side effects and dangerous adverse reactions.
Term Definition Example Severity & Management
Side Effect A secondary, typically undesirable effect of a drug or medical treatment Nausea from morphine, dry mouth from atropine Usually predictable, manageable, often dose-dependent
Adverse Effect A harmful or abnormal result caused by medication Respiratory depression from opioids, arrhythmia from adrenaline More serious, may require intervention or discontinuation
Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR) Any noxious, unintended, and undesired effect of a drug at normal doses Anaphylaxis to penicillin, Stevens-Johnson syndrome Serious, potentially life-threatening, must be reported
Type A ADR
(Augmented)		 Dose-dependent, predictable from drug's pharmacology Bradycardia from beta-blocker overdose Common (80% of ADRs), predictable, dose-related
Type B ADR
(Bizarre)		 Not dose-dependent, unpredictable, often immunological Anaphylaxis, drug-induced lupus Uncommon, unpredictable, often severe

3 Routes of Injectable Administration

3.1 Overview of Injectable Routes

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Skill: Choosing the correct route is as important as choosing the correct drug. The wrong route can render treatment ineffective or cause harm.
Route Abbreviation Description Typical Onset of Action Common Emergency Uses
IV Bolus IV push, IV stat Direct injection into vein over seconds to minutes 15-30 seconds Cardiac arrest drugs, rapid sequence intubation, status epilepticus
IV Infusion IV drip, IV infusion Continuous administration via infusion pump or gravity Minutes (depends on rate) Vasopressors, antiarrhythmics, maintenance fluids
Intramuscular IM Injection into muscle (deltoid, vastus lateralis, ventrogluteal) 5-15 minutes Anaphylaxis (adrenaline), sedation, antipsychotics, vaccines
Subcutaneous SC, SubQ Injection into fatty tissue beneath skin 15-30 minutes Insulin, heparin, some vaccines

3.2 Detailed Route Characteristics

IV Bolus (Intravenous Push)

  • Advantages: Immediate effect, complete bioavailability, precise control
  • Disadvantages: Risk of extravasation, phlebitis, rapid adverse effects
  • Maximum Rate Guidelines:
    • Potassium chloride: NEVER IV push (cardiac arrest risk)
    • Magnesium sulfate: 1-2 minutes for loading dose
    • Lidocaine: 1-3 minutes (50 mg/min maximum)

Intraosseous (IO) Access

üß† Clinical Pearl: IO is NOT a last resort‚Äîit's an alternative first-line access in critical situations. All resuscitation drugs given IV can be given IO at the same dose.
  • Primary Sites: Proximal tibia, distal tibia, proximal humerus, sternum
  • Flow Rates: 2-5 L/hr possible with pressure bag
  • Duration: Remove within 24 hours (risk of osteomyelitis)
  • Special Considerations: Painful (consider lidocaine), requires pressure for infusion
üìä Injection Site Selection Guide:
  • Deltoid (IM): Max 2 mL volume
  • Vastus Lateralis (IM): Max 5 mL (adults), 2-3 mL (children)
  • Ventrogluteal (IM): Safest site, max 4 mL
  • Dorsogluteal (IM): AVOID (risk of sciatic nerve injury)
  • Abdomen (SC): Rotate sites, avoid 2-inch circle around navel

4 Drug Preparation Basics

4.1 The Three C's of Drug Preparation

‚ö†Ô∏è Medication Error Alert: Up to 50% of medication errors occur during the preparation and administration phase. Always double-check calculations!
Concept Definition Calculation Formula Example
Dilution Adding a solvent (usually normal saline or sterile water) to a concentrated drug Volume needed = (Desired concentration √ó Desired volume) / Stock concentration Make 1 mg/mL adrenaline from 1:1000 (1 mg/mL): No dilution needed
Concentration Amount of drug per unit volume Concentration = Amount of drug (mg) / Volume (mL) 1:1000 adrenaline = 1 mg/mL
Compatibility Ability of drugs to be mixed without physical or chemical interaction N/A - Refer to compatibility charts Adrenaline compatible with most IV fluids; incompatible with sodium bicarbonate

4.2 Common Emergency Drug Concentrations

üß† Clinical Pearl: Memorize these common concentrations‚Äîthey're tested frequently and used daily in emergency practice.
  • Adrenaline (Epinephrine):
    • 1:1000 = 1 mg/mL (IM for anaphylaxis)
    • 1:10,000 = 0.1 mg/mL (IV for cardiac arrest)
  • Atropine: 0.1 mg/mL, 0.5 mg/mL, 1 mg/mL
  • Lidocaine: 1% = 10 mg/mL, 2% = 20 mg/mL
  • Morphine: 10 mg/mL, 15 mg/mL
  • Midazolam: 1 mg/mL, 5 mg/mL
  • Diazepam: 5 mg/mL

4.3 Y-Site Compatibility Rules

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule: When in doubt about compatibility, use separate IV lines. Precipitation or inactivation can occur instantly.

Common Incompatibilities (NEVER MIX):

  • Adrenaline + Sodium bicarbonate: Forms precipitate, inactivates adrenaline
  • Phenytoin + Most solutions: Precipitates in dextrose or other drugs
  • Amiodarone + Sodium bicarbonate: Incompatible
  • Insulin + Most emergency drugs: Adsorbs to plastic, inconsistent delivery

Always Compatible (Safe to Mix):

  • Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl) with most emergency drugs
  • 5% Dextrose (D5W) with most drugs (except phenytoin)
  • Lactated Ringer's with most drugs (caution with amphotericin, some antibiotics)

5 Emergency Drug Safety

5.1 High-Alert Medications

High-Alert Medications: Drugs that bear a heightened risk of causing significant patient harm when used in error. Although mistakes may or may not be more common with these drugs, the consequences of an error are clearly more devastating.

Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) List (Emergency Focus):

  • Adrenergic agonists (IV): adrenaline, noradrenaline
  • Adrenergic antagonists (IV): propranolol, metoprolol
  • Anesthetic agents (IV): propofol, ketamine
  • Antiarrhythmics (IV): amiodarone, lidocaine
  • Anticoagulants (IV): heparin, warfarin
  • Cardioplegic solutions
  • Chemotherapeutic agents
  • Dextrose (hypertonic, ‚â•20%)
  • Dialysis fluids (peritoneal and hemodialysis)
  • Epidural/intrathecal medications
  • Inotropic agents (IV): dopamine, dobutamine

  • Lipid emulsion (IV)

  • Moderate sedation agents (IV): midazolam
  • Moderate/opioid agonists (IV): fentanyl, morphine

6 Pediatric vs Adult Dosing Principles

6.1 Fundamental Differences

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Warning: Children are NOT small adults. Their pharmacokinetics differ significantly. Never simply "divide the adult dose."
Parameter Pediatric Considerations Adult Considerations Clinical Implications
Body Composition Higher water content (80% vs 60%), lower fat Lower water content, variable fat Water-soluble drugs: higher mg/kg dose needed
Protein Binding Lower albumin, less protein binding Normal protein binding Higher free drug levels in children
Hepatic Metabolism Immature enzyme systems (especially neonates) Mature enzyme systems Longer half-lives in neonates for some drugs
Renal Excretion Lower GFR (especially <6 months) Normal or reduced GFR Renally excreted drugs: reduce dose in infants
Blood-Brain Barrier More permeable in infants Fully developed Increased CNS effects in children

6.2 Dosing Calculations Methods

üß† Clinical Pearl: Always calculate pediatric doses using weight (mg/kg). For emergencies, use length-based systems (Broselow tape) when weight is unknown.

Common Pediatric Dosing Methods:

  1. Weight-Based (mg/kg): Most common method
    • Formula: Dose = Weight (kg) √ó Dose per kg
    • Example: Paracetamol 15 mg/kg for 20 kg child = 300 mg
  2. Body Surface Area (mg/m¬≤): More accurate for chemotherapy
    • Mosteller formula: BSA (m¬≤) = ‚àö[height (cm) √ó weight (kg) / 3600]
  3. Age-Based Rules: Quick estimation
    • Clark's rule: Child dose = (Weight in lbs/150) √ó Adult dose
    • Young's rule: Child dose = [Age/(Age + 12)] √ó Adult dose

7 Renal & Hepatic Dose Adjustment (Basic)

7.1 Renal Dose Adjustment

Creatinine Clearance (CrCl): The volume of blood plasma cleared of creatinine per unit time. It's a useful measure for approximating the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).

Cockcroft-Gault Formula (Most Common):

CrCl (mL/min) = [(140 - Age) √ó Weight (kg)] / [72 √ó Serum Cr (mg/dL)]
For women: Multiply result by 0.85

Renal Impairment Stages and Dosing:

Stage GFR (mL/min) Serum Creatinine Dosing Adjustment
Normal >90 <1.2 mg/dL No adjustment
Mild impairment 60-89 1.2-1.9 Usually no adjustment
Moderate impairment 30-59 2.0-3.9 Reduce dose by 25-50% or extend interval
Severe impairment 15-29 4.0-6.0 Reduce dose by 50-75%
Renal failure <15 >6.0 Avoid or use minimal doses with monitoring

Emergency Drugs Requiring Renal Adjustment:

  • Major adjustment needed:
    • Enoxaparin (LMWH)
    • Gentamicin and other aminoglycosides
    • Digoxin
    • Lithium
    • Most ACE inhibitors
  • Minor or no adjustment:
    • Adrenaline (epinephrine)
    • Atropine
    • Diazepam (metabolized hepatically)
    • Fentanyl

7.2 Hepatic Dose Adjustment

üß† Clinical Pearl: Hepatic impairment is harder to quantify than renal impairment. Use Child-Pugh score as a guide, but clinical judgment is essential.

Child-Pugh Classification:

Parameter 1 point 2 points 3 points
Albumin (g/dL) >3.5 2.8-3.5 <2.8
Bilirubin (mg/dL) <2 2-3 >3
INR <1.7 1.7-2.3 >2.3
Ascites None Mild Moderate-Severe
Encephalopathy None Grade 1-2 Grade 3-4

Child-Pugh Scores and Dosing:

  • Class A (5-6 points): Mild disease - Usually no dose adjustment
  • Class B (7-9 points): Moderate disease - Reduce dose by 25-50%
  • Class C (10-15 points): Severe disease - Avoid or use minimal doses

Emergency Drugs Requiring Hepatic Adjustment:

  • Major adjustment needed:
    • Morphine (increased sensitivity)
    • Midazolam (prolonged sedation)
    • Lidocaine (reduced clearance)
    • Propranolol (first-pass metabolism reduced)
  • Safe in hepatic impairment:
    • Noradrenaline (norepinephrine)
    • Dobutamine
    • Atropine
    • Furosemide

8 Anaphylaxis & Drug Reaction Management

8.1 Anaphylaxis Recognition

Anaphylaxis: A severe, life-threatening, generalized or systemic hypersensitivity reaction characterized by rapidly developing life-threatening airway, breathing, and/or circulation problems usually associated with skin and mucosal changes.

Diagnostic Criteria (ANY ONE of the following):

  1. Acute onset of illness (minutes to hours) with involvement of skin, mucosal tissue, or both (e.g., hives, pruritus, flushing, swollen lips/tongue/uvula) AND AT LEAST ONE OF:
    • Respiratory compromise (dyspnea, wheeze, stridor, hypoxemia)
    • Reduced BP or associated symptoms (collapse, syncope, incontinence)
  2. Two or more of the following that occur rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen:
    • Involvement of skin/mucosa
    • Respiratory compromise
    • Reduced BP or associated symptoms
    • Persistent gastrointestinal symptoms (cramping, vomiting)
  3. Reduced BP after exposure to known allergen:
    • Infants and children: Low systolic BP or >30% decrease
    • Adults: Systolic BP <90 mmHg or >30% decrease from baseline

8.2 Anaphylaxis Management Protocol

‚ö†Ô∏è Emergency Protocol: Adrenaline FIRST, adrenaline FAST, adrenaline INTRAMUSCULAR (anterolateral thigh). Do not delay for IV access or other medications.

Step-by-Step Management:

  1. Immediate Actions:
    • Remove trigger if possible (stop IV medication)
    • Call for help (crash team, senior help)
    • Place patient in supine position (or left lateral if pregnant)
    • High-flow oxygen (15 L/min via non-rebreather mask)
  2. Adrenaline (Epinephrine) Administration:
    • Route: IM anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis)
    • Dose: 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1000 (max 0.5 mg adults, 0.3 mg children)
    • Repeat: Every 5 minutes if no improvement
    • IV adrenaline: Only if refractory anaphylaxis or cardiac arrest
  3. Secondary Medications:
    • Chlorphenamine (Antihistamine): 10 mg IM/IV (child: 0.2 mg/kg)
    • Hydrocortisone (Steroid): 200 mg IM/IV (child: 2-4 mg/kg)
  4. Fluid Resuscitation:
    • Normal saline 500-1000 mL bolus (child: 20 mL/kg)
    • Repeat if hypotensive
  5. Refractory Cases:
    • Adrenaline infusion: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min
    • Glucagon: 1-2 mg IM/IV if on beta-blockers
    • Consider salbutamol neb if bronchospasm prominent

Time-Sensitive Interventions ("Golden Hours")

  • Myocardial infarction: Door-to-balloon <90 minutes, thrombolysis <30 minutes
  • Stroke: Door-to-needle (tPA) <60 minutes, thrombectomy <6 hours
  • Trauma: "Golden hour" concept, "platinum 10 minutes"
  • Sepsis: Antibiotics within 1 hour, lactate within 3 hours
  • Anaphylaxis: Adrenaline within 5 minutes
  • Status epilepticus: Benzodiazepines within 5 minutes
