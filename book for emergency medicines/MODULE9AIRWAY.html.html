1 Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI) Protocol

Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI): A systematic approach to emergency airway management that involves simultaneous administration of a sedative (induction agent) and a neuromuscular blocking agent to rapidly induce unconsciousness and paralysis, minimizing the time between loss of protective reflexes and securing the airway.

2 Core Drug 1: Ketamine

KETAMINE - DISSOCIATIVE ANESTHETIC WITH ANALGESIC PROPERTIES

Drug class: Dissociative anesthetic, NMDA receptor antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Non-competitive antagonist at NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) receptors
‚Ä¢ Produces "dissociative anesthesia" - patient appears awake but disconnected
‚Ä¢ Maintains protective airway reflexes better than other induction agents
‚Ä¢ Sympathomimetic effects - increases HR, BP, CO via catecholamine release
‚Ä¢ Direct myocardial depressant (unmasked in catecholamine-depleted states)
‚Ä¢ Bronchodilator (useful in asthma/status asthmaticus)
‚Ä¢ Analgesic at sub-anesthetic doses (0.1-0.5 mg/kg)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Induction agent for RSI (especially in hypotensive, asthmatic patients)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation and analgesia (PSA) for painful procedures
‚Ä¢ Sedation in agitated patients (Excited Delirium Syndrome)
‚Ä¢ Refractory status epilepticus (adjunctive therapy)
‚Ä¢ Analgesia for trauma, burns, dressing changes
‚Ä¢ Bronchospasm (asthma/COPD exacerbation refractory to standard therapy)
‚Ä¢ Depression treatment (off-label, sub-anesthetic doses)
‚Ä¢ Field anesthesia in resource-limited settings
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to ketamine
‚Ä¢ Significant ischemic heart disease (increases myocardial oxygen demand)
‚Ä¢ Uncontrolled hypertension (exacerbates hypertension)
‚Ä¢ Elevated intracranial pressure (increases cerebral metabolic rate and ICP)
‚Ä¢ Active psychosis/schizophrenia (may exacerbate symptoms)
‚Ä¢ Thyroid disorder (may precipitate thyroid storm)
‚Ä¢ Porphyria (relative contraindication)
‚Ä¢ Ocular injuries/glaucoma (increases intraocular pressure)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 1-2 mg/kg IV (typical 100-200 mg)
‚Ä¢ IM induction: 4-5 mg/kg IM (if no IV access)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.5-1 mg/kg IV, repeat 0.25-0.5 mg/kg as needed
‚Ä¢ Analgesia: 0.1-0.5 mg/kg IV/IM q30-60min
‚Ä¢ Infusion for sedation: 0.1-0.5 mg/kg/hr after loading dose
‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 1-2 mg/kg IV load, then 1-5 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Max single dose: 4 mg/kg IM, 2 mg/kg IV
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 1-2 mg/kg IV
‚Ä¢ IM induction: 4-5 mg/kg IM
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 1-2 mg/kg IV, 3-4 mg/kg IM
‚Ä¢ Analgesia: 0.1-0.5 mg/kg IV/IM
‚Ä¢ Neonates/infants: Use lower doses (0.5-1 mg/kg IV)
‚Ä¢ With atropine/midazolam: Often co-administered to reduce secretions/emergence reactions
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, oral, intranasal, rectal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10 mg/mL (50 mg/5 mL), 50 mg/mL (500 mg/10 mL), 100 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted over 30-60 seconds
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Dilute to 1-2 mg/mL in NS or D5W
‚Ä¢ IM administration: Deep IM injection, onset 3-5 minutes
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most IV solutions, NOT with barbiturates (precipitate)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Emergence reactions (vivid dreams, hallucinations) - 10-30%
‚Ä¢ Increased secretions (treat with atropine/glycopyrrolate)
‚Ä¢ Nystagmus (dose-related)
‚Ä¢ Hypertension, tachycardia (sympathomimetic effects)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (especially with movement during recovery)
‚Ä¢ Increased intraocular pressure (transient)
‚Ä¢ Transient apnea (with rapid IV administration)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Laryngospasm (1-2%, especially with upper airway procedures)
‚Ä¢ Emergence delirium (agitation, confusion - reduced by co-administering midazolam)
‚Ä¢ Myocardial depression (in catecholamine-depleted patients - sepsis, heart failure)
‚Ä¢ Increased intracranial pressure (controversial - avoid in known elevated ICP)
‚Ä¢ Psychotomimetic effects (especially in psychiatric patients)
‚Ä¢ Aspiration (rare - protective reflexes relatively preserved)
‚Ä¢ Allergic reactions (rare)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepines: Reduce emergence reactions but prolong recovery
‚Ä¢ Theophylline/aminophylline: Increased seizure risk
‚Ä¢ Sympathomimetics: Additive cardiovascular effects
‚Ä¢ Volatile anesthetics: Prolongs recovery
‚Ä¢ NMBAs: Potentiates neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Thyroid hormones: May precipitate thyroid storm
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Hemodynamically friendly": First choice for hypotensive/hemodynamically unstable patients
‚Ä¢ Bronchodilator: Only induction agent with bronchodilating properties - use in asthma
‚Ä¢ Emergence reactions: Prevent with midazolam 0.02-0.04 mg/kg IV 5 min before ketamine
‚Ä¢ Secretions: Pre-treat with atropine 0.01-0.02 mg/kg IV (especially pediatric)
‚Ä¢ "Kitty Magic": Ketamine 4 mg/kg IM + midazolam 0.05 mg/kg IM for agitated delirium
‚Ä¢ Catecholamine-depleted: Can cause myocardial depression in septic/critically ill patients
‚Ä¢ Analgesic properties: Provides analgesia at sub-anesthetic doses (0.1-0.5 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ "Cannot intubate, cannot ventilate": Consider ketamine to maintain spontaneous respirations

3 Core Drug 2: Propofol

PROPOFOL - ULTRASHORT-ACTING SEDATIVE-HYPNOTIC

Drug class: Alkylphenol, GABA-A receptor agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Potentiates GABA-A receptor activity ‚Üí increased chloride conductance ‚Üí neuronal hyperpolarization
‚Ä¢ No analgesic properties (pure sedative-hypnotic)
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset (30-60 seconds) due to high lipid solubility
‚Ä¢ Rapid redistribution and metabolism ‚Üí short duration (5-10 minutes single dose)
‚Ä¢ Antiemetic properties (useful for procedural sedation)
‚Ä¢ Decreases cerebral metabolic rate and intracranial pressure
‚Ä¢ Myocardial depressant and vasodilator ‚Üí hypotension common
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Induction agent for RSI (in hemodynamically stable patients)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation (endoscopy, cardioversion, reduction of dislocations)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance of sedation in mechanically ventilated patients (ICU)
‚Ä¢ Refractory status epilepticus (high-dose infusion)
‚Ä¢ Antiemetic (subhypnotic doses: 10-20 mg IV)
‚Ä¢ Treatment of refractory intracranial hypertension
‚Ä¢ Sedation for imaging studies
‚Ä¢ Post-operative nausea and vomiting prophylaxis
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to propofol, eggs, or soybean oil (lipid emulsion base)
‚Ä¢ Hemodynamic instability/shock (significant hypotension risk)
‚Ä¢ Increased intracranial pressure without adequate MAP (risk of cerebral hypoperfusion)
‚Ä¢ Egg or soybean allergy (contains egg lecithin and soybean oil)
‚Ä¢ Disorders of fat metabolism (lipid emulsion)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (Category B, but generally avoided in 1st trimester)
‚Ä¢ Not for prolonged sedation in children (risk of propofol infusion syndrome)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 1.5-2.5 mg/kg IV (typical 100-200 mg)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.5-1 mg/kg IV, then 0.25-0.5 mg/kg q3-5min as needed
‚Ä¢ Sedation infusion: 5-80 mcg/kg/min (0.3-4.8 mg/kg/hr)
‚Ä¢ ICU sedation: 5-50 mcg/kg/min (0.3-3 mg/kg/hr)
‚Ä¢ Refractory status epilepticus: 1-2 mg/kg bolus, then 1-15 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Antiemetic: 10-20 mg IV single dose
‚Ä¢ Elderly/debilitated: Reduce dose by 50% (0.5-1 mg/kg)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 2-3 mg/kg IV (higher mg/kg than adults)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 1-2 mg/kg IV, then 0.5-1 mg/kg q2-3min as needed
‚Ä¢ Sedation infusion: 25-200 mcg/kg/min (1.5-12 mg/kg/hr) - SHORT TERM ONLY
‚Ä¢ NOT for prolonged ICU sedation in children (propofol infusion syndrome risk)
‚Ä¢ Neonates/infants: Use with extreme caution, lower doses
‚Ä¢ Max infusion duration: <48 hours in children, <4 mg/kg/hr
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (painful on injection)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10 mg/mL emulsion (20 mL = 200 mg, 50 mL = 500 mg, 100 mL = 1000 mg)
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted over 30-60 seconds
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Use undiluted via syringe pump or infusion pump
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection: Can mix with lidocaine (1 mL 1% lidocaine per 10 mL propofol)
‚Ä¢ Aseptic technique: Supports bacterial growth - use within 6-12 hours of opening
‚Ä¢ Storage: Room temperature, protect from light, do not freeze
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Dose-dependent hypotension (20-30% of patients)
‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression/apnea (especially with rapid administration)
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection (30-40%, reduced by lidocaine or larger vein)
‚Ä¢ Myoclonus (involuntary muscle movements)
‚Ä¢ Hiccups, cough
‚Ä¢ Hypertriglyceridemia (with prolonged infusion)
‚Ä¢ Green urine (phenol metabolite)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Propofol Infusion Syndrome (PRIS) - fatal metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure
‚Ä¢ Severe hypotension/bradycardia/cardiac arrest
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (especially in egg/soy allergy)
‚Ä¢ Pancreatitis (with prolonged use)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (paradoxical, rare)
‚Ä¢ Infections (if contamination occurs - supports bacterial growth)
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia (in PRIS)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other CNS depressants: Synergistic effects (opioids, benzodiazepines, alcohol)
‚Ä¢ Opioids: Potentiates respiratory depression and hypotension
‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepines: Potentiates sedation, increases apnea risk
‚Ä¢ Inhalational anesthetics: Additive effects
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blockers: Increased hypotension/bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: May antagonize sedative effects
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Milk of Amnesia": White emulsion appearance
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset/offset: Ideal for short procedures (5-10 minute duration single dose)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension warning: Give slowly over 60 seconds, have fluids/vasopressors ready
‚Ä¢ No analgesic properties: Must co-administer analgesic for painful procedures
‚Ä¢ Propofol Infusion Syndrome (PRIS): Risk with >4-5 mg/kg/hr for >48h, especially in children
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection: Pretreat with lidocaine or use larger vein (antecubital)
‚Ä¢ Egg allergy: Contains egg lecithin - contraindicated in severe egg allergy
‚Ä¢ Caloric content: 1.1 kcal/mL (from lipid emulsion) - consider in long-term infusions

4 Core Drug 3: Etomidate

ETOMIDATE - HEMODYNAMICALLY NEUTRAL INDUCTION AGENT

Drug class: Imidazole derivative, GABA-A receptor agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Potentiates GABA-A receptor activity ‚Üí increased chloride conductance
‚Ä¢ Minimal effects on cardiovascular system (hemodynamically neutral)
‚Ä¢ Decreases cerebral metabolic rate and intracranial pressure
‚Ä¢ Maintains cerebral perfusion pressure (minimal effect on MAP)
‚Ä¢ Inhibits 11Œ≤-hydroxylase ‚Üí suppresses cortisol synthesis (dose-dependent)
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset (30-60 seconds) due to high lipid solubility
‚Ä¢ Short duration (3-5 minutes) due to rapid redistribution
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Induction agent for RSI (especially in hemodynamically unstable patients)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation for cardioversion (hemodynamically stable)
‚Ä¢ Induction in patients with coronary artery disease, heart failure
‚Ä¢ Patients with hypovolemia, sepsis, cardiac tamponade
‚Ä¢ Neurosurgical patients (reduces ICP while maintaining CPP)
‚Ä¢ Elderly patients with cardiovascular compromise
‚Ä¢ Patients on beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors (less hypotension risk)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to etomidate
‚Ä¢ Porphyria (relative contraindication)
‚Ä¢ Adrenal insufficiency (exacerbates adrenal suppression)
‚Ä¢ Sepsis (relative - adrenal suppression concern, but often still used)
‚Ä¢ Not for prolonged infusion (adrenal suppression)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (Category C, limited data)
‚Ä¢ Children (relative - more myoclonus, limited evidence in sepsis)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 0.3 mg/kg IV (typical 20 mg for 70 kg adult)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV
‚Ä¢ Elderly/debilitated: 0.2-0.3 mg/kg IV (reduce dose by 30-50%)
‚Ä¢ Cardioversion: 0.15-0.3 mg/kg IV
‚Ä¢ Single dose only - not for repeated dosing or infusion
‚Ä¢ Max dose: Usually 40 mg (rarely needed)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI induction: 0.2-0.3 mg/kg IV
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV
‚Ä¢ Controversial in pediatric sepsis due to adrenal suppression concerns
‚Ä¢ Neonates/infants: Limited data, use with caution
‚Ä¢ Max pediatric dose: 20-30 mg based on weight
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (painful on injection)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 2 mg/mL (10 mL = 20 mg, 20 mL = 40 mg)
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted over 30-60 seconds
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection: Very common - can pretreat with lidocaine or dilute
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Can dilute with NS to reduce pain (but not typically done in RSI)
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most IV solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Myoclonus (involuntary muscle movements) - 30-60% of patients
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection (30-50%)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (30-40%, especially with movement during recovery)
‚Ä¢ Adrenal suppression (dose-dependent, single dose suppresses 6-12 hours)
‚Ä¢ Hiccups
‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression (less than other induction agents)
‚Ä¢ Thrombophlebitis
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Adrenal insufficiency (with repeated dosing or infusion)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (rare, usually in epileptics)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Hemolysis (with propylene glycol formulation, rare with newer lipid emulsion)
‚Ä¢ Prolonged sedation in elderly (reduced clearance)
‚Ä¢ Increased mortality in sepsis (controversial - single dose probably safe)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (rare, usually with underlying cardiac disease)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioids/benzodiazepines: Potentiates respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Fentanyl: May reduce myoclonus when given before etomidate
‚Ä¢ CYP3A4 inhibitors: May prolong effect (ketoconazole, erythromycin)
‚Ä¢ CYP3A4 inducers: May shorten effect (rifampin, carbamazepine)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol: Additive CNS depression
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: May reduce seizure threshold
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Hemodynamically neutral": First choice for unstable patients with unknown volume status
‚Ä¢ Myoclonus: Not seizures - benign, self-limited, reduced by fentanyl pre-treatment
‚Ä¢ Adrenal suppression: Single dose suppresses cortisol for 6-12 hours - CONTROVERSIAL in sepsis
‚Ä¢ Sepsis controversy: Single dose probably safe, avoid repeated doses/infusions
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection: Pretreat with lidocaine or give through fast-flowing IV
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol formulation: Older formulation causes more pain/hemolysis - lipid emulsion preferred
‚Ä¢ Rapid recovery: 3-5 minutes - good for brief procedures
‚Ä¢ Not for analgesia: No pain relief - must co-administer analgesic for painful procedures

5 Core Drug 4: Succinylcholine

SUCCINYLCHOLINE - DEPOLARIZING NEUROMUSCULAR BLOCKING AGENT

Drug class: Depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at neuromuscular junction ‚Üí persistent depolarization
‚Ä¢ Initial fasciculations (muscle contractions) followed by paralysis
‚Ä¢ Phase I block: Persistent depolarization prevents further muscle contraction
‚Ä¢ Phase II block: With high/repeated doses, becomes non-depolarizing block
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset (30-60 seconds) due to structural similarity to acetylcholine
‚Ä¢ Short duration (5-10 minutes) due to rapid metabolism by plasma pseudocholinesterase
‚Ä¢ NOT reversible with neostigmine (anticholinesterases may prolong Phase II block)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI) - gold standard for rapid onset paralysis
‚Ä¢ Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) - to prevent musculoskeletal complications
‚Ä¢ Short surgical procedures requiring muscle relaxation
‚Ä¢ Laryngospasm refractory to other treatments
‚Ä¢ Emergency situations requiring rapid paralysis (traction on fetal head, laryngospasm)
‚Ä¢ When rapid recovery of spontaneous ventilation is desired
‚Ä¢ Patients with difficult airway where short duration is advantageous
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known malignant hyperthermia susceptibility (trigger agent)
‚Ä¢ History of malignant hyperthermia in patient or family
‚Ä¢ Major burns (>24 hours post-burn to 2 years post-healing)
‚Ä¢ Major trauma/crush injury (>24 hours to 2 years post-injury)
‚Ä¢ Spinal cord injury with paralysis (>24 hours to 2 years post-injury)
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular diseases (ALS, muscular dystrophy, myasthenia gravis)
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia or conditions predisposing to hyperkalemia
‚Ä¢ Pseudocholinesterase deficiency (prolonged paralysis)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI paralysis: 1-1.5 mg/kg IV (typical 100 mg for 70 kg adult)
‚Ä¢ IM administration: 3-4 mg/kg IM (max 150 mg per site)
‚Ä¢ Infusion for continuous paralysis: NOT recommended (risk of Phase II block)
‚Ä¢ Reduced dose with pseudocholinesterase deficiency: 0.5 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Repeat doses: Avoid if possible - increases risk of Phase II block, bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Max single dose: 150 mg IV
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI paralysis: 1-2 mg/kg IV (higher mg/kg than adults)
‚Ä¢ IM administration: 4-5 mg/kg IM (max 150 mg)
‚Ä¢ Infants (<2 years): 2 mg/kg IV (higher dose due to larger volume of distribution)
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 2 mg/kg IV, pre-treat with atropine 0.02 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia risk: Pre-treat with atropine 0.02 mg/kg if <5 years or second dose
‚Ä¢ Contraindications similar to adults (MH, burns, trauma, etc.)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, rarely subcutaneous
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 20 mg/mL (10 mL = 200 mg), 50 mg/mL (10 mL = 500 mg), 100 mg/mL (10 mL = 1000 mg)
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted rapidly (over 5-10 seconds)
‚Ä¢ IM administration: Deep IM injection, onset 2-3 minutes
‚Ä¢ Storage: Refrigerate (2-8¬∞C), but stable at room temperature for 14 days
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most IV solutions, acidic pH
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Fasciculations (muscle twitching) - precedes paralysis
‚Ä¢ Postoperative myalgia (24-48 hours after administration)
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia (especially with repeat doses, pediatric patients)
‚Ä¢ Increased intraocular pressure (transient, 5-10 minutes)
‚Ä¢ Increased intracranial pressure (transient)
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia (0.5-1 mEq/L increase normally)
‚Ä¢ Malignant hyperthermia (in susceptible individuals)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Massive hyperkalemia and cardiac arrest (in burns, trauma, neuromuscular disease)
‚Ä¢ Malignant hyperthermia (MH) - hypermetabolic crisis, fatal if untreated
‚Ä¢ Prolonged paralysis (pseudocholinesterase deficiency - minutes to hours)
‚Ä¢ Phase II block with high/repeated doses (prolonged non-depolarizing block)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest from profound bradycardia (especially pediatric, repeat doses)
‚Ä¢ Masseter spasm (may progress to MH)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Aminoglycosides, magnesium: Potentiate neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Lithium: Prolongs neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Cyclophosphamide, echothiophate: Inhibit pseudocholinesterase ‚Üí prolonged paralysis
‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers, digoxin: Increased bradycardia risk
‚Ä¢ Inhalational anesthetics: Trigger MH in susceptible individuals
‚Ä¢ Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors: Prolong Phase II block
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Gold standard for RSI": Fastest onset (30-60 sec), shortest duration (5-10 min)
‚Ä¢ Absolute contraindications: MH susceptibility, major burns (>24h), major trauma (>24h), hyperkalemia
‚Ä¢ Fasciculations: Can be reduced by "defasciculating dose" of non-depolarizer (rocuronium 0.06 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia prevention: Pre-treat pediatric patients or if giving repeat dose with atropine
‚Ä¢ Pseudocholinesterase deficiency: 1:3000 population, prolonged paralysis (hours)
‚Ä¢ MH treatment: Dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV, repeat to max 10 mg/kg, cooling, supportive care
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia risk: Avoid in renal failure, burns >24h old, crush injury, neuromuscular disease
‚Ä¢ "Cannot intubate, cannot ventilate": Short duration allows return of spontaneous breathing

6 Core Drug 5: Rocuronium

ROCURONIUM - NON-DEPOLARIZING NEUROMUSCULAR BLOCKING AGENT

Drug class: Aminosteroid, non-depolarizing neuromuscular blocking agent
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitive antagonist at nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at neuromuscular junction
‚Ä¢ Prevents acetylcholine from binding ‚Üí prevents depolarization ‚Üí paralysis
‚Ä¢ Non-depolarizing block (different mechanism from succinylcholine)
‚Ä¢ Intermediate onset (60-90 seconds with higher doses)
‚Ä¢ Intermediate duration (30-60 minutes with standard doses)
‚Ä¢ Reversible with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (neostigmine, sugammadex)
‚Ä¢ Minimal cardiovascular effects (no histamine release at standard doses)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Rapid Sequence Intubation (alternative to succinylcholine)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance of paralysis during surgery or mechanical ventilation
‚Ä¢ Patients with contraindications to succinylcholine (MH, burns, trauma, hyperkalemia)
‚Ä¢ Facilitation of tracheal intubation in operating room
‚Ä¢ ICU paralysis for ventilator synchrony (ARDS, status asthmaticus)
‚Ä¢ Continuous infusion for prolonged paralysis
‚Ä¢ Defasciculation before succinylcholine (low dose: 0.06 mg/kg)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to rocuronium or other aminosteroid NMBAs
‚Ä¢ Not for use when rapid return of spontaneous ventilation is critical
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular diseases (may have prolonged effect)
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment (prolonged duration)
‚Ä¢ Not as sole agent for RSI in known/suspected difficult airway (long duration)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (Category B, but generally safe)
‚Ä¢ No absolute contraindications like succinylcholine
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI paralysis: 1.2 mg/kg IV (typical 100 mg for 80 kg adult)
‚Ä¢ Facilitating intubation: 0.6 mg/kg IV (onset 90-120 seconds)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance dosing: 0.1-0.2 mg/kg IV q20-45min as needed
‚Ä¢ Continuous infusion: 0.3-1.2 mg/kg/hr after loading dose
‚Ä¢ Defasciculation: 0.06 mg/kg IV 3 minutes before succinylcholine
‚Ä¢ Reversal: Neostigmine 0.04-0.07 mg/kg + glycopyrrolate OR Sugammadex 16 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Max single dose: 1.2 mg/kg (higher doses don't significantly speed onset)

‚Ä¢ Infants (1-12 months): 0.9-1.2 mg/kg IV (similar to older children)
‚Ä¢ Neonates (<1 month): 0.45-0.6 mg/kg IV (reduced dose, prolonged effect)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 0.075-0.125 mg/kg IV as needed
‚Ä¢ Continuous infusion: 0.3-1.2 mg/kg/hr
‚Ä¢ Duration: Longer in neonates/infants due to immature hepatic function
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (not for IM use)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10 mg/mL (5 mL = 50 mg, 10 mL = 100 mg)
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted rapidly (over 5-10 seconds)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Dilute to 0.5-1 mg/mL in NS or D5W
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Compatible with most IV solutions, alkaline pH may precipitate
‚Ä¢ Storage: Room temperature, protect from light
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Tachycardia (dose-dependent, at higher doses >1.2 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (rare at standard doses)
‚Ä¢ Bronchospasm (rare)
‚Ä¢ Rash, flushing (rare, minimal histamine release)
‚Ä¢ Prolonged paralysis (in hepatic/renal impairment, hypothermia)
‚Ä¢ Residual neuromuscular blockade (inadequate reversal)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare: 1:10,000)
‚Ä¢ Prolonged paralysis/ICU-acquired weakness (with prolonged infusion)
‚Ä¢ Residual paralysis post-reversal (inadequate monitoring/reversal)
‚Ä¢ Inability to ventilate if intubation fails (long duration)
‚Ä¢ Malignant hyperthermia (rare association, not a trigger like succinylcholine)
‚Ä¢ Acute quadriplegic myopathy syndrome (with steroids in ICU)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Aminoglycosides, vancomycin, magnesium: Potentiate neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Inhalational anesthetics: Potentiate neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Lithium, quinidine: Prolong neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Carbamazepine, phenytoin: Resistance to neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Cyclosporine: Prolongs neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Succinylcholine: Potentiates if given shortly after
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Succinylcholine alternative": Use when succinylcholine contraindicated
‚Ä¢ Onset with high dose (1.2 mg/kg): 60-90 seconds - almost as fast as succinylcholine
‚Ä¢ Duration: 30-60 minutes - LONG compared to succinylcholine (5-10 min)
‚Ä¢ Reversal options: Traditional (neostigmine + glycopyrrolate) OR Sugammadex (fast, complete)
‚Ä¢ Sugammadex: 16 mg/kg reverses 1.2 mg/kg rocuronium in <3 minutes - game changer!
‚Ä¢ No MH risk: Safe in malignant hyperthermia susceptible patients
‚Ä¢ No hyperkalemia risk: Safe in burns, trauma, renal failure, neuromuscular disease
‚Ä¢ ICU paralysis: Use with caution - daily wake-up, monitor with train-of-four, avoid with steroids

7 Core Drug 6: Lidocaine (Airway/Local)

LIDOCAINE - LOCAL ANESTHETIC WITH MULTIPLE AIRWAY USES

Drug class: Amide local anesthetic, Class IB antiarrhythmic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels ‚Üí prevents nerve depolarization and conduction
‚Ä¢ For airway: Topical anesthesia of oropharynx, larynx, trachea
‚Ä¢ For RSI pre-treatment: Suppresses cough reflex and attenuates hemodynamic response
‚Ä¢ For ICP reduction: Theoretical neuroprotective effect via cerebral vasoconstriction
‚Ä¢ Antiarrhythmic: Suppresses ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF)
‚Ä¢ Systemic absorption: Can cause local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)
‚Ä¢ Metabolized by liver (CYP3A4, CYP1A2), active metabolites accumulate in renal failure
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Topical anesthesia for awake intubation, bronchoscopy
‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment for head injury (ICP protection)
‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment for reactive airway disease (bronchospasm prevention)
‚Ä¢ Local anesthesia for procedures (laceration repair, abscess drainage, regional blocks)
‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF, PVCs)
‚Ä¢ Post-herpetic neuralgia (topical patches)
‚Ä¢ Neuropathic pain (oral, though not first-line)
‚Ä¢ Mucosal anesthesia (gels, sprays for urethral/rectal procedures)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to lidocaine or other amide local anesthetics
‚Ä¢ Adam-Stokes syndrome, severe heart block (without pacemaker)
‚Ä¢ Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome (use with caution)
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment (reduced metabolism)
‚Ä¢ Methemoglobinemia (especially with prilocaine, but caution with all locals)
‚Ä¢ Injection into infected/inflamed tissue (altered absorption)
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (may worsen weakness)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment (ICP): 1.5 mg/kg IV 90 seconds before induction
‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment (asthma): 1.5 mg/kg IV 3 minutes before induction
‚Ä¢ Topical airway (4% spray): 4 mL maximum (160 mg)
‚Ä¢ Local infiltration (1%): 4.5 mg/kg maximum (7 mg/kg with epinephrine)
‚Ä¢ IV antiarrhythmic: 1-1.5 mg/kg bolus, then 1-4 mg/min infusion
‚Ä¢ Regional nerve block: Dose varies by block, use smallest effective dose
‚Ä¢ MAX dose (plain): 4.5 mg/kg (300 mg), (with epi): 7 mg/kg (500 mg)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment: 1.5 mg/kg IV (same as adults)
‚Ä¢ Topical airway: 3 mg/kg maximum (4% solution)
‚Ä¢ Local infiltration: 3-5 mg/kg maximum (with/without epinephrine)
‚Ä¢ IV antiarrhythmic: 1 mg/kg bolus, may repeat √ó2, then 20-50 mcg/kg/min infusion
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Reduced doses, consult specialist (reduced metabolism)
‚Ä¢ ALWAYS calculate mg/kg: Critical to avoid toxicity in children
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV, topical, infiltration, regional block, endotracheal, intra-articular
‚Ä¢ Concentrations: 0.5%, 1%, 2% for injection; 4% for topical; 5% ointment; 10% spray
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Give undiluted over 1-2 minutes
‚Ä¢ For infusion: 1-2 g in 250-500 mL D5W (4-8 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ Topical airway: Use mucosal atomizer device (MAD) for spray
‚Ä¢ With epinephrine: 1:100,000 or 1:200,000 - reduces absorption, prolongs duration
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ CNS: Lightheadedness, drowsiness, tinnitus, perioral tingling
‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular: Hypotension, bradycardia (at high systemic levels)
‚Ä¢ Allergic: Rash, urticaria (rare with amides)
‚Ä¢ Methemoglobinemia (with high doses, especially in infants)
‚Ä¢ Transient neurological symptoms (with spinal anesthesia)
‚Ä¢ Pain on injection (burning sensation)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST) - seizures, cardiovascular collapse
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare with amides, more common with esters)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (from profound bradycardia, asystole)
‚Ä¢ Methemoglobinemia requiring methylene blue
‚Ä¢ Neurological injury (from spinal/epidural injection)
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis (with epinephrine in end-arterial areas: fingers, toes, penis, ears)
‚Ä¢ Chondrolysis (with intra-articular infusion)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Increased lidocaine levels (reduced hepatic blood flow)
‚Ä¢ CYP3A4 inhibitors: Increased lidocaine levels (amiodarone, cimetidine, fluconazole)
‚Ä¢ Other local anesthetics: Additive toxicity
‚Ä¢ Succinylcholine: Prolongs neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Antiarrhythmics: Additive cardiac effects (Class I agents)
‚Ä¢ MAOIs, TCAs: Potentiate pressor effect of epinephrine in local anesthetics
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "LAST" - Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity: CNS symptoms first (tingling, seizures), then cardiac (CV collapse)
‚Ä¢ LAST treatment: 20% Lipid Emulsion 1.5 mL/kg bolus, then 0.25 mL/kg/min infusion, ACLS
‚Ä¢ Maximum doses: 4.5 mg/kg plain, 7 mg/kg with epinephrine - CALCULATE mg/kg!
‚Ä¢ RSI pre-treatment timing: ICP - 90 sec before induction; Asthma - 3 min before induction
‚Ä¢ Topical airway: Wait 3-5 minutes after spray for full effect, use <4 mL of 4% (160 mg)
‚Ä¢ With epinephrine: NEVER use in end-arterial areas (fingers, toes, penis, ears, nose tip)
‚Ä¢ Pediatric warning: More susceptible to LAST - use lowest effective dose
‚Ä¢ IV antiarrhythmic: Half-life increases with infusion duration - monitor for toxicity
üíâ LOCAL ANESTHETIC SYSTEMIC TOXICITY (LAST) MANAGEMENT:

EARLY SIGNS (CNS):
‚Ä¢ Perioral tingling, metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Tinnitus, dizziness, confusion
‚Ä¢ Visual disturbances, slurred speech
‚Ä¢ Muscle twitching, seizures

LATE SIGNS (CARDIAC):
‚Ä¢ Hypotension, bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF)
‚Ä¢ Asystole, cardiovascular collapse

TREATMENT:
1. Stop injection immediately
2. Call for help and lipid emulsion
3. Airway management (may need intubation)
4. Seizure control: Benzodiazepines (midazolam)
5. ACLS protocols (avoid vasopressin, calcium channel blockers, beta-blockers)
6. 20% Lipid Emulsion: 1.5 mL/kg bolus, then 0.25 mL/kg/min infusion
7. Consider cardiopulmonary bypass if available

8.2 Neuromuscular Blockade Monitoring & Reversal

üß† MONITORING NEUROMUSCULAR BLOCKADE:

TRAIN-OF-FOUR (TOF) MONITORING:
‚Ä¢ 4 supramaximal stimuli at 2 Hz (0.5 sec apart)
‚Ä¢ Count number of twitches: 4/4 = minimal blockade, 0/4 = complete blockade
‚Ä¢ Useful for determining depth of blockade and timing of reversal

REVERSAL AGENTS:
1. Traditional (Neostigmine + Glycopyrrolate):
‚Ä¢ Neostigmine 0.04-0.07 mg/kg (max 5 mg)
‚Ä¢ Glycopyrrolate 0.2 mg per 1 mg neostigmine
‚Ä¢ Wait for at least 1-2 twitches before reversal
‚Ä¢ Onset 5-10 minutes, may not reverse deep blockade

2. Sugammadex (Bridion¬Æ):
‚Ä¢ Cyclodextrin that encapsulates rocuronium/vecuronium
‚Ä¢ Dose: 16 mg/kg for 1.2 mg/kg rocuronium, 4 mg/kg for minimal blockade
‚Ä¢ Works in <3 minutes, even with deep blockade
‚Ä¢ Expensive (~$100 per vial) but game-changing
‚Ä¢ No anticholinergic side effects

9 Procedural Sedation & Analgesia (PSA)

9.1 Common PSA Combinations

MINIMAL SEDATION (ANXIOLYSIS):
‚Ä¢ Patient responds normally to verbal commands
‚Ä¢ Drugs: Midazolam 1-2 mg IV, titrated to effect
‚Ä¢ Procedures: Minor procedures, anxiety reduction

MODERATE SEDATION ("CONSCIOUS SEDATION"):
‚Ä¢ Purposeful response to verbal/tactile stimulation
‚Ä¢ Combinations:
  ‚Ä¢ Ketamine 0.5-1 mg/kg + Midazolam 0.02-0.04 mg/kg
  ‚Ä¢ Propofol 0.5-1 mg/kg + Fentanyl 1-2 mcg/kg
  ‚Ä¢ Fentanyl 1-2 mcg/kg + Midazolam 0.02-0.05 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Procedures: Fracture reduction, abscess drainage, cardioversion

DEEP SEDATION:
‚Ä¢ Purposeful response after repeated/painful stimulation
‚Ä¢ Drugs: Higher doses of above combinations
‚Ä¢ Requires advanced airway skills present
‚Ä¢ Procedures: Complex reductions, pediatric imaging

DISSOCIATIVE SEDATION (KETAMINE):
‚Ä¢ Trance-like state, maintains airway reflexes
‚Ä¢ Ketamine 1-2 mg/kg IV or 4-5 mg/kg IM
‚Ä¢ Pre-treat with atropine (secretions) and midazolam (emergence reactions)
‚Ä¢ Ideal for pediatric procedures, agitated patients

9.2 Monitoring During PSA

  1. Pre-procedure:
    • NPO status (if elective): 2h clear liquids, 6h light meal, 8h heavy meal
    • Airway assessment (Mallampati, thyromental distance, neck mobility)
    • ASA classification, comorbidities, allergies
    • Informed consent (risks: hypoxia, apnea, aspiration, death)
  2. During procedure:
    • Continuous pulse oximetry and ECG
    • Blood pressure every 3-5 minutes
    • Respiratory rate continuously
    • Capnography if available (early detection of apnea)
    • Level of sedation (modified Ramsay scale)
    • Dedicated observer (not performing procedure)
  3. Post-procedure:
    • Recovery until discharge criteria met (Aldrete score ‚â•9)
    • Monitoring for 30-120 minutes depending on drugs used
    • Discharge instructions (no driving, no signing legal documents for 24h)
    • Follow-up for potential complications
