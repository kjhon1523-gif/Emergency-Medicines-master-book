0 Introduction: The Antidote Arsenal

‚ö†Ô∏è POISONS WORK FAST, ANTIDOTES WORK FASTER: For organophosphates, minutes matter. For opioids, seconds count. For paracetamol, hours determine outcome. Know your antidotes, know them well, and give them early.
üß† Core Concept: Poisoning management follows three principles:
1. Supportive care (ABCs, monitoring, specific treatments)
2. Decontamination (charcoal, gastric lavage, skin decontamination)
3. Antidotes (specific reversal agents)
This module focuses on the high-yield antidotes you MUST know.

1 Toxidromes & Antidote Grid

Toxidrome: A constellation of signs and symptoms characteristic of a particular class of poison. Recognizing toxidromes allows for targeted treatment even before laboratory confirmation.

üß™ MUST-KNOW ANTIDOTES (Exam Favorites):

üíÄ Organophosphate/Carbamate: ATROPINE + PRALIDOXIME
‚Ä¢ Atropine for muscarinic effects, PAM for nicotinic effects
‚Ä¢ Give until secretions dry (atropinization)

‚Ä¢ Works in 1-2 minutes IV, 30-90 minute duration
‚Ä¢ Short-acting - may need infusion for long-acting opioids

‚Ä¢ Use Rumack-Matthew nomogram
‚Ä¢ Works up to 24+ hours post-ingestion (but better early)

‚Ä¢ Not for caustics, hydrocarbons, lithium, iron

2 Core Drug 1: Atropine (Poisoning Focus)

ATROPINE - ANTIDOTE FOR ORGANOPHOSPHATE/CARBAMATE POISONING

Drug class: Anticholinergic, competitive muscarinic antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitive inhibition of acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors
‚Ä¢ Reverses excessive parasympathetic activity in cholinergic crisis
‚Ä¢ Dries secretions, reduces bronchorrhea, improves oxygenation
‚Ä¢ Increases heart rate (reverses bradycardia)
‚Ä¢ Dilates pupils (reverses miosis)
‚Ä¢ Does NOT reverse nicotinic effects (muscle weakness, fasciculations)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Organophosphate poisoning (insecticides, nerve agents)
‚Ä¢ Carbamate poisoning (similar to organophosphates)
‚Ä¢ Mushroom poisoning (muscarinic-type: Clitocybe, Inocybe)
‚Ä¢ Symptomatic bradycardia (covered in Module 2)
‚Ä¢ Pre-anesthetic medication (reduce secretions)
‚Ä¢ Nerve agent exposure (sarin, VX, etc.)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Narrow-angle glaucoma (relative - life vs vision)
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (can worsen weakness)
‚Ä¢ Obstructive uropathy (BPH)
‚Ä¢ Obstructive GI disorders
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia (may worsen - but often needed in poisoning)
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to atropine
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Organophosphate poisoning: 2-5 mg IV bolus initially
‚Ä¢ Repeat: Double the dose every 5-10 minutes until atropinization
‚Ä¢ Severe cases: May require 20-50 mg in first hour, hundreds of mg total
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 10-20% of loading dose per hour as continuous infusion
‚Ä¢ Mushroom poisoning: 1-2 mg IV, repeat as needed
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Organophosphate poisoning: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg IV initially (min 0.1 mg)
‚Ä¢ Repeat: Double every 5-10 minutes until atropinization
‚Ä¢ Severe cases: May require 1-2 mg/kg in first 24 hours
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 0.02-0.08 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Nerve agent exposure: Auto-injector: 2 mg IM (‚â•10 years), 1 mg IM (2-9 years)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, IO, endotracheal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 0.1 mg/mL, 0.5 mg/mL, 1 mg/mL ampoules
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give rapidly (over 1-2 minutes) in poisoning
‚Ä¢ For IM: Auto-injectors available for nerve agent exposure
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Mix in NS or D5W, typical concentration 0.1-1 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ Compatible with most IV solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Dry mouth, dry skin (desired in poisoning)
‚Ä¢ Blurred vision, photophobia
‚Ä¢ Urinary retention
‚Ä¢ Constipation
‚Ä¢ Flushing, fever (especially children)
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia (expected and often desired)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Delirium, hallucinations (central anticholinergic syndrome)
‚Ä¢ Hyperthermia (reduced sweating)
‚Ä¢ Ileus, urinary retention requiring catheterization
‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias (with very high doses)
‚Ä¢ Paradoxical bradycardia with low doses (<0.5 mg in adults)
‚Ä¢ Angle-closure glaucoma (in susceptible individuals)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other anticholinergics: Additive effects (TCAs, antihistamines, antipsychotics)
‚Ä¢ Cholinergic drugs: Antagonizes effects (neostigmine, pilocarpine)
‚Ä¢ Pralidoxime: Synergistic - use together in organophosphate poisoning
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: May increase heart rate
‚Ä¢ Ketamine: Often given together (reduces secretions)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Secretions, secretions, secretions" - main target in poisoning
‚Ä¢ Atropinization endpoints: Clear chest (no wheezes/rales), dry axillae, HR >80 bpm, pupils dilating
‚Ä¢ Double dosing: Start 2 mg IV, double every 5-10 min until endpoints reached
‚Ä¢ Aging/reactivation: Organophosphates bind irreversibly - may need atropine for days
‚Ä¢ Carbamates vs organophosphates: Carbamates reversible - less PAM needed
‚Ä¢ Nerve agents: Auto-injectors (AtroPen¬Æ): 2 mg IM, may need 3 injections initially
‚Ä¢ Monitor: Secretions, lung sounds, heart rate, pupil size, temperature
‚Ä¢ Weaning: Taper slowly over days to avoid rebound cholinergic crisis
‚ò†Ô∏è ORGANOPHOSPHATE POISONING (SLUDGE + BBB):
‚Ä¢ Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis
‚Ä¢ Bronchospasm, Bradycardia, Bronchorrhea
‚Ä¢ PLUS: Muscle fasciculations, weakness, paralysis (nicotinic effects)
‚Ä¢ TREATMENT: 1. DECONTAMINATION ‚Üí 2. ATROPINE ‚Üí 3. PRALIDOXIME

3 Core Drug 2: Pralidoxime (PAM)

PRALIDOXIME (PAM) - REACTIVATES CHOLINESTERASE IN ORGANOPHOSPHATE POISONING

Drug class: Oxime, cholinesterase reactivator
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Reactivates acetylcholinesterase (AChE) by removing organophosphate molecule
‚Ä¢ Cleaves phosphorylated AChE ‚Üí restores enzyme function
‚Ä¢ Only effective before "aging" occurs (irreversible binding)
‚Ä¢ Aging time varies: Sarin (5 hours), VX (48 hours), soman (2 minutes!)
‚Ä¢ Also has some direct anticholinergic effects
‚Ä¢ Does NOT cross blood-brain barrier well (limited CNS effect)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Organophosphate poisoning (insecticides, nerve agents)
‚Ä¢ Carbamate poisoning (controversial - may worsen in some cases)
‚Ä¢ Nerve agent exposure (military/terrorism incidents)
‚Ä¢ Always use with atropine - PAM alone is insufficient
‚Ä¢ Not for pure muscarinic poisoning (mushrooms, pilocarpine)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to pralidoxime
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (relative - may worsen)
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment (requires dose adjustment)
‚Ä¢ Carbamate poisoning (relative - may worsen with certain carbamates)
‚Ä¢ No absolute contraindication in life-threatening poisoning
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Loading dose: 1-2 g IV over 15-30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Alternative loading: 30 mg/kg IV over 30 minutes (max 2 g)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance infusion: 500 mg/hr OR 8-10 mg/kg/hr
‚Ä¢ Intermittent dosing: 1 g IV every 4-12 hours (less preferred)
‚Ä¢ Severe cases: May give second 1-2 g dose 1 hour after first
‚Ä¢ Nerve agents: Auto-injector: 600 mg IM (may repeat √ó2)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Loading dose: 25-50 mg/kg IV over 15-30 minutes (max 2 g)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 10-20 mg/kg/hr IV infusion
‚Ä¢ Intermittent: 25-50 mg/kg every 4-12 hours
‚Ä¢ Nerve agent: Auto-injector: 15 mg/kg IM (600 mg if >40 kg)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV infusion (preferred), IM, slow IV push
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 1 g vial (powder), reconstitute with 20 mL sterile water = 50 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ For IV infusion: Dilute in 100-250 mL NS, infuse over 15-30 min
‚Ä¢ For IM: Auto-injector (600 mg/2 mL) - may use up to 3 injections
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with: Thiopental, many other drugs - use dedicated line
‚Ä¢ Stability: Use within hours of reconstitution (check manufacturer)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Hypertension, tachycardia
‚Ä¢ Dizziness, headache, blurred vision
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Pain at injection site (IM route)
‚Ä¢ Hyperventilation
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Laryngospasm, muscle rigidity (with rapid IV administration)
‚Ä¢ Respiratory arrest (rare)
‚Ä¢ Hypertension crisis
‚Ä¢ Hepatitis, elevated LFTs
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment (high doses)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (rare)
‚Ä¢ Cholinergic crisis if stopped abruptly
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Atropine: Synergistic - always use together
‚Ä¢ Barbiturates: May be antagonized (metabolism induction)
‚Ä¢ Succinylcholine: Prolonged paralysis in organophosphate poisoning
‚Ä¢ Theophylline, aminophylline: Increased toxicity risk
‚Ä¢ Morphine: May be potentiated
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Time is brain (and muscle)" - give within hours of exposure
‚Ä¢ Aging matters: Soman ages in 2 minutes - PAM useless unless given immediately
‚Ä¢ Nicotinic effects: PAM helps muscle weakness/fasciculations; atropine does not
‚Ä¢ Carbamate caution: Some carbamates (carbaryl) may worsen with PAM
‚Ä¢ Red urine: Normal - drug and metabolites turn urine red-brown
‚Ä¢ Duration: Half-life 1-2 hours - continuous infusion preferred
‚Ä¢ Monitoring: Muscle strength, respiratory function, atropine requirements
‚Ä¢ Weaning: Continue for 24-48 hours after atropine stopped, taper slowly
üß† ATROPINE + PAM SYNERGY:
‚Ä¢ Atropine: Treats muscarinic effects (secretions, bradycardia, bronchorrhea)
‚Ä¢ Pralidoxime: Treats nicotinic effects (muscle weakness, fasciculations, paralysis)
‚Ä¢ Both are needed for complete treatment of organophosphate poisoning

4 Core Drug 3: Naloxone (Poisoning Focus)

NALOXONE - LIFESAVER FOR OPIOID OVERDOSE

Drug class: Competitive opioid receptor antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitive antagonist at mu, kappa, and delta opioid receptors
‚Ä¢ Higher affinity than most opioids ‚Üí displaces opioids from receptors
‚Ä¢ Reverses respiratory depression, sedation, hypotension, miosis
‚Ä¢ Pure antagonist - no opioid agonist activity
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset but short duration (shorter than most opioids)
‚Ä¢ Also reverses opioid-induced pruritus, urinary retention
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose (respiratory depression <12/min, pinpoint pupils, coma)
‚Ä¢ Post-operative opioid reversal (respiratory depression)
‚Ä¢ Diagnostic: Suspected opioid overdose (therapeutic trial)
‚Ä¢ Opioid-induced pruritus (low dose 0.04-0.08 mg IV)
‚Ä¢ Neonatal respiratory depression from maternal opioids
‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose in pregnancy (saves both mother and fetus)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to naloxone (rare)
‚Ä¢ Note: No absolute contraindications in opioid overdose (life-saving)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Opioid-dependent patients (precipitates withdrawal)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac patients (may cause pulmonary edema, arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Neonates of opioid-dependent mothers (use with caution - withdrawal)
‚Ä¢ Mixed overdose with non-opioid sedatives (may cause seizures)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose (unknown opioid): 0.4-2 mg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min (max 10 mg)
‚Ä¢ Post-op reversal: 0.1-0.2 mg IV every 2-3 min to desired effect
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 2/3 of initial effective dose per hour (e.g., 0.4 mg/hr)
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: 2 mg (1 mg per nostril) - pre-hospital use
‚Ä¢ Auto-injector: 0.4 mg IM (Evzio¬Æ) or 2 mg IM (higher dose formulations)
‚Ä¢ Buprenorphine overdose: May need 2-10 mg (high receptor affinity)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min (max 2 mg)
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.0025-0.16 mg/kg/hr after initial bolus
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: 2 mg (same as adult - absorption varies)
‚Ä¢ Maternal opioid use: 0.1 mg/kg IM to mother before delivery
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (fastest), IM, SC, intranasal, endotracheal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 0.4 mg/mL, 1 mg/mL, 2 mg/2mL prefilled syringes
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give undiluted, rapid push for overdose, slow titration for post-op
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Mix 2 mg in 500 mL D5W or NS = 4 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: Use mucosal atomizer device or prefilled device (Narcan¬Æ)
‚Ä¢ Endotracheal: 2-2.5 times IV dose diluted in 5-10 mL NS
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Acute opioid withdrawal (agitation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia, hypertension
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Sweating, tremors
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (non-cardiogenic)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (rare, usually in mixed overdoses)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe opioid withdrawal (dangerous in opioid-dependent patients)
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (especially in cardiac patients)
‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias, cardiac arrest
‚Ä¢ Seizures (in mixed overdoses with pro-convulsants)
‚Ä¢ Hypertension ‚Üí cerebral hemorrhage (in hypertensive patients)
‚Ä¢ Re-sedation (when naloxone wears off before opioid)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioid agonists: Antagonizes effects (obvious)
‚Ä¢ Opioid partial agonists (buprenorphine): May require higher doses
‚Ä¢ Clonidine: May precipitate hypertension when reversing opioids
‚Ä¢ Flumazenil: Used together in mixed overdoses (opioid + benzodiazepine)
‚Ä¢ Naltrexone: Additive effects (both opioid antagonists)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Start low, go slow" in opioid-dependent patients to avoid severe withdrawal
‚Ä¢ Duration mismatch: Naloxone 30-90 min vs opioids hours to days ‚Üí RE-DOSE OFTEN
‚Ä¢ Observe period: At least 4-6 hours after last dose (longer for long-acting opioids)
‚Ä¢ Fentanyl analogs: May require higher doses (carfentanil up to 12 mg needed)
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: Takes 2-5 minutes vs 30-60 seconds IV
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema: Treat with oxygen, PEEP; usually resolves in 24-48 hours
‚Ä¢ Harm reduction: Provide take-home naloxone to at-risk patients
‚Ä¢ Mixed overdoses: May need both naloxone and flumazenil (give separately)
üíä OPIOID OVERDOSE MANAGEMENT:
1. ABCDE - especially Airway and Breathing (may need intubation)
2. Naloxone 0.4-2 mg IV/IM/IN - repeat every 2-3 min up to 10 mg
3. Observe 4-6 hours (longer for methadone, fentanyl patches)
4. Consider infusion if re-sedation occurs (2/3 of initial bolus per hour)
5. Treat complications (pulmonary edema, withdrawal, aspiration)

5 Core Drug 4: Acetylcysteine (NAC)

ACETYLCYSTEINE (NAC) - ANTIDOTE FOR PARACETAMOL OVERDOSE

Drug class: Thiol compound, glutathione precursor
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Replenishes glutathione stores ‚Üí detoxifies NAPQI (toxic paracetamol metabolite)
‚Ä¢ Directly conjugates with NAPQI as glutathione substitute
‚Ä¢ Antioxidant properties - scavenges free radicals
‚Ä¢ Mucolytic action (different mechanism - breaks disulfide bonds in mucus)
‚Ä¢ May improve hepatic blood flow
‚Ä¢ Multiple mechanisms of hepatoprotection
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Paracetamol (acetaminophen) overdose (within 24+ hours of ingestion)
‚Ä¢ Prevention of contrast-induced nephropathy (oral)
‚Ä¢ Mucolytic for thick secretions (nebulized, different dosing)
‚Ä¢ Diagnostic: Acetaminophen level in toxic range per Rumack-Matthew nomogram
‚Ä¢ Early acute liver failure (some evidence)
‚Ä¢ Carbon tetrachloride poisoning (limited evidence)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to acetylcysteine
‚Ä¢ Note: No absolute contraindication in paracetamol overdose (life-saving)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Asthma (nebulized form can cause bronchospasm)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (but benefits outweigh risks in overdose)
‚Ä¢ Caution in severe COPD/asthma when giving IV (anaphylactoid reactions)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ IV protocol (3-bag method):
  ‚Ä¢ Bag 1: 150 mg/kg in 200 mL D5W over 1 hour
  ‚Ä¢ Bag 2: 50 mg/kg in 500 mL D5W over 4 hours
  ‚Ä¢ Bag 3: 100 mg/kg in 1000 mL D5W over 16 hours
‚Ä¢ Oral protocol: 140 mg/kg loading, then 70 mg/kg every 4 hours √ó 17 doses
‚Ä¢ Late presentation (>24 hours): Start full protocol regardless of level
‚Ä¢ Mucolytic: 3-5 mL of 20% solution nebulized TID-QID (different use)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ IV protocol: Same weight-based dosing as adults
‚Ä¢ Oral protocol: Same as adults (140/70 mg/kg regimen)
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Limited data - consult toxicology/neonatology
‚Ä¢ Adjust fluid volumes for small children (may need more concentrated solutions)
‚Ä¢ Mucolytic: 3-5 mL of 10% solution nebulized
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), PO, nebulized
‚Ä¢ Preparation (IV): 200 mg/mL vial (10g/50mL), dilute in D5W only
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Use glass or PVC containers (not polyethylene)
‚Ä¢ Filters: Use 0.22 micron filter (IV administration set)
‚Ä¢ Oral: Dilute 20% solution 1:4 with juice/soda to mask taste (final 5%)
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with: Most other drugs - use dedicated line
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylactoid reactions (rash, flushing, bronchospasm) - IV route
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (especially oral route - "rotten egg" smell)
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Fever, chills
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (with rapid IV infusion)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe anaphylaxis (rare but serious)
‚Ä¢ Bronchospasm, respiratory distress (especially in asthmatics)
‚Ä¢ Angioedema, urticaria
‚Ä¢ Hypertension (paradoxical)
‚Ä¢ Hepatic encephalopathy (if given too late)
‚Ä¢ False-positive ketones in urine
‚Ä¢ Haemolysis in G6PD deficiency (theoretical)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Activated charcoal: Binds oral NAC - separate by 2 hours if possible
‚Ä¢ Nitroglycerin: Potentiates vasodilation ‚Üí severe hypotension
‚Ä¢ Antiemetics: May be needed for oral NAC administration
‚Ä¢ Paracetamol level: NAC does not affect assay
‚Ä¢ INR: May increase during treatment (hepatic injury)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ Rumack-Matthew nomogram: Use for single acute ingestions 4-24 hours post-ingestion
‚Ä¢ Treatment line: 150 mg/L (1000 Œºmol/L) at 4 hours, lower for extended-release
‚Ä¢ "150 rule": If level >150 mg/L at 4 hours ‚Üí TREAT
‚Ä¢ Late presentation: Start NAC if >24 hours and any detectable paracetamol or ALT elevated
‚Ä¢ Anaphylactoid reaction: Slow or stop infusion, give antihistamines, resume at slower rate
‚Ä¢ Extended-release: Check levels at 4 hours AND 8 hours, use lower treatment line
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy: NAC crosses placenta - treat mother to protect fetus
‚Ä¢ Monitoring: ALT, INR, creatinine, glucose during treatment
üß† PARACETAMOL OVERDOSE TIMELINE:
‚Ä¢ 0-24 hours: Often asymptomatic or mild GI symptoms
‚Ä¢ 24-72 hours: RUQ pain, elevated LFTs, rising INR
‚Ä¢ 72-96 hours: Peak hepatotoxicity (jaundice, encephalopathy)
‚Ä¢ >96 hours: Recovery or death (fulminant hepatic failure)
‚Ä¢ NAC works best early but has benefit even late (>24 hours)

6 Core Drug 5: Methylene Blue

METHYLENE BLUE - FOR METH AEMOGLOBINAEMIA

Drug class: Redox agent, electron acceptor
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Accepts electrons from NADPH via NADPH methaemoglobin reductase
‚Ä¢ Reduced to leukomethylene blue, which reduces methaemoglobin (MetHb) to haemoglobin
‚Ä¢ Requires functional NADPH pathway (G6PD dependent)
‚Ä¢ Also has mild antibacterial, antifungal properties
‚Ä¢ Urinary antiseptic (historical use)
‚Ä¢ Vasopressor at high doses (monoamine oxidase inhibition)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Symptomatic methaemoglobinaemia (dyspnoea, cyanosis, headache)
‚Ä¢ Methaemoglobin level >20% (or >10% with cardiac disease)
‚Ä¢ Known exposure to methaemoglobin-inducing agents
‚Ä¢ Ifosfamide-induced encephalopathy (different mechanism)
‚Ä¢ Vasoplegic syndrome post-cardiac surgery (high dose)
‚Ä¢ Cyanide poisoning (adjunct with sodium nitrite/thiosulfate)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ G6PD deficiency (may cause haemolysis)
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment (excreted renally)
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to methylene blue
‚Ä¢ Serotonin syndrome risk (MAOI properties)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - crosses placenta, fetal concerns)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent serotoninergic drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, tramadol, etc.)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Methaemoglobinaemia: 1-2 mg/kg IV over 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Repeat: May repeat 1 mg/kg in 1 hour if MetHb >30% or symptoms persist
‚Ä¢ Max total dose: 7 mg/kg (rarely needed)
‚Ä¢ Ifosfamide encephalopathy: 50 mg IV every 4-6 hours
‚Ä¢ Vasoplegic syndrome: 1.5 mg/kg over 1 hour, then 1 mg/kg/hr √ó 6 hours
‚Ä¢ Cyanide poisoning: Part of Cyanokit¬Æ protocol
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Methaemoglobinaemia: 1-2 mg/kg IV over 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Repeat: 1 mg/kg in 1 hour if needed
‚Ä¢ Infants: Same mg/kg dosing
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Use with caution - kernicterus risk
‚Ä¢ Max: 7 mg/kg total (as adult)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (slow push or infusion), oral (not for emergencies)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 1% solution = 10 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ For IV: Dilute in 50-100 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Direct IV push: Can give undiluted slowly (over 5+ minutes)
‚Ä¢ Extravasation: Causes tissue necrosis - ensure IV patency
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with: Many drugs - use dedicated line
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Blue-green discoloration of urine, skin, mucous membranes
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness, confusion
‚Ä¢ Sweating, fever
‚Ä¢ Injection site pain
‚Ä¢ Hypertension (high doses)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Haemolytic anaemia in G6PD deficiency
‚Ä¢ Serotonin syndrome (MAOI properties)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation
‚Ä¢ False low SpO‚ÇÇ readings (pulse oximetry interference)
‚Ä¢ Kernicterus in neonates (displaces bilirubin from albumin)
‚Ä¢ Methaemoglobinaemia paradoxically (at high doses >7 mg/kg)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Serotoninergic drugs: Serotonin syndrome risk (SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, tramadol)
‚Ä¢ MAOIs: Additive effects
‚Ä¢ Dapsone, nitrites, local anesthetics: May cause methaemoglobinaemia
‚Ä¢ Reducing agents (ascorbic acid): May reduce effectiveness
‚Ä¢ Chloroquine, primaquine: Increased haemolysis risk in G6PD deficiency
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Chocolate brown blood" - doesn't turn red with oxygen (bedside test)
‚Ä¢ Pulse oximetry: Reads 85% regardless of true saturation (plateau effect)
‚Ä¢ G6PD deficiency: Check if possible; if suspected, use lower dose or consider alternatives
‚Ä¢ Onset: 30-60 minutes, peak 1-2 hours
‚Ä¢ Repeat dosing: May need every 4-6 hours for dapsone poisoning (long half-life)
‚Ä¢ Refractory cases: Consider exchange transfusion, hyperbaric oxygen, ascorbic acid
‚Ä¢ Causes of methaemoglobinaemia: Nitrites/nitrates, dapsone, local anesthetics (prilocaine), aniline dyes
‚Ä¢ Monitoring: MetHb level 1 hour after dose, CBC, urine color (should be blue-green)

7 Core Drug 6: Flumazenil

FLUMAZENIL - BENZODIAZEPINE ANTAGONIST (USE WITH CAUTION)

Drug class: Competitive benzodiazepine receptor antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitive antagonist at benzodiazepine binding site on GABA-A receptor
‚Ä¢ Reverses sedation, respiratory depression, amnesia from benzodiazepines
‚Ä¢ Does NOT reverse effects of other CNS depressants (alcohol, barbiturates)
‚Ä¢ Short duration (45-90 minutes) - shorter than most benzodiazepines
‚Ä¢ Pure antagonist - no intrinsic agonist or inverse agonist activity
‚Ä¢ Also reverses effects of non-benzodiazepine hypnotics (zolpidem, zopiclone)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Reversal of conscious sedation after procedures
‚Ä¢ Suspected pure benzodiazepine overdose (diagnostic and therapeutic)
‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepine-induced respiratory depression in ICU
‚Ä¢ Reversal of benzodiazepines given for seizures (caution!)
‚Ä¢ Coma of unknown cause (diagnostic trial)
‚Ä¢ Hepatic encephalopathy (controversial, some evidence)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Patients on benzodiazepines for control of seizures
‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepine-dependent patients (precipitates withdrawal)
‚Ä¢ Mixed overdose with pro-convulsant drugs (TCAs, cocaine, etc.)
‚Ä¢ Increased ICP (benzodiazepines may be protective)
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to flumazenil or benzodiazepines
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - limited data)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Reversal of sedation: 0.2 mg IV over 15 seconds, repeat every minute (max 1 mg)
‚Ä¢ Overdose reversal: 0.2 mg IV, then 0.3 mg at 1 min, then 0.5 mg at 1 min intervals (max 3 mg)
‚Ä¢ ICU sedation reversal: 0.2-1 mg IV, titrated to effect
‚Ä¢ Repeat doses: May need every 20-30 minutes (short duration)
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.1-0.4 mg/hr for long-acting benzodiazepines (rarely used)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Reversal of sedation: 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.2 mg) IV over 15 seconds
‚Ä¢ Repeat: 0.01 mg/kg every minute (max total 0.05 mg/kg or 1 mg)
‚Ä¢ Overdose: 0.01 mg/kg, repeat every minute to max 0.05 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Not recommended (limited data)
‚Ä¢ Children <1 year: Use with extreme caution
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (never IM, SC, or other routes)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 0.1 mg/mL (5 mL vial = 0.5 mg, 10 mL vial = 1 mg)
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give slowly (over 15-30 seconds per 0.2 mg)
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Can dilute with NS, D5W, or LR to 0.1 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with: Many drugs - administer separately
‚Ä¢ Stability: Use within 24 hours if diluted
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Anxiety, agitation, emotional lability
‚Ä¢ Dizziness, headache
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Sweating, flushing
‚Ä¢ Blurred vision
‚Ä¢ Pain at injection site
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Seizures (especially in mixed overdoses or epileptics)
‚Ä¢ Acute benzodiazepine withdrawal syndrome
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrhythmias (tachycardia, PVCs)
‚Ä¢ Hypertension
‚Ä¢ Re-sedation (when flumazenil wears off)
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (rare)
‚Ä¢ Death (in high-risk patients)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepines: Antagonizes effects (obvious)
‚Ä¢ TCAs, cocaine, theophylline: Increased seizure risk
‚Ä¢ Alcohol, barbiturates, opioids: No reversal - may unmask their effects
‚Ä¢ Naloxone: May be used together in mixed overdoses
‚Ä¢ Antiepileptics: May be less effective if benzodiazepines withdrawn
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "DO NOT ROUTINELY USE" in unknown overdose - seizure risk
‚Ä¢ Best uses: 1) Reversal of procedural sedation 2) Pure BZD overdose in young healthy patient
‚Ä¢ Duration mismatch: Flumazenil 45-90 min vs BZDs hours to days ‚Üí RE-SEDATION RISK
‚Ä¢ Observe: At least 2 hours after last dose for re-sedation
‚Ä¢ Seizure treatment: If seizures occur, give benzodiazepine (diazepam/midazolam)
‚Ä¢ Chronic users: May precipitate severe withdrawal (anxiety, tachycardia, seizures)
‚Ä¢ Mixed overdoses: Most dangerous scenario - flumazenil may unmask TCA cardiotoxicity
‚Ä¢ ICU use: May help with ventilator weaning in selected patients

8 Activated Charcoal (Injectable Relevance Overview)

ACTIVATED CHARCOAL - THE UNIVERSAL ADSORBENT

Drug class: Adsorbent, decontamination agent
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Large surface area (1000 m¬≤/g) ‚Üí binds many drugs/toxins via adsorption
‚Ä¢ Reduces absorption from gastrointestinal tract
‚Ä¢ May enhance elimination via "gastrointestinal dialysis" (multiple doses)
‚Ä¢ Does NOT bind all toxins (see contraindications)
‚Ä¢ Works best within 1 hour of ingestion (some benefit up to 4 hours)
‚Ä¢ Multiple-dose activated charcoal (MDAC) enhances elimination of some drugs
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Recent ingestion of adsorbable toxin (within 1-2 hours, up to 4 hours)
‚Ä¢ Multiple-dose activated charcoal for specific drugs:
  ‚Ä¢ Theophylline
  ‚Ä¢ Phenobarbital
  ‚Ä¢ Carbamazepine
  ‚Ä¢ Dapsone
  ‚Ä¢ Quinine
  ‚Ä¢ Salicylates (controversial)
‚Ä¢ Reduced absorption of orally administered drugs (in overdose)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Caustic ingestion (acids, alkalis) - obscures endoscopy
‚Ä¢ Hydrocarbon ingestion (aspiration risk)
‚Ä¢ Bowel obstruction/perforation
‚Ä¢ Decreased consciousness without protected airway
‚Ä¢ Toxins NOT adsorbed: Lithium, iron, heavy metals, alcohols, hydrocarbons
‚Ä¢ Planned whole bowel irrigation (WBI)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Single dose: 50-100 g (1 g/kg) PO/NG
‚Ä¢ Multiple-dose (MDAC): 50 g initially, then 25-50 g every 2-4 hours
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Mix with water (240-480 mL) to make slurry
‚Ä¢ Duration: Continue until clinical improvement or drug levels decline
‚Ä¢ Cathartic: May add sorbitol to first dose only (70% solution 1-2 mL/kg)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Single dose: 1 g/kg (max 50 g) PO/NG
‚Ä¢ Multiple-dose: 1 g/kg initially, then 0.5 g/kg every 2-4 hours
‚Ä¢ Dilution: Mix with water or juice to improve palatability
‚Ä¢ Sorbitol caution: Avoid in children <1 year, limit use due to electrolyte disturbances
‚Ä¢ Small children: May need NG tube administration
Administration notes: ‚Ä¢ Always check airway first - consider intubation if GCS <8
‚Ä¢ NG tube placement: Confirm position before administration
‚Ä¢ Palatability: Mix with chocolate milk, juice, or soda (not cola - caffeine)
‚Ä¢ Storage: Ready-to-use formulations available (less messy)
‚Ä¢ Black stools: Expected - warn patient/family
‚Ä¢ Aspiration risk: Can cause pneumonitis - protect airway
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Time window": Most benefit within 1 hour, some up to 4 hours
‚Ä¢ Delayed absorption drugs: May give beyond 4 hours (SR preparations, anticholinergics)
‚Ä¢ MDAC drugs: Remember "The Poor Cat Dies Quietly Soon": Theophylline, Phenobarbital, Carbamazepine, Dapsone, Quinine, Salicylates
‚Ä¢ Charcoal vs NAC: Give charcoal first if within time window, then NAC (separate by 2 hours if possible)
‚Ä¢ Whole bowel irrigation: Consider for iron, lithium, sustained-release preparations
‚Ä¢ Aspiration pneumonitis: Treat with supportive care, not steroids/antibiotics prophylactically
‚Ä¢ Controversies: Routine use declining - consider benefit/risk for each patient
‚Ä¢ Hospital administration: Often given in ED, MDAC may require ICU/HDU monitoring

9 Poisoning Management Protocol

‚ö†Ô∏è GENERAL POISONING MANAGEMENT (ABCDE TOXICOLOGY):

A - Airway with C-spine protection
‚Ä¢ Intubate early if GCS <8, loss of gag reflex, respiratory failure
‚Ä¢ Rapid sequence intubation with etomidate + succinylcholine/rocuronium

B - Breathing & Oxygenation
‚Ä¢ High-flow oxygen (except paraquat, bleomycin)
‚Ä¢ Ventilatory support if needed
‚Ä¢ Consider ABG/VBG

C - Circulation & Cardiac monitoring
‚Ä¢ IV access x2 (large bore)
‚Ä¢ ECG immediately (QT, QRS, arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Treat hypotension: Fluids ‚Üí vasopressors (norepinephrine usually first)

D - Disability & Decontamination
‚Ä¢ Glasgow Coma Scale, neurological exam
‚Ä¢ Check glucose (fingerstick) - treat if <4 mmol/L
‚Ä¢ Naloxone trial if opioid toxidrome
‚Ä¢ Thiamine if alcoholic/malnourished
‚Ä¢ DECONTAMINATION:
  ‚Ä¢ Dermal: Remove clothing, wash with soap and water
  ‚Ä¢ Ocular: Irrigate with normal saline (15-20 minutes minimum)
  ‚Ä¢ GI: Activated charcoal if indicated (within 1-4 hours)

E - Exposure & Examination
‚Ä¢ Full exposure, look for toxidromes, track marks, pills
‚Ä¢ Core temperature (hyper/hypothermia)
‚Ä¢ Consider specific antidotes (see grid)
‚Ä¢ Consult toxicology/poisons information centre

10 Toxicology Syndromes & Specific Treatments

10.1 High-Yield Poisoning Scenarios

‚ò†Ô∏è ORGANOPHOSPHATE POISONING:
‚Ä¢ Presentation: SLUDGE + BBB + muscle fasciculations/weakness
‚Ä¢ Treatment:
  1. DECONTAMINATION (remove clothes, wash skin)
  2. ATROPINE 2-5 mg IV, double every 5-10 min until secretions dry
  3. PRALIDOXIME 1-2 g IV over 30 min, then 500 mg/hr infusion
  4. Benzodiazepines for seizures
  5. ICU admission, ventilatory support often needed

üíä PARACETAMOL OVERDOSE:
‚Ä¢ Presentation: Often asymptomatic early, then RUQ pain, jaundice
‚Ä¢ Treatment:
  1. Activated charcoal if within 1-4 hours (separate from NAC)
  2. NAC protocol (IV or oral) if level above treatment line or >24 hours with risk
  3. Monitor LFTs, INR, creatinine
  4. Consider liver transplant if King's College criteria met

üåÄ TRICYCLIC ANTIDEPRESSANT OVERDOSE:
‚Ä¢ Presentation: Anticholinergic + cardiac (wide QRS, QT prolongation)
‚Ä¢ Treatment:
  1. Sodium bicarbonate for QRS >100 ms (1-2 mEq/kg bolus)
  2. Hypertonic saline if refractory (controversial)
  3. Lipid emulsion therapy for cardiac arrest refractory to standard ACLS
  4. AVOID flumazenil (seizure risk)
  5. AVOID class Ia/Ic antiarrhythmics (proarrhythmic)
