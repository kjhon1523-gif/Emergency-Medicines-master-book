0 Introduction: The Critical Minute

‚ö†Ô∏è TIME IS MYOCARDIUM: For every minute without CPR and defibrillation, survival decreases by 7-10%. Early drug administration is critical but must not delay high-quality CPR and defibrillation.
üß† Core Concept: Vasoactive drugs work by manipulating three key cardiovascular parameters:
1. Preload (volume/venous return)
2. Afterload (systemic vascular resistance)
3. Contractility (myocardial squeeze)
Understanding this helps predict each drug's effects.

1 ACLS Relevance & Cardiac Arrest Management

1.1 Universal Cardiac Arrest Algorithm

BASIC LIFE SUPPORT (BLS) FIRST:
1. Check responsiveness
2. Activate emergency response
3. Check breathing/pulse (‚â§10 seconds)
4. Begin CPR: 30:2 ratio, 100-120 compressions/min
5. Apply AED/defibrillator when available

ADVANCED LIFE SUPPORT (ACLS):
6. Continue high-quality CPR
7. Establish airway (advanced airway every 2 minutes)
8. Obtain IV/IO access
9. Identify rhythm: Shockable vs Non-shockable
10. Administer appropriate drugs
11. Treat reversible causes (Hs and Ts)

2 Shock Types: Pathophysiology & Management

Shock: A life-threatening condition of circulatory failure leading to inadequate tissue perfusion and oxygenation. Characterized by hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg or MAP <65 mmHg) with evidence of organ dysfunction.

2.1 Classification of Shock

1. HYPOVOLEMIC SHOCK

  • Pathophysiology: ‚Üì Preload ‚Üí ‚Üì Stroke volume ‚Üí ‚Üì Cardiac output
  • Causes: Hemorrhage, dehydration, burns, pancreatitis
  • Clinical Signs: Cool/clammy skin, tachycardia, ‚Üì urine output
  • Management: FLUIDS FIRST (crystalloids, blood), control bleeding
  • Drugs: Vasopressors only if fluids fail (norepinephrine)

2. CARDIOGENIC SHOCK

  • Pathophysiology: Pump failure ‚Üí ‚Üì Contractility ‚Üí ‚Üì Cardiac output
  • Causes: MI, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, valvular disorders
  • Clinical Signs: Pulmonary edema, jugular venous distension, cool extremities
  • Management: Inotropes, revascularization, mechanical support
  • Drugs: Dobutamine (first-line), dopamine, milrinone

3. DISTRIBUTIVE SHOCK

  • Pathophysiology: Vasodilation ‚Üí ‚Üì SVR ‚Üí ‚Üì Blood pressure
  • Causes: Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic shock, adrenal crisis
  • Clinical Signs: Warm/flushed skin, wide pulse pressure (early)
  • Management: Fluids + vasopressors, treat underlying cause
  • Drugs: Norepinephrine (first-line), vasopressin, adrenaline

4. OBSTRUCTIVE SHOCK

  • Pathophysiology: Mechanical obstruction ‚Üí ‚Üì Cardiac output
  • Causes: Tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, PE
  • Clinical Signs: Beck's triad (tamponade), unilateral breath sounds
  • Management: Treat obstruction (needle decompression, pericardiocentesis)
  • Drugs: None specific - treat cause

3 Core Drug 1: Adrenaline (Epinephrine)

ADRENALINE (EPINEPHRINE) - THE UNIVERSAL RESUSCITATION DRUG

Drug class: Non-selective alpha & beta adrenergic agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Alpha-1 (Œ±‚ÇÅ): Vasoconstriction ‚Üí ‚Üë SVR, ‚Üë BP, ‚Üì mucosal edema
‚Ä¢ Beta-1 (Œ≤‚ÇÅ): ‚Üë Heart rate, ‚Üë contractility, ‚Üë cardiac output
‚Ä¢ Beta-2 (Œ≤‚ÇÇ): Bronchodilation, vasodilation (skeletal muscle), glycogenolysis
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (VF/pVT, PEA, asystole) - ACLS
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis/severe allergic reactions
‚Ä¢ Symptomatic bradycardia (after atropine fails)
‚Ä¢ Severe hypotension/shock unresponsive to fluids
‚Ä¢ Severe asthma/bronchospasm refractory to bronchodilators
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia in heart transplant patients (denervated heart)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Absolute: None in cardiac arrest
‚Ä¢ Relative: Hypertension, CAD, arrhythmias, hyperthyroidism, pheochromocytoma
‚Ä¢ Caution with halogenated anesthetics (arrhythmia risk)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest: 1 mg IV/IO every 3-5 minutes (1:10,000 = 0.1 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis IM: 0.3-0.5 mg IM (1:1000 = 1 mg/mL) in anterolateral thigh
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis IV (refractory): 0.1 mg IV slowly (1:10,000) over 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Infusion: Start 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min, titrate to effect (max 1 mcg/kg/min)
‚Ä¢ Symptomatic bradycardia: 2-10 mcg/min infusion
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (max 1 mg) every 3-5 minutes
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis IM: 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.3 mg <30kg, 0.5 mg >30kg)
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.1-1 mcg/kg/min, titrate
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ IM: 1:1000 (1 mg/mL) - anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis)
‚Ä¢ IV bolus: 1:10,000 (0.1 mg/mL) - dilute if needed
‚Ä¢ Infusion: Mix 1 mg in 250 mL NS = 4 mcg/mL OR 6 mg in 100 mL = 60 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Endotracheal: 2-2.5 times IV dose diluted in 5-10 mL NS (use only if no IV/IO)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Anxiety, tremor, headache, pallor
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia, palpitations
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Dry mouth
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias (VF, VT)
‚Ä¢ Severe hypertension ‚Üí cerebral hemorrhage
‚Ä¢ Myocardial ischemia/infarction
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation
‚Ä¢ Hyperglycemia, hyperlactatemia
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Unopposed alpha effects ‚Üí severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ TCAs, MAOIs: Potentiate effects - hypertensive crisis
‚Ä¢ Halogenated anesthetics: Increased arrhythmia risk
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Increased arrhythmia risk
‚Ä¢ Ergot alkaloids: Extreme vasoconstriction
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis: IM FIRST, NOT IV (IV can cause fatal arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest: Give during CPR, NOT during pulse checks
‚Ä¢ Infusions: Use central line if possible (tissue necrosis risk)
‚Ä¢ Never mix with sodium bicarbonate (inactivates adrenaline)
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 2-3 minutes - frequent dosing needed in arrest
‚Ä¢ Color: Clear solution, turns pink/brown if oxidized (DISCARD)

4 Core Drug 2: Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine)

NORADRENALINE (NOREPINEPHRINE) - FIRST-LINE VASOPRESSOR

Drug class: Predominant alpha, some beta-1 adrenergic agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Alpha-1 (Œ±‚ÇÅ): Potent vasoconstriction ‚Üí ‚Üë SVR, ‚Üë BP
‚Ä¢ Alpha-2 (Œ±‚ÇÇ): Presynaptic inhibition of norepinephrine release
‚Ä¢ Beta-1 (Œ≤‚ÇÅ): Mild ‚Üë inotropy, chronotropy
‚Ä¢ Beta-2 (Œ≤‚ÇÇ): Minimal effect
Indications: ‚Ä¢ First-line for septic shock (after adequate fluid resuscitation)
‚Ä¢ Distributive shock (neurogenic, anaphylactic - after adrenaline)
‚Ä¢ Cardiogenic shock with vasodilation
‚Ä¢ Refractory hypotension
‚Ä¢ Perioperative hypotension

‚Ä¢ Mesenteric or peripheral vascular thrombosis
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use of MAOIs (within 14 days)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Severe peripheral vascular disease, hypertension

‚Ä¢ Titration: Increase by 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min every 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Usual range: 0.01-0.3 mcg/kg/min (up to 1 mcg/kg/min in extreme cases)
‚Ä¢ Bolus: NOT recommended (severe hypertension risk)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Starting dose: 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Titrate: Similar to adults, careful monitoring
‚Ä¢ Max: 1-2 mcg/kg/min (rarely needed)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Route: Central venous line STRONGLY recommended
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 4 mg in 250 mL D5W or NS = 16 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Alternative: 8 mg in 250 mL = 32 mcg/mL (for fluid restriction)
‚Ä¢ Must use infusion pump with pressure bag if peripheral
‚Ä¢ Use in-line filter (0.22 micron) recommended
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Headache, anxiety
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia (reflex response to hypertension)
‚Ä¢ Reduced urine output at high doses
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis/ischemia with extravasation
‚Ä¢ Arrhythmias (less than adrenaline)
‚Ä¢ Mesenteric ischemia
‚Ä¢ Digital ischemia/gangrene
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment at high doses
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ MAOIs: Hypertensive crisis
‚Ä¢ TCAs: Potentiate pressor effect
‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Unopposed alpha ‚Üí severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ Ergot alkaloids: Extreme vasoconstriction
‚Ä¢ Guamethidine: Potentiates effect

‚Ä¢ If extravasation occurs: Stop infusion, aspirate residual, infiltrate phentolamine (5-10 mg in 10 mL saline)
‚Ä¢ Monitor: BP every 2-5 minutes during titration
‚Ä¢ In septic shock: Target MAP ‚â•65 mmHg
‚Ä¢ Weaning: Decrease by 0.05 mcg/kg/min every 30-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Incompatible with alkaline solutions (bicarbonate)
üß† NORADRENALINE vs ADRENALINE:
‚Ä¢ Noradrenaline: Strong alpha, weak beta ‚Üí ‚Üë BP via vasoconstriction
‚Ä¢ Adrenaline: Strong alpha & beta ‚Üí ‚Üë BP via vasoconstriction AND ‚Üë cardiac output

5 Core Drug 3: Dopamine

Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Dose-dependent effects:

‚Ä¢ Inotropic dose: 3-10 mcg/kg/min

6 Core Drug 4: Dobutamine

‚Ä¢ Titration: Increase by 1-2 mcg/kg/min every 10-30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Usual range: 2-20 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Max: 40 mcg/kg/min (rarely effective above 20)
‚Ä¢ No loading dose required

‚Ä¢ Range: 2-20 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Same titration as adults

‚Ä¢ Preparation: 250 mg in 250 mL D5W or NS = 1000 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Alternative: 500 mg in 250 mL = 2000 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Protect from light
‚Ä¢ Stable for 24 hours at room temperature, 48 hours refrigerated
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Tachycardia, palpitations
‚Ä¢ Headache
‚Ä¢ Nausea
‚Ä¢ Mild hypotension (due to vasodilation)
‚Ä¢ Increased myocardial oxygen demand

‚Ä¢ Myocardial ischemia (increases O‚ÇÇ demand)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (if vasodilation predominates)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia (beta-2 effect)
‚Ä¢ Tachyphylaxis (develops within 72 hours)

‚Ä¢ MAOIs: Hypertensive crisis possible
‚Ä¢ Insulin: May require adjustment (affects glucose)
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Increased arrhythmia risk

7 Core Drug 5: Atropine

ATROPINE - ANTICHOLINERGIC FOR BRADYCARDIA

Drug class: Anticholinergic, competitive muscarinic antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Blocks acetylcholine at muscarinic receptors
‚Ä¢ Cardiac: Blocks vagal tone ‚Üí ‚Üë heart rate (positive chronotropy)
‚Ä¢ Other: Dries secretions, relaxes smooth muscle, dilates pupils
‚Ä¢ Has CNS effects at high doses (can cross blood-brain barrier)

‚Ä¢ Organophosphate/nerve agent poisoning
‚Ä¢ Premedication to reduce secretions (anesthesia)
‚Ä¢ Asystole/pulseless electrical activity (PEA) with bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Reversal of muscarinic side effects of other drugs

‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (can worsen weakness)
‚Ä¢ Obstructive uropathy (BPH)
‚Ä¢ Obstructive GI disorders
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia (may worsen)

‚Ä¢ Asystole/PEA: 1 mg IV (single dose, repeat once if needed)
‚Ä¢ Organophosphate poisoning: 2-5 mg IV, then 1-2 mg every 5-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ Premedication: 0.4-0.6 mg IM 30-60 minutes pre-op

‚Ä¢ Repeat: May repeat once in 5 minutes
‚Ä¢ Min dose: 0.1 mg (to avoid paradoxical bradycardia)
‚Ä¢ Organophosphate: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg, then 0.02-0.05 mg/kg every 10-30 minutes
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV, IO, IM, endotracheal, subcutaneous
‚Ä¢ Preparation: Available as 0.1 mg/mL, 0.5 mg/mL, 1 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give rapidly (over 1 minute)
‚Ä¢ For ET: Use 2-2.5 times IV dose diluted in 5-10 mL NS
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: Most IV solutions, avoid alkaline solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Dry mouth, dry skin
‚Ä¢ Blurred vision, photophobia
‚Ä¢ Urinary retention
‚Ä¢ Constipation
‚Ä¢ Flushing, fever (especially children)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Tachycardia ‚Üí myocardial ischemia
‚Ä¢ Hypertension
‚Ä¢ CNS effects: restlessness, confusion, hallucinations (high dose)
‚Ä¢ Paralytic ileus
‚Ä¢ Hyperthermia (reduced sweating)
‚Ä¢ Paradoxical bradycardia with low doses (<0.5 mg)

‚Ä¢ Amantadine, antihistamines, TCAs: Additive anticholinergic effects
‚Ä¢ Ketamine: Increased secretions (atropine sometimes given with)
‚Ä¢ Metoclopramide: Antagonizes GI effects

‚Ä¢ Onset: 1-2 minutes, peak: 2-4 minutes, duration: 2-6 hours
‚Ä¢ Not effective for beta-blocker/calcium channel blocker overdose
‚Ä¢ In organophosphate poisoning: "Secretions, secretions, secretions" - keep giving until secretions dry
‚Ä¢ Red man syndrome: Flushing, especially in children (not allergic)
‚Ä¢ Antidote for overdose: Physostigmine (rarely used)

8 Core Drug 6: Amiodarone

AMIODARONE - BROAD-SPECTRUM ANTIARRHYTHMIC

Drug class: Class III antiarrhythmic (Vaughan Williams), with I, II, IV properties
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Class III: Blocks potassium channels ‚Üí ‚Üë action potential duration, ‚Üë refractory period

‚Ä¢ Atrial fibrillation/flutter with rapid ventricular response
‚Ä¢ Ventricular tachycardia (stable and unstable)
‚Ä¢ Recurrent ventricular arrhythmias prophylaxis

‚Ä¢ Cardiogenic shock (except for arrhythmia treatment)
‚Ä¢ Severe hypotension
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to iodine/amiodarone
‚Ä¢ Thyroid dysfunction (relative)
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary fibrosis (relative)

‚Ä¢ Oral loading: 800-1600 mg/day √ó 1-3 weeks, then 400 mg/day

‚Ä¢ Perfusing VT: 5 mg/kg over 20-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 5-15 mcg/kg/min (consult specialist)
‚Ä¢ Use with extreme caution - not well studied in children

‚Ä¢ Preparation: Comes as 150 mg/3 mL ampoule (50 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Dilute in D5W only (NOT saline - precipitates)
‚Ä¢ Use in-line filter (1.2 micron) recommended

‚Ä¢ Bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (especially with rapid infusion)
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Photosensitivity, skin discoloration (blue-gray)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Pulmonary fibrosis (dose and duration dependent)
‚Ä¢ Thyroid dysfunction (hypo or hyper)
‚Ä¢ Hepatotoxicity (elevated LFTs)
‚Ä¢ Corneal microdeposits (vision changes)

‚Ä¢ Torsades de pointes (less than other class III drugs)

‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Increases levels (reduce digoxin dose by 50%)

‚Ä¢ Cyclosporine: Increases levels

‚Ä¢ Contains iodine - can interfere with thyroid tests

9 Core Drug 7: Lidocaine (Cardiac Use)

LIDOCAINE - CLASS IB ANTIARRHYTHMIC

Drug class: Class IB antiarrhythmic, local anesthetic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Blocks fast sodium channels (use-dependent)
‚Ä¢ Suppresses automaticity in Purkinje fibers
‚Ä¢ Shortens action potential duration in ventricles
‚Ä¢ Raises ventricular fibrillation threshold
‚Ä¢ Minimal effect on conduction velocity at normal doses
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Alternative to amiodarone for shock-refractory VF/pVT
‚Ä¢ Stable monomorphic VT (especially with normal LV function)
‚Ä¢ VT prophylaxis after MI (historical use)
‚Ä¢ Digitalis-induced ventricular arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Local anesthesia (different formulation/use)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Severe heart block (without pacemaker)
‚Ä¢ Adams-Stokes syndrome
‚Ä¢ Severe sinoatrial node dysfunction
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to amide local anesthetics
‚Ä¢ Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome (relative)
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (VF/pVT): 1-1.5 mg/kg IV push, repeat 0.5-0.75 mg/kg in 5-10 min (max 3 mg/kg total)
‚Ä¢ Perfusing VT loading: 1-1.5 mg/kg IV, then 0.5-0.75 mg/kg every 5-10 min (max 3 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance infusion: 1-4 mg/min (typically 2 mg/min)
‚Ä¢ ET tube: 2-2.5 times IV dose (2-4 mg/kg) diluted in 5-10 mL NS
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest: 1 mg/kg IV/IO (max 100 mg)
‚Ä¢ Bolus: 1 mg/kg, may repeat in 10-15 minutes
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 20-50 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Use with caution - not first-line in children
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV, IO, endotracheal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: Available as 1% (10 mg/mL), 2% (20 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Mix 1 g in 250 mL D5W = 4 mg/mL OR 2 g in 250 mL = 8 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ Cardiac formulation: Preservative-free preferred for IV
‚Ä¢ Compatible with most IV solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Drowsiness, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Paresthesias, numbness
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Lightheadedness
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ CNS toxicity: confusion ‚Üí seizures ‚Üí coma
‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular toxicity: hypotension, bradycardia, asystole
‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression/arrest
‚Ä¢ Allergic reactions (rare)
‚Ä¢ Torsades de pointes (rare)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Increased lidocaine levels (reduced hepatic blood flow)
‚Ä¢ Cimetidine: Increases levels (inhibits metabolism)
‚Ä¢ Phenytoin: Additive cardiac depression
‚Ä¢ Other local anesthetics: Additive toxicity
‚Ä¢ Succinylcholine: Prolongs paralysis
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ Toxicity signs: "Lidocaine Lethargy" ‚Üí Lip tingling ‚Üí Lightheadedness ‚Üí Loss of consciousness ‚Üí Seizures
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 1.5-2 hours (prolonged with heart failure, liver disease)
‚Ä¢ Metabolite MEGX is cardioactive and toxic
‚Ä¢ Reduce dose in heart failure, hepatic disease, elderly
‚Ä¢ Not effective for atrial arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Monitor: ECG, neurological status, plasma levels if prolonged use (>3 mg/min)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic level: 1.5-5 mcg/mL, toxic >6 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Antidote for toxicity: Stop infusion, supportive care, lipids if severe
üß† AMIODARONE vs LIDOCAINE in CARDIAC ARREST:
‚Ä¢ Amiodarone: Preferred for recurrent VF/pVT, better evidence for survival to hospital admission
‚Ä¢ Lidocaine: Alternative if amiodarone unavailable, may be better for ischemia-related VF
‚Ä¢ Both: Consider after 2-3 shocks, during CPR, while preparing for next shock

10 Core Drug 8: Magnesium Sulfate

MAGNESIUM SULFATE - ELECTROLYTE WITH ANTIARRHYTHMIC PROPERTIES

Drug class: Electrolyte replacement, natural calcium channel blocker
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Natural calcium antagonist ‚Üí vasodilation, bronchodilation
‚Ä¢ Membrane stabilizer ‚Üí prolongs conduction, raises fibrillation threshold
‚Ä¢ Cofactor for Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase (helps correct potassium)
‚Ä¢ Inhibits acetylcholine release at neuromuscular junction
‚Ä¢ Smooth muscle relaxant (uterus, bronchial, vascular)

‚Ä¢ Polymorphic VT (with normal QT)
‚Ä¢ Severe asthma exacerbations (adjunct)
‚Ä¢ Eclampsia/severe preeclampsia
‚Ä¢ Hypomagnesemia
‚Ä¢ Digoxin toxicity (for arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Refractory VF/VT (consider)

‚Ä¢ Heart block (without pacemaker)
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min)
‚Ä¢ Hypermagnesemia
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use of calcium channel blockers (relative)

‚Ä¢ Severe asthma: 2 g IV over 20 minutes
‚Ä¢ Eclampsia: 4-6 g IV load over 20 min, then 1-2 g/hr infusion

‚Ä¢ Asthma: 25-50 mg/kg IV (max 2 g) over 20 minutes

‚Ä¢ Use with caution - limited evidence

‚Ä¢ Preparation: Available as 1 g/2 mL (50%), 2 g/10 mL (20%), 4 g/8 mL (50%)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Dilute to ‚â§20% concentration (200 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ IV push for arrest: Give undiluted or diluted in 10 mL D5W/NS
‚Ä¢ IM: Use 50% solution, deep injection, massage site
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Flushing, warmth sensation
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Muscle weakness
‚Ä¢ Sedation
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Hypotension (vasodilation)
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia, heart block
‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression/arrest
‚Ä¢ Loss of deep tendon reflexes (>4-5 mEq/L)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (>10-15 mEq/L)
‚Ä¢ Calcium antagonist effect (reversed with calcium)

‚Ä¢ Digoxin: May worsen heart block
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blockers: Additive effects

‚Ä¢ Calcium: Antagonizes effects

‚Ä¢ Onset: Immediate for arrhythmias, 30-60 min for asthma
‚Ä¢ Renal excretion: Reduce dose/frequency in renal impairment

‚Ä¢ Check magnesium level 2 hours after loading dose

11 Core Drug 9: Phenylephrine

PHENYLEPHRINE - PURE ALPHA AGONIST

Drug class: Selective alpha-1 adrenergic agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Pure alpha-1 agonist ‚Üí vasoconstriction
‚Ä¢ No beta activity ‚Üí no direct cardiac stimulation
‚Ä¢ Increases SVR and BP via peripheral vasoconstriction
‚Ä¢ Reflex bradycardia often occurs (baroreceptor response)
‚Ä¢ Reduces mucosal edema (nasal decongestant effect)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Drug-induced hypotension (especially anesthesia-related)
‚Ä¢ Spinal/neurogenic shock
‚Ä¢ Supraventricular tachycardia (to increase vagal tone via reflex)
‚Ä¢ Nasal decongestion (topical)
‚Ä¢ Pupillary dilation (ophthalmic)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension in aortic stenosis (preserves coronary perfusion)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypovolemia (correct first)
‚Ä¢ Severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ Ventricular tachycardia
‚Ä¢ Severe peripheral vascular disease
‚Ä¢ Narrow-angle glaucoma (for ophthalmic use)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent MAOI use
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ IV bolus: 40-100 mcg every 1-2 minutes as needed
‚Ä¢ Infusion: Start 0.5-1 mcg/kg/min, titrate to effect (usual 0.5-6 mcg/kg/min)
‚Ä¢ For SVT: 100-250 mcg rapid IV push (causes reflex bradycardia)
‚Ä¢ Max single dose: 200 mcg, max infusion: 10 mcg/kg/min
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ IV bolus: 1-2 mcg/kg every 10-15 minutes
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.1-0.5 mcg/kg/min, titrate
‚Ä¢ Use with caution - limited pediatric data
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV, IM, subcutaneous, ophthalmic, intranasal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: Comes as 10 mg/mL (1%)
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Mix 10 mg in 250 mL NS = 40 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ For bolus: Dilute 1 mg (0.1 mL of 10 mg/mL) in 9.9 mL NS = 100 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Compatible with most IV solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Reflex bradycardia
‚Ä¢ Headache
‚Ä¢ Anxiety, restlessness
‚Ä¢ Reduced urine output (renal vasoconstriction)
‚Ä¢ Extravasation injury risk
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ Myocardial ischemia (increased afterload)
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation
‚Ä¢ Arrhythmias (less than other pressors)
‚Ä¢ Mesenteric/renal ischemia
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary hypertension
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ MAOIs: Hypertensive crisis
‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Severe hypertension, reflex bradycardia exaggerated
‚Ä¢ TCAs: Potentiates effect
‚Ä¢ Oxytocics: Severe hypertension
‚Ä¢ Ergot alkaloids: Extreme vasoconstriction
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Pharmacologic tourniquet" - pure vasoconstrictor
‚Ä¢ Causes reflex bradycardia - can be used to terminate SVT
‚Ä¢ Good for spinal shock (preserves spinal perfusion pressure)
‚Ä¢ Monitor urine output - can cause renal impairment
‚Ä¢ Short half-life (~5 minutes) - easy titration
‚Ä¢ Extravasation treatment: Phentolamine infiltration
‚Ä¢ Less arrhythmogenic than other pressors (no beta effect)
‚Ä¢ Not first-line for septic shock (lacks cardiac support)

12 Core Drug 10: Vasopressin (Overview)

VASOPRESSIN - NON-ADRENERGIC VASOPRESSOR

Drug class: Endogenous hormone (ADH), V‚ÇÅ receptor agonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ V‚ÇÅ receptors: Vasoconstriction (splanchnic, cutaneous, coronary)
‚Ä¢ V‚ÇÇ receptors: Water reabsorption in kidneys (antidiuretic effect)

‚Ä¢ Hyponatremia (relative)

‚Ä¢ Abdominal cramping
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Bronchoconstriction
‚Ä¢ Increased bowel sounds

‚Ä¢ Hyponatremia (SIADH-like effect)
‚Ä¢ Peripheral/splanchnic ischemia
‚Ä¢ Thrombocytopenia
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (with rapid IV push)

‚Ä¢ Lithium, demeclocycline: Antagonize antidiuretic effect

‚Ä¢ Fixed dose - not weight-based (40 units for all adults)
‚Ä¢ Works in acidotic states where catecholamines fail
‚Ä¢ Monitor sodium - causes water retention
‚Ä¢ For septic shock: Add when norepinephrine >0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 10-35 minutes (shorter than catecholamines)

Appendix: Quick Reference Guide

Shock Drug Selection Algorithm

STEP 1: Assess volume status ‚Üí Give fluids if hypovolemic
STEP 2: Identify shock type:
  ‚Ä¢ Distributive (warm, vasodilated): Norepinephrine FIRST
  ‚Ä¢ Cardiogenic (cold, wet, low CO): Dobutamine FIRST (if BP okay)
  ‚Ä¢ Cardiogenic + Low BP: Dopamine or Dobutamine + Norepinephrine
  ‚Ä¢ Hypovolemic (cold, dry): FLUIDS ‚Üí Norepinephrine if refractory
  ‚Ä¢ Obstructive: Treat cause (needle decompression, etc.)
STEP 3: Add second agent if refractory:
  ‚Ä¢ Norepinephrine + Vasopressin (for distributive)
  ‚Ä¢ Dobutamine + Norepinephrine (for cardiogenic with low BP)
  ‚Ä¢ Consider adrenaline if severely hypotensive

Monitoring Parameters for Vasoactive Drugs

  • Continuous: ECG, BP (arterial line preferred), pulse oximetry
  • Every 5-15 minutes during titration: BP, HR, perfusion
  • Every hour: Urine output (target >0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Every 4-6 hours: Lactate, ABG if indicated
  • Daily: ECG, electrolytes, renal function
  • For specific drugs:
    • Dobutamine: Cardiac output if available
    • Amiodarone: LFTs, TFTs, CXR periodically
    • Vasopressin: Sodium daily
    • Magnesium: Reflexes during infusion, Mg level
