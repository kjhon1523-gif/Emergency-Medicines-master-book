0 Introduction: The Body's Chemistry Set

‚ö†Ô∏è METABOLIC EMERGENCES KILL QUIETLY: A potassium of 7.0 mEq/L can cause cardiac arrest without warning. A pH of 6.9 can stop enzyme function. A glucose of 1.5 mmol/L can cause irreversible brain damage. Monitor, interpret, and act.
üß† Core Concept: Metabolic emergencies involve imbalances in:
1. Energy (glucose - too high or too low)
2. Electrical stability (potassium, calcium, magnesium)
3. Acid-base balance (pH, bicarbonate)
Each requires specific correction strategies to avoid complications.

1 Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) & Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

DKA: Hyperglycemia (blood glucose >13.9 mmol/L or 250 mg/dL), metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15 mEq/L), ketonemia/ketonuria. Caused by absolute or relative insulin deficiency.

HHS: Extreme hyperglycemia (blood glucose >33.3 mmol/L or 600 mg/dL), hyperosmolality (>320 mOsm/kg), absence of significant ketosis. Usually slower onset than DKA.

1.1 DKA vs HHS Comparison

Parameter Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)
Typical patient Type 1 DM, younger Type 2 DM, elderly
Onset Hours to 1-2 days Days to weeks
Blood glucose >13.9 mmol/L (250 mg/dL) >33.3 mmol/L (600 mg/dL), often >55.5 mmol/L (1000 mg/dL)
pH <7.3 (often <7.0 in severe cases) >7.3 (usually normal or mild acidosis)
Bicarbonate <15 mEq/L >15 mEq/L
Ketones Large in urine and serum Small or absent
Osmolality Variable, usually <320 mOsm/kg >320 mOsm/kg (often >350)
Mental status Alert to comatose More likely comatose (25-50%)
Mortality 1-5% 10-20% (higher due to age/comorbidities)

1.2 DKA Management Protocol

üíâ DKA MANAGEMENT PROTOCOL (ABCDEFG):

A - Assessment & Access:
‚Ä¢ IV access x2 (large bore)
‚Ä¢ Bloods: Glucose, electrolytes, VBG/ABG, ketones, renal function
‚Ä¢ ECG (check for hyperkalemia changes)
‚Ä¢ Urine dipstick (glucose, ketones)

B - Begin Fluids:
‚Ä¢ 1-2 L NS over 1-2 hours (15-20 mL/kg)
‚Ä¢ Then switch to 0.45% NaCl or NS based on sodium
‚Ä¢ Maintain urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr

C - Continuous Insulin:
‚Ä¢ Bolus: 0.1 units/kg IV
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hr (mix 50 units in 50 mL NS = 1 unit/mL)
‚Ä¢ Target glucose drop: 2.8-4.2 mmol/L/hr (50-75 mg/dL/hr)

D - Dextrose when needed:
‚Ä¢ Add D5W when glucose <14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL)
‚Ä¢ Continue insulin until anion gap closed, ketones cleared

E - Electrolytes (especially K‚Å∫):
‚Ä¢ K‚Å∫ often high initially but total body K‚Å∫ depleted
‚Ä¢ Add K‚Å∫ to fluids when: K‚Å∫ <5.5 mEq/L and urine output adequate
‚Ä¢ Usually 20-40 mEq K‚Å∫/L at 10-20 mEq/hr

F - Find & Fix precipitant:
‚Ä¢ Infection (chest, urine, skin)
‚Ä¢ Myocardial infarction
‚Ä¢ Non-compliance with insulin
‚Ä¢ New-onset diabetes

G - Glucose monitoring:
‚Ä¢ Hourly glucose checks
‚Ä¢ Monitor electrolytes every 2-4 hours
‚Ä¢ Transition to SC insulin when eating, gap closed

2 Core Drug 1: Dextrose (10%, 25%, 50%)

DEXTROSE - RAPID CORRECTION OF HYPOGLYCEMIA

Drug class: Carbohydrate, monosaccharide
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Provides immediate source of glucose for cellular metabolism
‚Ä¢ Reverses neuroglycopenia (brain glucose deprivation)
‚Ä¢ Stimulates endogenous insulin secretion (in non-diabetics)
‚Ä¢ Osmotic agent - draws water into vascular space
‚Ä¢ 50% dextrose is hypertonic (2525 mOsm/L) - significant fluid shift
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Hypoglycemia (blood glucose <4.0 mmol/L or 72 mg/dL)
‚Ä¢ Symptomatic hypoglycemia regardless of reading
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia (adjunct with insulin - drives K‚Å∫ into cells)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol intoxication (provides glucose for metabolism)
‚Ä¢ Insulin overdose treatment
‚Ä¢ Pre-hospital management of altered mental status (if hypoglycemia suspected)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Intracranial or intraspinal hemorrhage (relative - hypertonic solution)
‚Ä¢ Diabetic ketoacidosis or HHS (unless hypoglycemic)
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to corn products (some preparations)
‚Ä¢ Anuria, oliguria (relative - fluid overload risk)
‚Ä¢ Delirium tremens (may worsen - give thiamine first)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Mild hypoglycemia (alert): 15-20g PO (4 glucose tablets, 120-180 mL juice)
‚Ä¢ Severe hypoglycemia (altered): 25g IV (50 mL of 50% or 250 mL of 10%)
‚Ä¢ Refractory hypoglycemia: Repeat 25g IV, then start 10% dextrose infusion
‚Ä¢ Infusion: D5W at 100 mL/hr (5g glucose/hour) or D10W at 50-100 mL/hr
‚Ä¢ For hyperkalemia: 25g dextrose (50 mL 50%) with 10 units regular insulin IV
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonates: 2 mL/kg of 10% dextrose (200 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Infants/children: 0.5-1 g/kg (2-4 mL/kg of 25% OR 5-10 mL/kg of 10%)
‚Ä¢ Adolescents: 25g IV (as adult)
‚Ä¢ Infusion: D10W at maintenance rate (varies by weight)
‚Ä¢ MAX concentration for peripheral IV: 12.5% (higher risks phlebitis)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (emergencies), PO (if alert), buccal gel
‚Ä¢ Concentrations: 5% (D5W), 10% (D10W), 25%, 50%
‚Ä¢ For IV push: Use 50% dextrose (25g/50mL) - give over 1-3 minutes
‚Ä¢ Peripheral IV caution: 50% dextrose is VERY hypertonic - ensure IV patency
‚Ä¢ Extravasation: Causes tissue necrosis - use large vein, check placement
‚Ä¢ Buccal gel: 15g/30g tubes (GlucoGel¬Æ, DextroGel¬Æ) - rub on gums
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Hyperglycemia (rebound)
‚Ä¢ Fluid overload (especially with large volumes)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia (insulin-mediated shift)
‚Ä¢ Local irritation/phlebitis
‚Ä¢ Pain at injection site (hypertonic solution)
‚Ä¢ Burning with buccal administration
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Wernicke's encephalopathy (in thiamine-deficient patients)
‚Ä¢ Cerebral edema (especially in children with rapid correction)
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation (50% dextrose)
‚Ä¢ Hyperosmolar coma (if given to hyperglycemic patient)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia-induced arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (fluid overload in cardiac patients)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Insulin: Antagonizes effect (obvious)
‚Ä¢ Blood products: Causes hemolysis if given through same line
‚Ä¢ Phenytoin: Incompatible - precipitates
‚Ä¢ Many antibiotics: Check compatibility before Y-site administration
‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Variable effect on INR
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "THIAMINE FIRST" in alcoholics/malnourished before dextrose (prevents Wernicke's)
‚Ä¢ Check glucose 15 minutes after treatment - rebound hypoglycemia common
‚Ä¢ Oral vs IV: 15g PO raises glucose by ~2.2 mmol/L (40 mg/dL) in 20 minutes
‚Ä¢ 50% dextrose: Raises glucose by ~4.2 mmol/L (75 mg/dL) per 25g in 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Children: Use 10% or 25% (not 50%) to reduce cerebral edema risk
‚Ä¢ Sulfonylurea overdose: May need 10% dextrose infusion for 24-48 hours
‚Ä¢ Glucagon alternative: 1mg IM if no IV access (works in 10-15 minutes)
‚Ä¢ Storage: 50% dextrose crystallizes when cold - warm before use if crystals visible
üç¨ HYPOGLYCEMIA MANAGEMENT:
‚Ä¢ Mild (alert): 15-20g fast-acting carbs ‚Üí Check in 15 min ‚Üí Repeat if <4.0 mmol/L ‚Üí Follow with complex carb
‚Ä¢ Severe (altered): IV dextrose 25g OR Glucagon 1mg IM ‚Üí Check in 15 min ‚Üí Start 10% dextrose infusion if recurrent
‚Ä¢ Refractory: Consider hydrocortisone 100mg IV, octreotide 50mcg SC (for sulfonylurea overdose)

3 Core Drug 2: Insulin (Regular - IV Use)

INSULIN (REGULAR) - FOR DKA, HYPERKALEMIA, AND TIGHT CONTROL

Drug class: Pancreatic hormone, short-acting insulin
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds insulin receptors ‚Üí facilitates glucose uptake into cells (muscle, fat)
‚Ä¢ Stimulates glycogen synthesis, inhibits glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis
‚Ä¢ Promotes potassium entry into cells (activates Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase)
‚Ä¢ Inhibits lipolysis ‚Üí reduces ketone production in DKA
‚Ä¢ IV regular insulin onset 10-30 minutes, peak 30-60 minutes, duration 4-6 hours
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
‚Ä¢ Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS)
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia (with dextrose unless hyperglycemic)
‚Ä¢ Perioperative glycemic control
‚Ä¢ Critical illness hyperglycemia
‚Ä¢ Insulin tolerance test (diagnostic)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypoglycemia
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia (relative - correct K‚Å∫ >3.3 mEq/L first in DKA)
‚Ä¢ Allergy to insulin (rare) or preservatives
‚Ä¢ During MRI (some insulin pens contain metal)
‚Ä¢ No absolute contraindications in DKA/HHS (life-saving)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ DKA/HHS bolus: 0.1 units/kg IV (optional - many protocols skip bolus)
‚Ä¢ DKA/HHS infusion: 0.1 units/kg/hr (mix 50 units in 50 mL NS = 1 unit/mL)
‚Ä¢ Titration: Increase by 0.05 units/kg/hr if glucose not falling by 2.8-4.2 mmol/L/hr
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 10 units regular insulin IV with 25g dextrose (50 mL 50%)
‚Ä¢ Critical care infusion: 0.5-5 units/hr, titrate to glucose 6-10 mmol/L (110-180 mg/dL)
‚Ä¢ Transition to SC: Give first SC dose 1-2 hours before stopping infusion
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ DKA: 0.1 units/kg IV bolus, then 0.1 units/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 0.1 units/kg with 0.5-1 g/kg dextrose
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Start 0.02-0.1 units/kg/hr, careful monitoring
‚Ä¢ Children <5 years: Consider lower starting dose (0.05 units/kg/hr)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV infusion (preferred for emergencies), IV bolus, SC, IM
‚Ä¢ Preparation for infusion: 50 units regular insulin in 50 mL NS = 1 unit/mL
‚Ä¢ Alternative: 100 units in 100 mL NS = 1 unit/mL
‚Ä¢ Must use dedicated line (adsorbs to plastic tubing - flush 50 mL through first)
‚Ä¢ Pump required - never gravity drip
‚Ä¢ Adsorption loss: Up to 80% loss to tubing without priming - use albumin-containing solutions or flush tubing
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Hypoglycemia (most common)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia (insulin drives K‚Å∫ into cells)
‚Ä¢ Hypomagnesemia, hypophosphatemia
‚Ä¢ Lipohypertrophy at injection sites (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Weight gain (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Local allergic reactions (rare with human insulin)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe hypoglycemia ‚Üí seizures, coma, death
‚Ä¢ Cerebral edema (especially in children with DKA)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (extremely rare with human insulin)
‚Ä¢ Insulin antibodies with animal insulin (rare now)
‚Ä¢ Diabetic retinopathy progression (with rapid glucose lowering)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia-induced arrhythmias
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Beta-blockers: Mask hypoglycemia symptoms, impair recovery
‚Ä¢ Corticosteroids, thiazides, niacin: Increase insulin requirements
‚Ä¢ Alcohol, ACE inhibitors, MAOIs: Increase hypoglycemia risk
‚Ä¢ Sympathomimetics (adrenaline): Increase insulin requirements
‚Ä¢ Octreotide: Alters insulin requirements
‚Ä¢ Many herbal supplements: Affect glucose control
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ DKA: Continue insulin until anion gap closed (not until glucose normal)
‚Ä¢ Glucose drop target: 2.8-4.2 mmol/L/hr (50-75 mg/dL/hr) - faster causes cerebral edema
‚Ä¢ Potassium: Check K‚Å∫ every 2-4 hours, add to fluids when K‚Å∫ <5.5 mEq/L
‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia dose: Works in 15-30 minutes, lasts 4-6 hours
‚Ä¢ Adsorption solution: Add 1 mL of patient's blood to bag OR use 0.1% albumin in NS
‚Ä¢ Half-life: IV: 4-6 minutes (very short) - why continuous infusion needed
‚Ä¢ SC overlap: Give first SC dose 1-2 hours before stopping IV to avoid rebound
‚Ä¢ Monitoring: Hourly glucose, 2-4 hour electrolytes, VBG every 4 hours in DKA

4 Core Drug 3: Calcium Gluconate

CALCIUM GLUCONATE - FIRST-LINE FOR HYPERKALEMIA

Drug class: Electrolyte supplement, cation
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Raises extracellular calcium ‚Üí increases myocardial action potential threshold
‚Ä¢ Stabilizes cardiac membrane ‚Üí protects against hyperkalemia-induced arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Does NOT lower serum potassium (only protects heart)
‚Ä¢ Provides calcium for neuromuscular function, coagulation, bone
‚Ä¢ Gluconate salt contains 9% elemental calcium (vs 27% in chloride)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia with ECG changes (first-line treatment)
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia (symptomatic or ionized calcium <0.8 mmol/L)
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blocker overdose
‚Ä¢ Magnesium toxicity (antagonizes neuromuscular effects)
‚Ä¢ Hydrofluoric acid burns (chelates fluoride ion)
‚Ä¢ Citrate toxicity (massive blood transfusion)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypercalcemia
‚Ä¢ Digoxin toxicity (relative - may worsen arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Ventricular fibrillation (if from hyperkalemia, treat hyperkalemia first)
‚Ä¢ Renal calculi (relative)
‚Ä¢ Sarcoidosis (relative - may cause hypercalcemia)
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 10 mL of 10% calcium gluconate (1g) IV over 5-10 minutes
‚Ä¢ Repeat: May repeat in 5-10 minutes if ECG changes persist
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia: 1-2g IV over 10-20 minutes, then infusion if needed
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 2g in 1L D5W/NS at 0.5-1.5 mL/kg/hr (0.5-1.5 mg/kg/hr elemental Ca)
‚Ä¢ Hydrofluoric acid burns: 10% gel topically OR intra-arterial infusion
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 0.5-1 mL/kg of 10% calcium gluconate IV over 5-10 min (max 10 mL)
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia: 200-500 mg/kg/day IV divided Q6-8H
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 200 mg/kg IV over 10-30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 200-400 mg/kg/day IV/PO divided Q6-8H
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), intra-arterial (for HF burns), topical, PO
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10% solution = 100 mg/mL (10 mg/mL elemental calcium)
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give slowly (max 1-2 mL/min or 100-200 mg/min)
‚Ä¢ Peripheral IV acceptable (less irritating than chloride)
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with many drugs: bicarb, phosphates, ceftriaxone
‚Ä¢ Warm to room temperature before administration (crystallizes when cold)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Flushing, warmth sensation
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Sweating
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Injection site pain (less than chloride)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Bradycardia, heart block (with rapid administration)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension
‚Ä¢ Hypercalcemia (with overdose)
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation
‚Ä¢ Renal calculi (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Digitalis toxicity exacerbation (in patients on digoxin)
‚Ä¢ Milk-alkali syndrome (with chronic oral use + alkali)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Increased arrhythmias (monitor ECG closely)
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blockers: Antagonizes effect
‚Ä¢ Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones: Decreased absorption if given orally
‚Ä¢ Thiazide diuretics: Hypercalcemia risk
‚Ä¢ Sodium polystyrene sulfonate: May cause metabolic alkalosis
‚Ä¢ Ceftriaxone: Incompatible - forms precipitates in urine/gallbladder
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ For hyperkalemia: Works in 1-3 minutes, lasts 30-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ Does NOT lower K‚Å∫ - only cardiac protection. Must follow with K‚Å∫-lowering treatments
‚Ä¢ Gluconate vs chloride: Gluconate preferred for peripheral IV (less irritating)
‚Ä¢ Elemental calcium: 1g calcium gluconate = 90mg elemental Ca (vs 270mg in chloride)
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with bicarb - forms calcium carbonate precipitate
‚Ä¢ Digoxin patients: Use with extreme caution, give slowly, monitor ECG continuously
‚Ä¢ Repeat dosing: May need repeat doses every 30-60 minutes if K‚Å∫ still high
‚Ä¢ Storage: May crystallize when cold - warm to dissolve before use

5 Core Drug 4: Calcium Chloride

CALCIUM CHLORIDE - HIGHER POTENCY, MORE IRRITATING

Drug class: Electrolyte supplement, cation
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Provides ionized calcium ‚Üí stabilizes cardiac cell membranes
‚Ä¢ Increases myocardial contractility (positive inotrope)
‚Ä¢ Essential for neuromuscular function, coagulation, enzyme systems
‚Ä¢ Higher elemental calcium content than gluconate (27% vs 9%)
‚Ä¢ Acidifying effect (chloride ion) - may worsen acidosis
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia with ECG changes (when central line available)
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia (especially with rapid correction needed)
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blocker overdose (severe hypotension)
‚Ä¢ Magnesium sulfate overdose/toxicity
‚Ä¢ Beta-blocker overdose (with severe hypotension)
‚Ä¢ CPR (controversial - not in ACLS guidelines except specific situations)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypercalcemia
‚Ä¢ Digoxin toxicity (absolute contraindication - ventricular fibrillation risk)
‚Ä¢ Ventricular fibrillation (unless from hyperkalemia)
‚Ä¢ Renal failure with hyperphosphatemia (metastatic calcification risk)
‚Ä¢ Peripheral IV (relative - causes severe tissue necrosis if extravasates)
‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 5-10 mL of 10% calcium chloride (500-1000 mg) IV over 5-10 min
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia: 5-10 mL of 10% solution IV over 10-20 minutes
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blocker OD: 10-20 mL of 10% over 10 min, repeat as needed
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 1-2 mg/kg/hr elemental calcium (3.7-7.4 mg/kg/hr calcium chloride)
‚Ä¢ CPR (if indicated): 5-10 mL of 10% IV (historical use)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia: 0.2-0.5 mL/kg of 10% calcium chloride IV over 5-10 min
‚Ä¢ Hypocalcemia: 10-20 mg/kg (0.1-0.2 mL/kg of 10%) IV over 10-20 min
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 20 mg/kg IV over 10-30 minutes
‚Ä¢ MAX peripheral concentration: 20 mg/mL (2%) - dilute for peripheral use
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Route: IV through CENTRAL LINE ONLY (highly vesicant)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10% solution = 100 mg/mL (27 mg/mL elemental calcium)
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give slowly (max 0.7-1.4 mL/min or 70-140 mg/min)
‚Ä¢ NEVER give peripherally unless absolutely necessary and well diluted
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with bicarb, phosphates, ceftriaxone, amphotericin B
‚Ä¢ Contains aluminum (up to 4.5 mcg/mL) - concern for neonates/renal failure
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Severe burning at injection site (even with central line)
‚Ä¢ Flushing, warmth sensation
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (paradoxical, with rapid administration)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis, sloughing with extravasation (SERIOUS)
‚Ä¢ Hypercalcemia (with overdose) - stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones
‚Ä¢ Bradycardia, heart block (rapid administration)
‚Ä¢ Digoxin toxicity exacerbation (ventricular fibrillation)
‚Ä¢ Metabolic acidosis (chloride load)
‚Ä¢ Aluminum toxicity (with prolonged use in renal failure)
‚Ä¢ Phlebitis, thrombosis
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Digoxin: ABSOLUTE contraindication in toxicity (fatal arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blockers: Antagonizes effect (therapeutic in overdose)
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular blockers: Potentiates effects
‚Ä¢ Tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones: Decreased absorption
‚Ä¢ Sodium bicarbonate: Incompatible - precipitates
‚Ä¢ Ceftriaxone: Incompatible - risk of concretions
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ CENTRAL LINE ONLY - causes severe tissue necrosis if extravasates
‚Ä¢ 3x more elemental calcium than gluconate: 1g chloride = 270mg Ca¬≤‚Å∫ vs 90mg in gluconate
‚Ä¢ For hyperkalemia: Works faster than gluconate (higher ionized calcium)
‚Ä¢ Digoxin patients: CONTRAINDICATED if digoxin toxicity suspected
‚Ä¢ Calcium channel blocker OD: High-dose therapy (up to 10g over hours)
‚Ä¢ Extravasation treatment: Stop infusion, aspirate, hyaluronidase injection
‚Ä¢ ACLS: Not recommended in routine CPR (limited to hyperK‚Å∫, hypocalcemia, CCB OD)
‚Ä¢ Storage: Store at room temperature, crystals form when cold
üß† CALCIUM GLUCONATE vs CHLORIDE:
‚Ä¢ Calcium Gluconate: 9% elemental Ca, peripheral IV safe, first choice for hyperkalemia
‚Ä¢ Calcium Chloride: 27% elemental Ca, central line only, for critical cases/CCB overdose
‚Ä¢ 1g Calcium Chloride = 3g Calcium Gluconate in elemental calcium content

6 Core Drug 5: Potassium Chloride (Emergency Use)

POTASSIUM CHLORIDE - HIGH-RISK ELECTROLYTE REPLACEMENT

Drug class: Electrolyte supplement, cation
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Principal intracellular cation (98% of total body K‚Å∫ is intracellular)
‚Ä¢ Essential for resting membrane potential, cardiac rhythm, neuromuscular function
‚Ä¢ Activates Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase (with insulin, beta-agonists)
‚Ä¢ Renal excretion regulated by aldosterone, distal tubular flow, pH
‚Ä¢ Chloride salt helps correct metabolic alkalosis (chloride-responsive)

‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia with ECG changes (U waves, flattened T waves)
‚Ä¢ Digitalis toxicity (hypokalemia potentiates toxicity)
‚Ä¢ Periodic paralysis (hypokalemic type)
‚Ä¢ Diabetic ketoacidosis (K‚Å∫ shifts out during treatment)
‚Ä¢ Metabolic alkalosis (chloride-responsive)

‚Ä¢ Renal failure with oliguria/anuria (relative)
‚Ä¢ Addison's disease (relative - impaired excretion)
‚Ä¢ Severe tissue trauma (crush injury - K‚Å∫ release)
‚Ä¢ Digitalis toxicity with AV block (relative)
‚Ä¢ NEVER IV push (cardiac arrest)

‚Ä¢ Moderate (K‚Å∫ 2.5-3.0): 20-40 mEq IV over 2-4 hours

‚Ä¢ Max central concentration: 80-100 mEq/L (depends on institution)

‚Ä¢ IV replacement: 0.5-1 mEq/kg over 1-2 hours (max 40 mEq)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 2-4 mEq/kg/day
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 0.5-1 mEq/kg over 1-2 hours (max 10 mEq)
‚Ä¢ Max rate: 0.5 mEq/kg/hr (with cardiac monitoring)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV infusion (preferred for emergencies), PO, NG
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 2 mEq/mL (concentrated) or pre-mixed bags (10, 20, 40 mEq/L)
‚Ä¢ NEVER IV push or bolus - cardiac arrest risk
‚Ä¢ Peripheral IV: Max 40 mEq/L (or 10 mEq/100mL) to avoid phlebitis
‚Ä¢ Central line: Higher concentrations possible (follow institutional policy)
‚Ä¢ Use infusion pump with dose limit set
‚Ä¢ Incompatible with many drugs: amphotericin, ceftriaxone, fat emulsions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (especially PO)
‚Ä¢ Phlebitis, pain at IV site
‚Ä¢ Diarrhea (PO preparation)
‚Ä¢ Metallic taste
‚Ä¢ Sensation of warmth with IV
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Hyperkalemia ‚Üí cardiac arrest (most serious)
‚Ä¢ Tissue necrosis with extravasation (high concentrations)
‚Ä¢ Small bowel lesions/ulceration (enteric-coated tablets)
‚Ä¢ Metabolic acidosis (with chloride salt in renal impairment)
‚Ä¢ Phlebitis, thrombosis
‚Ä¢ Arrhythmias (both hypo- and hyperkalemia)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs, potassium-sparing diuretics: Hyperkalemia risk
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Hypokalemia increases toxicity; hyperkalemia decreases effect
‚Ä¢ Beta-agonists: Lower K‚Å∫ (intracellular shift)
‚Ä¢ Insulin: Lowers K‚Å∫ (intracellular shift)
‚Ä¢ Loop/thiazide diuretics: Increase K‚Å∫ loss
‚Ä¢ Sodium polystyrene sulfonate: Lowers K‚Å∫
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "NEVER IV PUSH" - causes immediate hyperkalemia, cardiac arrest
‚Ä¢ ECG monitoring required for rates >10 mEq/hr or K‚Å∫ <2.5 mEq/L
‚Ä¢ Magnesium matters: Correct hypomagnesemia first (renal wasting of K‚Å∫)
‚Ä¢ DKA: K‚Å∫ often high initially but total body deficit - add to fluids when K‚Å∫ <5.5
‚Ä¢ Recheck K‚Å∫ 2-4 hours after starting replacement
‚Ä¢ Chloride vs other salts: Chloride for metabolic alkalosis; acetate/phosphate for acidosis
‚Ä¢ Extravasation: Hyaluronidase injection for tissue salvage
