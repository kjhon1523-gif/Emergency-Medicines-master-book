2 Core Drug 1: Ceftriaxone

CEFTRIAXONE - BROAD-SPECTRUM 3RD GENERATION CEPHALOSPORIN

Drug class: Third-generation cephalosporin, beta-lactam antibiotic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) ‚Üí inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis
‚Ä¢ Bactericidal (concentration-dependent killing)
‚Ä¢ Excellent CSF penetration (10-20% of serum levels) ‚Üí good for meningitis
‚Ä¢ Time-dependent killing: Efficacy depends on time above MIC
‚Ä¢ Long half-life (6-9 hours) ‚Üí once or twice daily dosing
‚Ä¢ Excreted dual pathway (renal 40%, biliary 60%) ‚Üí safe in renal failure
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP)
‚Ä¢ Bacterial meningitis (with vancomycin for empiric coverage)
‚Ä¢ Complicated urinary tract infections (pyelonephritis)
‚Ä¢ Intra-abdominal infections (with metronidazole)
‚Ä¢ Gonorrhoea (single dose 500mg-1g IM)
‚Ä¢ Lyme disease (neurologic, cardiac, late manifestations)
‚Ä¢ Febrile neutropenia (with other agents)
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis (colorectal, some orthopaedic procedures)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known severe hypersensitivity to ceftriaxone or cephalosporins
‚Ä¢ History of anaphylaxis to penicillins (5-10% cross-reactivity)
‚Ä¢ Hyperbilirubinemic neonates (displaces bilirubin from albumin)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent IV calcium administration in neonates/infants (precipitation)
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment with renal impairment (adjust dose)
‚Ä¢ Gallbladder sludge/calculi history (ceftriaxone causes pseudolithiasis)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Standard: 1-2 g IV/IM once daily
‚Ä¢ Meningitis: 2 g IV q12h (higher dosing for CNS penetration)
‚Ä¢ Severe infections: 2 g IV q12-24h
‚Ä¢ Gonorrhoea: 500 mg IM single dose + azithromycin 1g PO
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis: 1-2 g IV single dose 30-60 min before incision
‚Ä¢ Max daily dose: 4 g (rarely needed, usually 2-3 g)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonates (0-28 days): 50 mg/kg IV once daily
‚Ä¢ Infants/children: 50-75 mg/kg IV once daily (max 2 g)
‚Ä¢ Meningitis: 100 mg/kg IV loading, then 50-75 mg/kg q12h (max 4 g/day)
‚Ä¢ Acute otitis media: 50 mg/kg IM single dose (max 1 g)
‚Ä¢ Avoid concurrent calcium in neonates/infants (risk of precipitation)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM (painful, add lidocaine)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 250 mg, 500 mg, 1 g, 2 g vials (powder)
‚Ä¢ IV reconstitution: Add 10 mL sterile water per 1 g = 100 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: Further dilute in 50-100 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 30 min
‚Ä¢ IM preparation: Add 1% lidocaine (without adrenaline) to reduce pain
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: NOT with calcium-containing solutions, aminoglycosides (separate lines)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Diarrhea (3-10%, less than other broad-spectrum antibiotics)
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions (pain, phlebitis)
‚Ä¢ Rash (1-2%, usually maculopapular)
‚Ä¢ Elevated LFTs (transient, benign)
‚Ä¢ Eosinophilia
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare: 0.01-0.1%)
‚Ä¢ Clostridioides difficile infection (antibiotic-associated colitis)
‚Ä¢ Biliary pseudolithiasis (sludge in gallbladder, usually reversible)
‚Ä¢ Hemolytic anemia (immune-mediated, rare)
‚Ä¢ Neutropenia, thrombocytopenia (rare, reversible)
‚Ä¢ Nephrolithiasis (rare, from calcium-ceftriaxone precipitates)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (high doses in renal failure)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Increased INR (suppresses vitamin K-producing gut flora)
‚Ä¢ Calcium-containing solutions: Precipitation risk (do NOT mix or infuse concurrently)
‚Ä¢ Aminoglycosides: Synergistic but physically incompatible (separate lines)
‚Ä¢ Probenecid: Increases ceftriaxone levels (not usually co-administered)
‚Ä¢ Oral contraceptives: Reduced efficacy (theoretical, use backup)
‚Ä¢ Live vaccines: Reduced immune response (typhoid, cholera)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Meningitis dose = 2g q12h" - double dose, double frequency for CNS penetration
‚Ä¢ Once daily dosing: Long half-life allows single daily dose for most indications
‚Ä¢ Penicillin allergy: 5-10% cross-reactivity - use with caution in Type I hypersensitivity
‚Ä¢ Renal/hepatic dual excretion: No dose adjustment needed for renal failure alone
‚Ä¢ IM administration: Very painful - MUST use 1% lidocaine as diluent
‚Ä¢ Calcium warning: Do not mix with calcium-containing solutions (precipitation risk)
‚Ä¢ C. diff risk: Lower than clindamycin, similar to other broad-spectrum antibiotics
‚Ä¢ Gonorrhoea resistance: Worldwide concern - always add azithromycin 1g PO

3 Core Drug 2: Ampicillin

AMPICILLIN - AMINOPENICILLIN FOR GRAM-POSITIVE & SOME GRAM-NEGATIVE COVERAGE

Drug class: Aminopenicillin, beta-lactam antibiotic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds penicillin-binding proteins ‚Üí inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis
‚Ä¢ Bactericidal (time-dependent killing)
‚Ä¢ Broader spectrum than penicillin G: covers some gram-negatives (E. coli, Proteus, H. influenzae)
‚Ä¢ Destroyed by beta-lactamases (most staph, many gram-negatives)
‚Ä¢ Good CSF penetration when meninges inflamed (10-30% of serum levels)
‚Ä¢ Primarily renal excretion (dose adjust in renal impairment)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Bacterial meningitis (with ceftriaxone/cefotaxime for Listeria coverage)
‚Ä¢ Enterococcal infections (endocarditis, UTI - with aminoglycoside for synergy)
‚Ä¢ Listeria monocytogenes infections (meningitis in neonates, elderly, immunocompromised)
‚Ä¢ Community-acquired respiratory infections (with beta-lactamase inhibitor)
‚Ä¢ Urinary tract infections (uncomplicated, but resistance common)
‚Ä¢ Intra-abdominal infections (with metronidazole)
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis (gastrointestinal, genitourinary procedures)
‚Ä¢ Typhoid fever (though resistance increasing)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known severe hypersensitivity to ampicillin or penicillins
‚Ä¢ Infectious mononucleosis (high rash incidence: 90-100%)
‚Ä¢ Cytomegalovirus infection (increased rash risk)
‚Ä¢ Acute lymphocytic leukemia (increased rash risk)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent allopurinol use (increased rash risk)
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <10 mL/min) without dose adjustment
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Moderate infections: 1-2 g IV q6h
‚Ä¢ Severe infections: 2 g IV q4-6h
‚Ä¢ Meningitis (Listeria coverage): 2 g IV q4h + gentamicin
‚Ä¢ Enterococcal endocarditis: 2 g IV q4h + gentamicin 1 mg/kg q8h
‚Ä¢ Urinary tract infections: 500 mg PO q6h or 1 g IV q6h
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis: 2 g IV single dose 30-60 min before incision
‚Ä¢ Max daily dose: 12 g (usually 8 g/day sufficient)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonates (0-7 days): 50 mg/kg IV q12h
‚Ä¢ Neonates (8-28 days): 50 mg/kg IV q8h
‚Ä¢ Infants/children: 100-200 mg/kg/day IV divided q6h (max 12 g/day)
‚Ä¢ Meningitis: 200-300 mg/kg/day IV divided q4-6h (max 12 g/day)
‚Ä¢ Oral: 50-100 mg/kg/day PO divided q6h (max 4 g/day)
‚Ä¢ Renal adjustment: Required for CrCl <50 mL/min
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred for severe infections), IM, PO
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 250 mg, 500 mg, 1 g, 2 g vials (powder)
‚Ä¢ IV reconstitution: Add 10 mL sterile water per 1 g = 100 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: Further dilute in 50-100 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 30 min
‚Ä¢ IM: Deep IM injection, rotate sites (painful)
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: NOT with aminoglycosides (inactivates them), separate lines
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Rash (5-10%, usually maculopapular, non-allergic)
‚Ä¢ Diarrhea (oral more than IV)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (especially oral)
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions
‚Ä¢ Vaginal candidiasis (disturbance of normal flora)
‚Ä¢ Elevated LFTs (transient)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (0.01-0.05%)
‚Ä¢ Ampicillin rash (90-100% in EBV/CMV infections - non-allergic)
‚Ä¢ Clostridioides difficile infection
‚Ä¢ Pseudomembranous colitis
‚Ä¢ Hemolytic anemia (immune-mediated)
‚Ä¢ Interstitial nephritis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (high doses, renal impairment)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Aminoglycosides: Physically incompatible (inactivates aminoglycosides)
‚Ä¢ Allopurinol: Increased rash incidence (avoid combination)
‚Ä¢ Methotrexate: Increased methotrexate toxicity (reduced renal clearance)
‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Increased INR (suppresses vitamin K-producing gut flora)
‚Ä¢ Oral contraceptives: Reduced efficacy (use backup contraception)
‚Ä¢ Probenecid: Increases ampicillin levels (sometimes used intentionally)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Listeria coverage": Must add ampicillin to meningitis regimen if >50 years, immunocompromised, or neonate
‚Ä¢ Ampicillin rash: 90-100% in EBV mononucleosis - NOT allergic, but often mistaken for allergy
‚Ä¢ Enterococcus coverage: Only penicillin/ampicillin reliably cover enterococci (not cephalosporins)
‚Ä¢ Beta-lactamase vulnerable: Destroyed by staph beta-lactamase - use flucloxacillin for staph
‚Ä¢ Synergy with aminoglycosides: For enterococcal endocarditis - bactericidal combination
‚Ä¢ Renal adjustment: Essential - ampicillin accumulates in renal failure
‚Ä¢ CSF penetration: Good only with inflamed meninges (10-30% of serum levels)
‚Ä¢ Oral absorption: Only 40% oral bioavailability - IV preferred for serious infections

4 Core Drug 3: Gentamicin

GENTAMICIN - CONCENTRATION-DEPENDENT AMINOGLYCOSIDE

Drug class: Aminoglycoside antibiotic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds 30S ribosomal subunit ‚Üí inhibits bacterial protein synthesis
‚Ä¢ Bactericidal (concentration-dependent killing)
‚Ä¢ Post-antibiotic effect (PAE) - continues killing after levels drop below MIC
‚Ä¢ Synergistic with beta-lactams (penicillins/cephalosporins) for gram-positives
‚Ä¢ Poor CSF penetration (even with inflamed meninges)
‚Ä¢ Renal excretion exclusively ‚Üí accumulates in renal impairment
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Gram-negative sepsis (Pseudomonas, E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter)
‚Ä¢ Febrile neutropenia (with anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam)
‚Ä¢ Synergy for gram-positive infections (enterococcal endocarditis with ampicillin)
‚Ä¢ Complicated urinary tract infections (pyelonephritis)
‚Ä¢ Intra-abdominal infections (with metronidazole/anaerobic coverage)
‚Ä¢ Neonatal sepsis (with ampicillin)
‚Ä¢ Osteomyelitis/septic arthritis (with anti-staphylococcal agent)
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis (colorectal, some urological procedures)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to gentamicin or aminoglycosides
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (exacerbates neuromuscular blockade)
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min) without dose adjustment
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use of other nephrotoxic/ototoxic drugs (if avoidable)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - risk of fetal ototoxicity, Category D)
‚Ä¢ Pre-existing hearing loss/vestibular dysfunction
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Once daily dosing (ODD): 5-7 mg/kg IV once daily (preferred)
‚Ä¢ Traditional dosing: 1.5-2 mg/kg IV q8h (less preferred now)
‚Ä¢ Synergy dosing (endocarditis): 1 mg/kg IV q8h with ampicillin
‚Ä¢ Pneumonia (with pseudomonas risk): 7 mg/kg IV once daily
‚Ä¢ Urinary tract infections: 5 mg/kg IV once daily (lower dose sufficient)
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis: 5 mg/kg IV single dose 30-60 min before incision
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM): Essential for >3 days therapy
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonates (0-7 days, <2kg): 3 mg/kg IV q24h
‚Ä¢ Neonates (0-7 days, >2kg): 4 mg/kg IV q24h
‚Ä¢ Neonates (>7 days): 5 mg/kg IV q24h
‚Ä¢ Infants/children: 7.5 mg/kg IV once daily (or 2.5 mg/kg q8h)
‚Ä¢ Cystic fibrosis: Higher doses often needed (10-12 mg/kg/day)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic drug monitoring: CRUCIAL in pediatrics
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, topical, intraocular, intra-thecal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10 mg/mL, 40 mg/mL vials
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: Dilute in 50-100 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 30-60 minutes
‚Ä¢ IM: Deep IM injection, rotate sites
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: NOT with penicillins/cephalosporins (inactivates gentamicin)
‚Ä¢ Storage: Room temperature, protected from light
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Nephrotoxicity (5-25%, usually reversible)
‚Ä¢ Ototoxicity (2-25%, often irreversible)
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular blockade (especially with rapid infusion)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Rash, pruritus
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Acute kidney injury (dose and duration dependent)
‚Ä¢ Permanent hearing loss (high frequency first)
‚Ä¢ Vestibular toxicity (vertigo, ataxia, nystagmus)
‚Ä¢ Respiratory paralysis (with rapid IV push or in myasthenia gravis)
‚Ä¢ Hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia, hypocalcemia
‚Ä¢ Encephalopathy (in renal failure)
‚Ä¢ Peripheral neuropathy (rare)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other nephrotoxic drugs: Additive toxicity (vancomycin, amphotericin, cisplatin)
‚Ä¢ Other ototoxic drugs: Additive toxicity (loop diuretics, cisplatin)
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular blockers: Potentiation of paralysis (suxamethonium, vecuronium)
‚Ä¢ Penicillins/cephalosporins: Physically incompatible (inactivates gentamicin)
‚Ä¢ Cyclosporin/tacrolimus: Increased nephrotoxicity
‚Ä¢ IV contrast: Increased nephrotoxicity risk
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Once daily dosing (ODD) preferred": 5-7 mg/kg once daily - better efficacy, less toxicity
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM): Check levels before 4th dose (trough <1 mg/L)
‚Ä¢ Peak levels: Not needed for ODD, only for traditional q8h dosing
‚Ä¢ Nephrotoxicity: Monitor creatinine daily, stop if >50% increase from baseline
‚Ä¢ Ototoxicity: Often irreversible - ask about tinnitus, hearing loss, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Duration limit: Usually ‚â§7-10 days to minimize toxicity
‚Ä¢ Synergy dosing: Lower dose (1 mg/kg q8h) for enterococcal endocarditis with ampicillin
‚Ä¢ Do NOT mix with penicillins: Inactivates gentamicin - give at different times, different lines

5 Core Drug 4: Piperacillin-Tazobactam

PIPERACILLIN-TAZOBACTAM - BROAD-SPECTRUM ANTI-PSEUDOMONAL PENICILLIN + BETA-LACTAMASE INHIBITOR

Drug class: Extended-spectrum penicillin + beta-lactamase inhibitor
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Piperacillin: Binds penicillin-binding proteins ‚Üí inhibits cell wall synthesis
‚Ä¢ Tazobactam: Irreversibly inhibits beta-lactamases (especially TEM, SHV, some ESBLs)
‚Ä¢ Synergistic combination extends spectrum to beta-lactamase producers
‚Ä¢ Bactericidal (time-dependent killing)
‚Ä¢ Anti-pseudomonal activity (piperacillin component)
‚Ä¢ Anaerobic coverage (good for intra-abdominal infections)
‚Ä¢ Renal excretion (both components) ‚Üí dose adjust in renal impairment
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Hospital-acquired pneumonia (including ventilator-associated)
‚Ä¢ Complicated intra-abdominal infections
‚Ä¢ Complicated urinary tract infections (including pyelonephritis)
‚Ä¢ Febrile neutropenia (with/without additional anti-pseudomonal)
‚Ä¢ Skin and soft tissue infections (complicated, diabetic foot)
‚Ä¢ Septicemia of unknown source
‚Ä¢ Pelvic inflammatory disease (hospitalized patients)
‚Ä¢ Bone and joint infections (as part of combination therapy)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known severe hypersensitivity to piperacillin, tazobactam, or penicillins
‚Ä¢ History of anaphylaxis to penicillins
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment (CrCl <20 mL/min) without extended interval dosing
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use of sodium salts in sodium-restricted patients (high sodium content)
‚Ä¢ Infectious mononucleosis (increased rash risk with penicillins)
‚Ä¢ Cystic fibrosis with frequent use (may select for resistant organisms)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Standard dosing: 4.5 g (4g piperacillin + 0.5g tazobactam) IV q6-8h
‚Ä¢ Extended infusion: 4.5 g IV over 4 hours q8h (better PK/PD for resistant organisms)
‚Ä¢ Pseudomonas infections: 4.5 g IV q6h or extended infusion
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment (CrCl 20-40): 3.375 g IV q6h or 2.25 g IV q6-8h
‚Ä¢ Renal impairment (CrCl <20): 2.25 g IV q8h or 4.5 g IV q12h
‚Ä¢ Hemodialysis: 2.25 g IV q12h, give after dialysis
‚Ä¢ Max daily dose: 18 g piperacillin component (usually 13.5-16 g)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Children 2-9 months: 80 mg piperacillin/kg (with 10 mg tazobactam/kg) IV q8h
‚Ä¢ Children ‚â•9 months to 12 years: 100 mg piperacillin/kg (with 12.5 mg tazobactam/kg) IV q8h
‚Ä¢ Children >12 years or >40 kg: Adult dosing
‚Ä¢ Appendicitis/peritonitis: 100 mg piperacillin/kg (with 12.5 mg tazobactam/kg) IV q8h
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Limited data, consult specialist
‚Ä¢ Renal adjustment: Required for pediatric patients with renal impairment
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV only (not for IM use)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 2.25 g, 3.375 g, 4.5 g vials (powder)
‚Ä¢ Reconstitution: Add diluent (sterile water/NS) per manufacturer instructions
‚Ä¢ Standard infusion: Dilute in 50-100 mL, infuse over 30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Extended infusion: Dilute in 50-250 mL, infuse over 4 hours
‚Ä¢ Compatibility: NOT with aminoglycosides in same line (inactivates them)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Diarrhea (7-11%)
‚Ä¢ Headache (7%)
‚Ä¢ Insomnia (7%)
‚Ä¢ Constipation (7%)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting (4-6%)
‚Ä¢ Rash, pruritus (3-4%)
‚Ä¢ Injection site reactions (2%)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Clostridioides difficile infection (antibiotic-associated colitis)
‚Ä¢ Leukopenia, neutropenia (especially with prolonged use)
‚Ä¢ Hepatitis, elevated LFTs (1-2%)
‚Ä¢ Seizures (high doses, renal impairment)
‚Ä¢ Interstitial nephritis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Hypokalemia (piperacillin has anti-mineralocorticoid effect)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Aminoglycosides: Synergistic but physically incompatible (separate lines)
‚Ä¢ Vecuronium: Prolongs neuromuscular blockade
‚Ä¢ Methotrexate: Increases methotrexate toxicity (reduced renal clearance)
‚Ä¢ Oral contraceptives: Reduced efficacy (use backup)
‚Ä¢ Probenecid: Increases piperacillin levels (rarely used together)
‚Ä¢ Heparin: May increase bleeding risk (affects platelet function)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Workhorse of empiric sepsis therapy": Broad coverage for most ICU pathogens
‚Ä¢ Extended infusion: 4.5g over 4h q8h ‚Üí better time above MIC for resistant organisms
‚Ä¢ Pseudomonas coverage: One of few penicillins with reliable anti-pseudomonal activity
‚Ä¢ ESBL coverage: Tazobactam inhibits some (not all) ESBLs - carbapenems preferred for known ESBL
‚Ä¢ Sodium load: 4.5g contains ~9 mmol sodium - consider in heart failure, renal failure
‚Ä¢ Renal adjustment: CRITICAL - accumulates in renal failure (seizure risk)
‚Ä¢ Not for MRSA: Does NOT cover MRSA - add vancomycin/linezolid if MRSA suspected
‚Ä¢ Penicillin allergy: Avoid if Type I (anaphylaxis) - carbapenem or aztreonam alternative

6 Core Drug 5: Vancomycin (Emergency Basics)

VANCOMYCIN - GLYCOPEPTIDE FOR MRSA & SERIOUS GRAM-POSITIVE INFECTIONS

Drug class: Glycopeptide antibiotic
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds D-alanyl-D-alanine portion of cell wall precursors ‚Üí inhibits cell wall synthesis
‚Ä¢ Bactericidal against most gram-positives (bacteriostatic against enterococci)
‚Ä¢ Time-dependent killing (AUC/MIC ratio predicts efficacy)
‚Ä¢ Poor CSF penetration (achieves therapeutic levels only with inflamed meninges)
‚Ä¢ Renal excretion exclusively ‚Üí accumulates in renal impairment
‚Ä¢ Not absorbed orally (except for C. difficile colitis treatment)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections
‚Ä¢ Penicillin-resistant Streptococcus pneumoniae infections
‚Ä¢ Serious gram-positive infections in penicillin-allergic patients
‚Ä¢ Infective endocarditis (with gentamicin for synergy in some cases)
‚Ä¢ Meningitis (with ceftriaxone for empiric coverage of resistant pneumococcus)
‚Ä¢ Clostridioides difficile colitis (oral formulation only)
‚Ä¢ Surgical prophylaxis (in penicillin-allergic patients, procedures with MRSA risk)
‚Ä¢ Empirical therapy for severe sepsis of unknown source
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known severe hypersensitivity to vancomycin
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use with other ototoxic/nephrotoxic drugs (if alternatives exist)
‚Ä¢ Severe renal impairment without dose adjustment
‚Ä¢ Avoid in pregnancy unless absolutely necessary (Category C)
‚Ä¢ Not for trivial infections or as first-line for susceptible organisms
‚Ä¢ Avoid in patients with hearing loss (ototoxicity risk)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Loading dose: 25-30 mg/kg IV (actual body weight, max 2-3g)
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (based on renal function)
‚Ä¢ Meningitis/endocarditis: Higher doses to achieve trough 15-20 mg/L
‚Ä¢ Skin/soft tissue: Lower trough target 10-15 mg/L usually sufficient
‚Ä¢ Oral for C. diff: 125 mg PO q6h for 10 days (IV vancomycin NOT effective for C. diff)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM): ESSENTIAL - check trough before 4th dose
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Neonates (0-7 days): 15 mg/kg IV q24h
‚Ä¢ Neonates (8-28 days): 15 mg/kg IV q12h
‚Ä¢ Infants/children: 15 mg/kg IV q6-8h (adjust for renal function)
‚Ä¢ Meningitis: 60 mg/kg/day IV divided q6h (higher doses needed)
‚Ä¢ Oral for C. diff: 10 mg/kg PO q6h (max 125 mg/dose)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic drug monitoring: CRITICAL in pediatrics
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (for systemic infections), PO (for C. diff only)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 500 mg, 1 g, 5 g, 10 g vials (powder)
‚Ä¢ IV reconstitution: Add 10 mL sterile water per 500 mg = 50 mg/mL
‚Ä¢ IV infusion: Further dilute to ‚â§5 mg/mL (e.g., 1g in 200 mL), infuse over ‚â•60 min

7 Core Drug 6: Artesunate

ARTESUNATE - FIRST-LINE FOR SEVERE MALARIA

Drug class: Artemisinin derivative, antimalarial
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Active metabolite (dihydroartemisinin) generates free radicals ‚Üí damages parasite membranes
‚Ä¢ Rapidly reduces parasite biomass (most rapid of all antimalarials)
‚Ä¢ Acts on all stages of parasite life cycle (including early gametocytes)
‚Ä¢ Short half-life (45 minutes) but active metabolites last longer
‚Ä¢ Synergistic with longer-acting partner drugs (prevents recrudescence)
‚Ä¢ No cross-resistance with other antimalarial classes (usually)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Severe malaria (Plasmodium falciparum, knowlesi, vivax with severe features)
‚Ä¢ Uncomplicated malaria (as part of Artemisinin Combination Therapy - ACT)
‚Ä¢ First-line for all malaria in artemisinin-sensitive regions
‚Ä¢ Malaria in pregnancy (2nd/3rd trimester - 1st trimester quinine preferred)
‚Ä¢ Cerebral malaria (superior to quinine)
‚Ä¢ Imported malaria in non-endemic countries
‚Ä¢ Pre-referral treatment in remote settings (rectal formulation)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Known hypersensitivity to artemisinins
‚Ä¢ First trimester of pregnancy (relative - quinine preferred)
‚Ä¢ Concurrent use with strong CYP2A6 inducers (may reduce efficacy)
‚Ä¢ History of prolonged QT interval (less risk than other antimalarials)
‚Ä¢ Breastfeeding (limited data, but benefits usually outweigh risks in severe malaria)
‚Ä¢ Neonates (limited data, use under specialist guidance)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Severe malaria (IV): 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24h, then once daily until oral possible
‚Ä¢ Uncomplicated malaria (oral): 4 mg/kg daily √ó 3 days + partner drug (ACT)
‚Ä¢ IM alternative: 2.4 mg/kg IM, then at 12h and 24h, then daily
‚Ä¢ Switch to oral: When patient can tolerate, complete with 3-day ACT course
‚Ä¢ Rectal (pre-referral): 10 mg/kg single dose (adults/children same)
‚Ä¢ Max single dose: 150 mg (vial size limitation)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Severe malaria (IV): 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24h, then once daily
‚Ä¢ Weight-based vials: Use appropriate vial size (60mg if 20-25kg, etc.)
‚Ä¢ Neonates/infants: Same mg/kg dosing (limited data in <5kg)
‚Ä¢ Oral ACT: Weight-based tablets available (20/120mg, 40/240mg artesunate/sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine)
‚Ä¢ Rectal: 10 mg/kg single dose for pre-referral
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, rectal, oral
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 60 mg powder vial with 1 mL 5% sodium bicarbonate ampoule
‚Ä¢ IV reconstitution: Add sodium bicarbonate to powder, swirl until clear (~1 min)
‚Ä¢ IV dilution: Add to 5 mL 5% dextrose (final ~10 mg/mL), give immediately
‚Ä¢ IV administration: Bolus over 1-2 minutes (NOT infusion)
‚Ä¢ Stability: Use within 1 hour of reconstitution (unstable in solution)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Transient reticulocytopenia (days 7-14, recovers by day 21)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
‚Ä¢ Dizziness, headache
‚Ä¢ Transient first-degree heart block (rare, resolves spontaneously)
‚Ä¢ Dark urine (not haemoglobinuria)
‚Ä¢ Injection site pain (IM route)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Delayed haemolytic anaemia (7-21 days post-treatment, "post-artesunate delayed haemolysis")
‚Ä¢ Allergic reactions (rare)
‚Ä¢ Neutropenia (rare)
‚Ä¢ Hepatotoxicity (rare)
‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular effects (QT prolongation less than quinine)
‚Ä¢ Neurotoxicity (in animal studies, not clearly demonstrated in humans)
‚Ä¢ Teratogenicity (theoretical, avoid in 1st trimester)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other antimalarials: Synergistic (used in combination therapy)
‚Ä¢ Enzyme inducers: Rifampicin, carbamazepine may reduce levels
‚Ä¢ Antiretroviral drugs: Complex interactions, consult specialist
‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Potential interaction (monitor INR)
‚Ä¢ CYP2A6 inhibitors: May increase artesunate levels
‚Ä¢ Mefloquine: Increased risk of neuropsychiatric effects
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "First-line for severe malaria": 35% mortality reduction compared to quinine
‚Ä¢ Dosing regimen: 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24h, then daily (NOT weight bands, exact kg)

‚Ä¢ IV bolus, not infusion: Give over 1-2 minutes (stability issues)

8.2 Antibiotic Pharmacokinetics/Pharmacodynamics (PK/PD)

üß™ ANTIBIOTIC PK/PD CLASSES:

1. TIME-DEPENDENT KILLING (Œ≤-lactams, vancomycin):
‚Ä¢ Efficacy determined by time above MIC (%T>MIC)
‚Ä¢ Goal: Maintain levels above MIC for 40-70% of dosing interval
‚Ä¢ Dosing strategy: Frequent dosing or continuous infusion
‚Ä¢ Examples: Penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, vancomycin

2. CONCENTRATION-DEPENDENT KILLING (Aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones):
‚Ä¢ Efficacy determined by peak concentration to MIC ratio (Cmax/MIC)
‚Ä¢ Have post-antibiotic effect (PAE)
‚Ä¢ Dosing strategy: High dose, less frequent (once daily for aminoglycosides)
‚Ä¢ Examples: Gentamicin, tobramycin, ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin

3. AUC-DEPENDENT KILLING (Vancomycin, azithromycin, tetracyclines):
‚Ä¢ Efficacy determined by area under curve to MIC ratio (AUC/MIC)
‚Ä¢ Dosing strategy: Optimize total daily dose (can be divided or once daily)
‚Ä¢ Examples: Vancomycin, linezolid, tigecycline, azithromycin

9 Antimicrobial Stewardship in Emergencies

9.1 The 5 D's of Antimicrobial Stewardship

1. DIAGNOSIS: Accurate diagnosis before antibiotics if possible
‚Ä¢ Cultures before antibiotics (blood x2, urine, sputum, CSF as indicated)
‚Ä¢ Imaging to identify source
‚Ä¢ Biomarkers (procalcitonin to guide antibiotic duration)

2. DRUG: Right drug for the likely pathogen
‚Ä¢ Local antibiogram knowledge
‚Ä¢ Narrowest spectrum that covers likely pathogens
‚Ä¢ Consider resistance risk factors (recent antibiotics, healthcare exposure, travel)

3. DOSE: Right dose for the site and patient
‚Ä¢ Weight-based dosing for obese, pediatric patients
‚Ä¢ Renal/hepatic adjustment
‚Ä¢ Loading doses for severe infections (vancomycin, aminoglycosides)

4. DURATION: Right duration - not too short, not too long
‚Ä¢ CAP: 5-7 days (if good response)
‚Ä¢ UTI: 7 days (pyelonephritis), 3 days (cystitis)
‚Ä¢ Intra-abdominal: 4-7 days if source controlled
‚Ä¢ Stop antibiotics if non-infectious cause identified

5. DE-ESCALATION: Narrow spectrum when culture results available
‚Ä¢ Day 3: Review cultures, narrow spectrum if possible
‚Ä¢ Switch IV to oral when appropriate (clinical improvement, tolerating oral)
‚Ä¢ Stop antibiotics if cultures negative and alternative diagnosis

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM) Guidelines

  1. Vancomycin: Trough level before 4th dose
    • Target: 10-15 mg/L for most infections
    • Target: 15-20 mg/L for pneumonia, meningitis, endocarditis
    • Check weekly if stable renal function, more often if changing
  2. Gentamicin/Tobramycin (once daily): Trough before 4th dose
    • Target: <1 mg/L (to prevent accumulation)
    • Peak levels not needed for once daily dosing
    • Check every 3-4 days or with changing renal function
  3. Aminoglycosides (traditional dosing): Peak and trough
    • Peak (30 min post-dose): 5-10 mg/L
    • Trough (<1 mg/L for q8h, <2 mg/L for q12h)
  4. Beta-lactams (selected cases): Trough levels
    • Consider for critically ill, burns, cystic fibrosis, renal replacement
    • Target: 4-8x MIC for the suspected pathogen
