0 Introduction: Brain Under Threat

‚ö†Ô∏è BRAIN CELLS DIE QUICKLY: For every minute of status epilepticus, irreversible neuronal damage occurs. For every 1 mmHg rise in ICP above 20, cerebral perfusion decreases. Time is brain tissue.
üß† Core Concept: Neurological emergencies involve four key threats:
1. Electrical storm (seizures, status epilepticus)
2. Pressure crisis (raised ICP, herniation)
3. Chemical assault (overdose, withdrawal)
4. Behavioral emergency (agitation, psychosis)
Each requires specific pharmacological approaches.

1 Status Epilepticus: Definition & Phases

Status Epilepticus: A condition resulting either from the failure of the mechanisms responsible for seizure termination or from the initiation of mechanisms that lead to abnormally prolonged seizures. Defined as: ‚Ä¢ ‚â•5 minutes of continuous seizure activity OR ‚Ä¢ ‚â•2 seizures without full recovery of consciousness between them

1.1 Phases of Status Epilepticus

Phase Time Frame Pathophysiology Treatment Goals Drug Choices
Early 0-30 minutes Excessive neuronal firing, GABA receptors still functional Stop seizure, protect airway Benzodiazepines (first-line)
Established 30-60 minutes GABA receptor internalization, glutamate excitotoxicity Stop seizure, prevent neuronal damage Antiepileptics (phenytoin, valproate, levetiracetam)
Refractory >60 minutes Neuronal death, blood-brain barrier breakdown, cerebral edema ICU management, burst suppression Anesthetics (propofol, midazolam, thiopental)
Super-refractory >24 hours Failure to respond to anesthetic agents Experimental therapies, ketamine, immunomodulation Ketamine, inhalational anesthetics, hypothermia

2 Core Drug 1: Diazepam

DIAZEPAM - RAPID-ONSET, LONG-ACTING BENZODIAZEPINE

Drug class: Benzodiazepine (long-acting)
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Potentiates GABA activity by binding to GABA-A receptor
‚Ä¢ Increases frequency of chloride channel opening ‚Üí neuronal hyperpolarization
‚Ä¢ Central muscle relaxant effect (acts on spinal cord)
‚Ä¢ Anticonvulsant, anxiolytic, sedative, amnestic properties
‚Ä¢ Highly lipophilic ‚Üí rapid CNS penetration but also rapid redistribution
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Acute seizure control (status epilepticus)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal seizures and delirium tremens
‚Ä¢ Muscle spasm (tetanus, spasticity)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation (especially in children)
‚Ä¢ Anxiety/agitation (though lorazepam often preferred)
‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepine withdrawal
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Severe respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Acute narrow-angle glaucoma
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment
‚Ä¢ Sleep apnea (relative)
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (relative - muscle weakness)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (especially first trimester - teratogenic)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 5-10 mg IV over 2-5 min, repeat every 10-15 min (max 30 mg)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal: 5-10 mg IV/PO, repeat hourly until calm (symptom-triggered)
‚Ä¢ Muscle spasm: 2-10 mg IV/IM, then 2-10 mg PO TID-QID
‚Ä¢ Anxiety/agitation: 2-10 mg IV/IM/PO, repeat in 3-4 hours if needed
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 5-10 mg IV 5-10 min before procedure
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV (max 10 mg) over 2-5 min
‚Ä¢ Febrile seizures: 0.2-0.5 mg/kg PR (rectal gel) if no IV
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.2-0.3 mg/kg IV (max 10 mg)
‚Ä¢ Muscle spasm: 0.12-0.8 mg/kg/day PO divided TID-QID
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred for emergencies), IM, PO, PR (rectal)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 5 mg/mL or 10 mg/2 mL ampoules
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give slowly (5 mg/min) to avoid respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ For IM: Painful, erratic absorption - avoid if possible
‚Ä¢ Rectal gel (Diastat¬Æ): 2.5, 5, 10, 15, 20 mg prefilled syringes
‚Ä¢ Diluent warning: Contains propylene glycol - risk of toxicity with high doses
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Drowsiness, fatigue, ataxia
‚Ä¢ Confusion (especially in elderly)
‚Ä¢ Dizziness, vertigo
‚Ä¢ Amnesia (anterograde)
‚Ä¢ Slurred speech
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (especially with rapid IV)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression/arrest (especially with opioids)
‚Ä¢ Paradoxical reactions (agitation, aggression)
‚Ä¢ Dependence and withdrawal syndrome
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol toxicity (high IV doses): acidosis, hemolysis, renal failure
‚Ä¢ Hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis
‚Ä¢ Teratogenicity (cleft palate if used in first trimester)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioids, alcohol, other CNS depressants: Potentiates respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Enzyme inducers (phenytoin, carbamazepine): Increased metabolism
‚Ä¢ Enzyme inhibitors (cimetidine, fluoxetine): Increased levels
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: May antagonize sedative effect
‚Ä¢ Probenecid: Slows metabolism
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Increased digoxin levels
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ Rapid redistribution: Initial effect lasts 15-30 minutes, but drug persists for days
‚Ä¢ Active metabolites: Nordiazepam has 50-100 hour half-life ‚Üí prolonged sedation
‚Ä¢ For status epilepticus: Lorazepam preferred (longer duration in brain)
‚Ä¢ Rectal route: Useful for children with seizures when IV not available
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol: Max 1 mg/kg/min to avoid toxicity (50 mg/mL contains 40% PG)
‚Ä¢ Flumazenil caution: Can precipitate seizures in epileptics or benzodiazepine-dependent patients
‚Ä¢ Elderly: Start with half dose, increased sensitivity
‚Ä¢ Storage: Protect from light, refrigerate rectal gel
üß† DIAZEPAM vs LORAZEPAM for SEIZURES:
‚Ä¢ Diazepam: Faster brain penetration (2 min) but rapid redistribution out of brain (15-30 min duration)
‚Ä¢ Lorazepam: Slower brain entry (5 min) but stays in brain longer (12-24 hours)
‚Ä¢ For status epilepticus: Lorazepam is first choice if available

3 Core Drug 2: Midazolam

MIDAZOLAM - WATER-SOLUBLE, SHORT-ACTING BENZODIAZEPINE

Drug class: Benzodiazepine (short-acting), imidazobenzodiazepine
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds GABA-A receptor ‚Üí increases chloride conductance ‚Üí neuronal hyperpolarization
‚Ä¢ Water-soluble at acidic pH (in vial), becomes highly lipophilic at physiological pH ‚Üí rapid CNS penetration
‚Ä¢ More potent than diazepam (3-4 times)
‚Ä¢ Anticonvulsant, anxiolytic, sedative, amnestic properties
‚Ä¢ No active metabolites (unlike other benzodiazepines)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (especially when no IV access - IM route)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation (endoscopy, cardioversion, fracture reduction)
‚Ä¢ Induction of anesthesia (adjunct)
‚Ä¢ Sedation in mechanically ventilated patients (ICU)
‚Ä¢ Premedication before surgery
‚Ä¢ Acute agitation (combined with antipsychotic)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Acute narrow-angle glaucoma
‚Ä¢ Severe respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Shock, hypotension
‚Ä¢ Allergy to benzodiazepines
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (relative)
‚Ä¢ Sleep apnea (relative)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - crosses placenta)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (IM): 10 mg IM (if >40 kg), repeat in 10 min if needed
‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (IV): 0.1-0.2 mg/kg IV (max 10 mg), repeat every 5-10 min
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 1-2.5 mg IV slow push, titrate every 2-3 min (usual total 2.5-7.5 mg)
‚Ä¢ ICU sedation: 0.02-0.1 mg/kg/hr infusion
‚Ä¢ Premedication: 0.07-0.08 mg/kg IM 30-60 min pre-op
‚Ä¢ Acute agitation: 2.5-5 mg IM with haloperidol 5-10 mg
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 0.2 mg/kg IM (max 10 mg) OR 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 10 mg)
‚Ä¢ Procedural sedation: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg IV, titrate (max 0.4 mg/kg total)
‚Ä¢ ICU sedation: 0.05-0.2 mg/kg loading, then 0.06-0.12 mg/kg/hr
‚Ä¢ Premedication: 0.25-0.5 mg/kg PO (max 20 mg) or 0.1-0.15 mg/kg IM
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV, IM, PO, intranasal, buccal, rectal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 1 mg/mL, 5 mg/mL (for injection); 2 mg/mL syrup (PO)
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give slowly over 2-5 minutes (respiratory depression risk)
‚Ä¢ For IM: Well absorbed (unlike diazepam) - use deltoid or vastus lateralis
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: 0.2-0.5 mg/kg (max 10 mg) - use mucosal atomizer device
‚Ä¢ Compatible with most IV fluids but may precipitate with alkaline solutions
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression (dose-related)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (especially with opioids)
‚Ä¢ Hiccups (common with IV administration)
‚Ä¢ Paradoxical reactions (agitation, especially in children/elderly)
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Headache, dizziness
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Apnea, respiratory arrest
‚Ä¢ Severe hypotension, cardiac arrest
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol toxicity with high-dose infusions (similar to diazepam)
‚Ä¢ Withdrawal seizures with abrupt discontinuation after prolonged infusion
‚Ä¢ Tolerance with long-term use
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioids: Synergistic respiratory depression (reduce doses by 30-50%)
‚Ä¢ Protease inhibitors (ritonavir): Markedly increased levels
‚Ä¢ Erythromycin, diltiazem, verapamil: Increased levels
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: May antagonize sedative effect
‚Ä¢ Alcohol, other CNS depressants: Additive effects
‚Ä¢ Flumazenil: Antagonizes effects
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ IM route advantage: Well absorbed, rapid onset (5-15 min) - excellent for status epilepticus without IV
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 1.5-3.5 hours (short) - ideal for procedural sedation
‚Ä¢ Context-sensitive half-time: Increases with prolonged infusion (accumulates)
‚Ä¢ No active metabolites - better for elderly, liver disease
‚Ä¢ Blue urine: May turn urine blue-green (normal, from dye)
‚Ä¢ For RSI: Often used with ketamine or etomidate (0.1-0.3 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Pediatric sedation: Intranasal route popular for laceration repair (0.2-0.3 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ ICU sedation: Monitor for propylene glycol toxicity with high doses (>1 mg/kg/hr)

4 Core Drug 3: Lorazepam

LORAZEPAM - FIRST-LINE FOR STATUS EPILEPTICUS

Drug class: Benzodiazepine (intermediate-acting)
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Binds GABA-A receptor at Œ±-Œ≥ subunit interface ‚Üí enhances GABA effects
‚Ä¢ Increases chloride influx ‚Üí neuronal hyperpolarization ‚Üí reduced excitability
‚Ä¢ Less lipophilic than diazepam ‚Üí slower brain entry but longer duration in CNS
‚Ä¢ No active metabolites (unlike diazepam)
‚Ä¢ Anticonvulsant, anxiolytic, sedative, amnestic properties

‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal seizures and delirium tremens
‚Ä¢ Anxiety/agitation (especially in medically ill patients)
‚Ä¢ Chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting (adjunct)
‚Ä¢ Premedication before surgery or procedures
‚Ä¢ Insomnia (short-term)
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Severe respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Sleep apnea (especially untreated)
‚Ä¢ Acute narrow-angle glaucoma
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment (may precipitate encephalopathy)
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (relative)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (especially first trimester)
‚Ä¢ Allergy to benzodiazepines or polyethylene glycol

‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal: 1-4 mg IV/PO, repeat every 1-2 hours until calm (CIWA protocol)
‚Ä¢ Anxiety/agitation: 0.5-2 mg IV/IM/PO, repeat every 4-6 hours as needed
‚Ä¢ Premedication: 0.05 mg/kg IM (max 4 mg) 2 hours pre-op
‚Ä¢ Chemotherapy N/V: 1.5 mg/m¬≤ IV (max 3 mg) before chemotherapy

‚Ä¢ Anxiety/premedication: 0.05 mg/kg PO/IV (max 2 mg)
‚Ä¢ Insomnia: 0.05 mg/kg PO at bedtime (max 2 mg)
‚Ä¢ Chemotherapy N/V: 0.05 mg/kg IV before chemo
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred for emergencies), IM, PO, sublingual
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 2 mg/mL or 4 mg/mL vials; 0.5, 1, 2 mg tablets
‚Ä¢ For IV: Dilute with equal volume of sterile water/NS, give over 2-5 minutes
‚Ä¢ For IM: Well absorbed but slower onset than IV (15-30 min)
‚Ä¢ Contains polyethylene glycol and benzyl alcohol (caution in neonates)
‚Ä¢ Stable for 24 hours when diluted
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Sedation, drowsiness
‚Ä¢ Dizziness, ataxia (especially in elderly)
‚Ä¢ Weakness
‚Ä¢ Amnesia (desirable for procedures)
‚Ä¢ Blurred vision
‚Ä¢ Paradoxical reactions (rare)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression/arrest (especially with opioids)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (with rapid IV administration)
‚Ä¢ Hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis
‚Ä¢ Benzodiazepine withdrawal syndrome
‚Ä¢ Dependence with long-term use
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol toxicity with high doses (similar to diazepam)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioids, alcohol, other CNS depressants: Potentiates respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Valproate: May increase lorazepam levels
‚Ä¢ Probenecid: Increases levels by inhibiting glucuronidation
‚Ä¢ Oral contraceptives: May increase levels
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: May reduce sedative effect
‚Ä¢ Flumazenil: Reverses effects (use with caution in epilepsy)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ FIRST CHOICE for status epilepticus - longer duration in brain than diazepam
‚Ä¢ Onset: 2-5 minutes IV, duration 12-24 hours for seizure control
‚Ä¢ No active metabolites - safer in elderly, liver disease
‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal: Preferred due to longer duration, less abuse potential
‚Ä¢ CIWA protocol: Symptom-triggered dosing reduces total benzodiazepine use
‚Ä¢ IM absorption: Good but slower (15-30 min) - not ideal for status epilepticus
‚Ä¢ Stability: Degrades rapidly in plastic syringes - draw up just before use
‚Ä¢ Sublingual: Good alternative when IV/IM not possible (80% bioavailability)
üß† BENZODIAZEPINE COMPARISON FOR STATUS EPILEPTICUS:
‚Ä¢ Lorazepam: Onset 2-5 min, duration 12-24h - FIRST CHOICE
‚Ä¢ Midazolam (IM): Onset 5-15 min, duration 1-2h - BEST if no IV access
‚Ä¢ Diazepam: Onset 2 min, duration 15-30 min - rapid but short (needs second agent)

5 Core Drug 4: Phenytoin / Fosphenytoin

PHENYTOIN & FOSPHENYTOIN - SECOND-LINE FOR STATUS EPILEPTICUS

Drug class: Hydantoin anticonvulsant (phenytoin); phosphate ester prodrug (fosphenytoin)
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Blocks voltage-dependent sodium channels ‚Üí reduces neuronal excitability
‚Ä¢ Inhibits high-frequency repetitive firing of neurons (use-dependent blockade)
‚Ä¢ Stabilizes neuronal membranes
‚Ä¢ May also affect calcium channels and neurotransmitter release
‚Ä¢ Fosphenytoin: Water-soluble prodrug converted to phenytoin by phosphatases
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (second-line after benzodiazepines)
‚Ä¢ Prophylaxis/treatment of tonic-clonic and partial seizures
‚Ä¢ Neurosurgical prophylaxis (post-craniotomy)
‚Ä¢ Trigeminal neuralgia (second-line)
‚Ä¢ Digitalis-induced arrhythmias (historical use)
‚Ä¢ Fosphenytoin preferred due to better safety profile
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Sinus bradycardia, SA block, AV block
‚Ä¢ Adams-Stokes syndrome
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to phenytoin, fosphenytoin, or other hydantoins
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (teratogenic - fetal hydantoin syndrome)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Hepatic impairment, porphyria
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (phenytoin): 20 mg/kg IV at max 50 mg/min
‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus (fosphenytoin): 20 mg PE/kg IV at 150 mg PE/min
‚Ä¢ Loading (non-emergent): 15-20 mg/kg IV/PO
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 300-400 mg/day PO/IV (5 mg/kg/day)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic level: 10-20 mg/L (toxic >20 mg/L)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 20 mg/kg IV (phenytoin) or 20 mg PE/kg (fosphenytoin)
‚Ä¢ Loading: 15-20 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 5-10 mg/kg/day divided BID-TID
‚Ä¢ Neonates: Loading 15-20 mg/kg, maintenance 4-8 mg/kg/day
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Phenytoin: IV ONLY (never IM - painful, erratic absorption)
‚Ä¢ Fosphenytoin: IV or IM (well tolerated IM)
‚Ä¢ Phenytoin IV: Dilute in NS only (precipitates in dextrose)
‚Ä¢ Infusion rate: Phenytoin max 50 mg/min, fosphenytoin max 150 mg PE/min
‚Ä¢ IM fosphenytoin: Divide dose into multiple sites if >10 mL total
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Nystagmus (earliest sign of toxicity)
‚Ä¢ Ataxia, dizziness
‚Ä¢ Slurred speech
‚Ä¢ Gingival hyperplasia (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Hirsutism (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Coarsening of facial features (chronic use)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular collapse (with rapid IV phenytoin - "purple glove syndrome")
‚Ä¢ Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis
‚Ä¢ Drug rash with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS)
‚Ä¢ Hepatotoxicity
‚Ä¢ Blood dyscrasias (aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis)
‚Ä¢ Cerebellar atrophy with chronic toxicity
‚Ä¢ Teratogenicity (fetal hydantoin syndrome)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin: Complex interaction (monitor INR)
‚Ä¢ Oral contraceptives: Reduced efficacy
‚Ä¢ Isoniazid, chloramphenicol, cimetidine: Increased levels
‚Ä¢ Carbamazepine, phenobarbital: Decreased levels
‚Ä¢ Valproate: Displaces from protein binding, complex interaction
‚Ä¢ Alcohol: Acute use increases levels, chronic decreases
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ FOSPHENYTOIN PREFERRED: Water-soluble, can give faster, IM option, less tissue damage
‚Ä¢ Purple glove syndrome: Limb edema, discoloration, pain with phenytoin extravasation
‚Ä¢ PE = Phenytoin Equivalent: 1.5 mg fosphenytoin = 1 mg phenytoin
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic monitoring: Check level 2 hours after IV load, then periodically
‚Ä¢ Non-linear kinetics: Small dose increases can cause large level increases
‚Ä¢ Free vs total levels: Measure free phenytoin in renal failure, hypoalbuminemia
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 24 hours initially, increases with chronic use (up to 60 hours)
‚Ä¢ Antidote for overdose: Multiple-dose activated charcoal, supportive care

6 Core Drug 5: Phenobarbital

PHENOBARBITAL - OLD BUT EFFECTIVE ANTICONVULSANT

Drug class: Barbiturate (long-acting)
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Potentiates GABA activity by increasing chloride channel opening duration
‚Ä¢ Also has direct GABA-mimetic effect at high doses
‚Ä¢ Reduces glutamate-induced excitotoxicity
‚Ä¢ Suppresses neuronal excitability throughout CNS
‚Ä¢ Induces hepatic microsomal enzymes (P450 induction)

‚Ä¢ Neonatal seizures (first-line in some centers)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal (severe cases)
‚Ä¢ Sedation in mechanically ventilated patients (historical)
‚Ä¢ Prevention of seizures in eclampsia (with magnesium)
‚Ä¢ Hyperbilirubinemia in neonates (enzyme induction)

‚Ä¢ Severe respiratory depression
‚Ä¢ Severe hepatic impairment
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to barbiturates
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - neonatal withdrawal)
‚Ä¢ Myasthenia gravis (relative)

‚Ä¢ May repeat: 5-10 mg/kg every 15-30 min (max total 30-40 mg/kg)
‚Ä¢ Alcohol withdrawal: 130-260 mg IV/IM, then 30-60 mg TID-QID
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 1-3 mg/kg/day (60-200 mg/day)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic level: 15-40 mg/L (toxic >40 mg/L)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Status epilepticus: 20 mg/kg IV at 1-2 mg/kg/min
‚Ä¢ Neonatal seizures: 20 mg/kg IV load, then 3-5 mg/kg/day
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 3-8 mg/kg/day divided BID
‚Ä¢ Hyperbilirubinemia: 5-10 mg/kg/day √ó 3-7 days
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (preferred), IM, PO
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 30 mg/mL, 60 mg/mL, 130 mg/mL ampoules
‚Ä¢ For IV: Dilute in equal volume of sterile water, give slowly (max 100 mg/min)
‚Ä¢ IM: Painful, can cause sterile abscess - avoid if possible
‚Ä¢ Contains propylene glycol (risk of toxicity with rapid infusion)
‚Ä¢ Compatible with most IV solutions but best in NS
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Sedation, drowsiness, lethargy
‚Ä¢ Ataxia, nystagmus
‚Ä¢ Cognitive impairment
‚Ä¢ Respiratory depression (dose-related)
‚Ä¢ Hypotension (especially with rapid IV)
‚Ä¢ Enzyme induction (affects many drugs)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Severe respiratory depression/apnea
‚Ä¢ Cardiovascular collapse
‚Ä¢ Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare)
‚Ä¢ Megaloblastic anemia (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Osteomalacia (chronic use - vitamin D metabolism)
‚Ä¢ Dependence and withdrawal syndrome
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol toxicity (high doses)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Warfarin, oral contraceptives, steroids: Reduces efficacy (enzyme induction)
‚Ä¢ Valproate: Increases phenobarbital levels
‚Ä¢ Alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids: Additive CNS depression
‚Ä¢ Theophylline: Reduced levels
‚Ä¢ Chloramphenicol, isoniazid: Increased phenobarbital levels
‚Ä¢ Doxycycline: Reduced levels
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ For refractory status: Often requires intubation and ventilation
‚Ä¢ Onset: 5 minutes IV, peak 30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 80-120 hours (long!) - accumulates with repeated doses
‚Ä¢ Neonates: First-line in some centers (better evidence than phenytoin)
‚Ä¢ Therapeutic monitoring: Check levels 2-4 hours after load, then weekly
‚Ä¢ Withdrawal: Taper slowly over weeks to avoid seizures
‚Ä¢ Cost: Very inexpensive compared to newer agents
‚Ä¢ Propylene glycol: Max 1 mg/kg/min to avoid toxicity (similar to diazepam)

7 Core Drug 6: Mannitol

MANNITOL - OSMOTIC AGENT FOR RAISED ICP

Drug class: Osmotic diuretic, sugar alcohol
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Creates osmotic gradient ‚Üí draws water from brain tissue into plasma
‚Ä¢ Reduces brain volume and intracranial pressure (ICP)
‚Ä¢ Improves cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP - ICP)
‚Ä¢ Free radical scavenger (neuroprotective)
‚Ä¢ Induces diuresis by osmotic effect in renal tubules
‚Ä¢ May improve blood rheology (reduces blood viscosity)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Raised intracranial pressure (traumatic brain injury, stroke, tumor)
‚Ä¢ Cerebral edema (vasogenic and cytotoxic)
‚Ä¢ Prevention/treatment of acute renal failure (especially rhabdomyolysis)
‚Ä¢ Glaucoma (to reduce intraocular pressure)
‚Ä¢ Diuresis in drug overdose (forced diuresis)
‚Ä¢ Diagnostic agent for oliguric renal failure
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Anuria (if not from dehydration)
‚Ä¢ Severe pulmonary edema/congestive heart failure
‚Ä¢ Active intracranial bleeding (relative - may expand hematoma)
‚Ä¢ Severe dehydration
‚Ä¢ Metabolic edema (toxic ingestion)
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to mannitol
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Raised ICP: 0.25-1 g/kg IV over 20-30 minutes (usual 1 g/kg)
‚Ä¢ Repeat doses: 0.25-0.5 g/kg every 4-8 hours as needed
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 0.25-0.5 g/kg every 4-6 hours (keep serum osmolality 300-320 mOsm/kg)
‚Ä¢ Renal protection: 25 g IV, then infusion to maintain urine output >100 mL/hr
‚Ä¢ Glaucoma: 1.5-2 g/kg IV over 30-60 minutes
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Raised ICP: 0.25-1 g/kg IV over 20-30 minutes
‚Ä¢ Maintenance: 0.25-0.5 g/kg every 4-8 hours
‚Ä¢ Renal protection: 0.5-1 g/kg, then 0.1-0.2 g/kg/hr
‚Ä¢ Test dose: 0.2 g/kg over 3-5 min to assess renal function
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Route: IV infusion only (never IM or SC)
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 10%, 20%, 25% solutions (10% = 100 mg/mL, 20% = 200 mg/mL)
‚Ä¢ Use filter needle to draw up (crystals may form)
‚Ä¢ Warm solution to dissolve crystals if present
‚Ä¢ Administer through large-bore IV (phlebitis risk)
‚Ä¢ Use infusion pump for accurate rate control
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Headache, nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Dry mouth, thirst
‚Ä¢ Blurred vision
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary congestion (fluid shift)
‚Ä¢ Diuresis (expected)
‚Ä¢ Injection site pain/phlebitis
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Fluid and electrolyte imbalance (dehydration, hypernatremia)
‚Ä¢ Congestive heart failure/pulmonary edema (fluid overload)
‚Ä¢ Renal failure (osmotic nephrosis)
‚Ä¢ Rebound intracranial hypertension
‚Ä¢ Cerebral herniation (if given too rapidly with mass lesion)
‚Ä¢ Anaphylaxis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Rhabdomyolysis (with extravasation)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other diuretics: Additive effects
‚Ä¢ Lithium: Increased renal excretion
‚Ä¢ Digoxin: Toxicity if hypokalemia occurs
‚Ä¢ Nephrotoxic drugs: Increased risk of renal failure
‚Ä¢ Neuromuscular blockers: May potentiate effects
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ Monitor: Serum osmolality (target 300-320 mOsm/kg), electrolytes, urine output
‚Ä¢ Osmolar gap: Useful to monitor effectiveness (measured - calculated osmolality)
‚Ä¢ Rebound effect: May occur as mannitol enters brain - use hypertonic saline as alternative
‚Ä¢ Test dose: Give 0.2 g/kg over 3-5 min, if no diuresis in 2 hours - renal impairment
‚Ä¢ For TBI: Use only if signs of herniation or ICP >20-25 mmHg
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 100 minutes (prolonged in renal failure)
‚Ä¢ Hypertonic saline vs mannitol: Hypertonic saline may have less rebound, better CPP improvement
‚Ä¢ Crystallization: Warm bottle in water bath if crystals present, do not use if cannot dissolve
üìä RAISED ICP MANAGEMENT: Mannitol FIRST for signs of herniation ‚Üí Hypertonic saline SECOND ‚Üí Sedation/analgesia ‚Üí Neuromuscular blockade ‚Üí Barbiturate coma ‚Üí Decompressive craniectomy

8 Core Drug 7: Haloperidol

HALOPERIDOL - TYPICAL ANTIPSYCHOTIC FOR ACUTE AGITATION

Drug class: Typical (first-generation) antipsychotic, butyrophenone
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Potent dopamine D‚ÇÇ receptor antagonist (particularly in mesolimbic pathway)
‚Ä¢ Also has some anticholinergic, antihistaminic, and alpha-adrenergic blockade
‚Ä¢ Reduces psychotic symptoms, agitation, aggression
‚Ä¢ Minimal sedative effects at low doses (unlike phenothiazines)
‚Ä¢ Anti-emetic effect (acts on chemoreceptor trigger zone)
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Acute agitation/psychosis (often combined with benzodiazepine)
‚Ä¢ Delirium (especially hyperactive)
‚Ä¢ Chemical restraint for violent patients
‚Ä¢ Nausea/vomiting (especially chemotherapy-induced)
‚Ä¢ Tourette syndrome, Huntington's chorea
‚Ä¢ Intractable hiccups
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Parkinson's disease, Lewy body dementia
‚Ä¢ Severe CNS depression, coma
‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to haloperidol
‚Ä¢ QT prolongation, torsades de pointes risk
‚Ä¢ Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (from previous antipsychotic)
‚Ä¢ Pregnancy (relative - crosses placenta)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Acute agitation IM: 2-10 mg IM (usual 5 mg), repeat every 30-60 min (max 20 mg/24h)
‚Ä¢ Acute agitation IV: 0.5-10 mg IV, repeat every 30 min (off-label but common)
‚Ä¢ Delirium: 0.5-2 mg IV/IM, repeat every 30 min until calm, then scheduled doses
‚Ä¢ Psychosis: 0.5-5 mg PO BID-TID, increase gradually
‚Ä¢ Nausea: 1-2 mg IV/IM every 4-8 hours as needed
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Agitation (adolescents): 0.5-2 mg IM, repeat every 30-60 min
‚Ä¢ Psychosis: 0.5-5 mg/day PO divided BID-TID
‚Ä¢ Tourette syndrome: 0.5-2 mg PO BID-TID
‚Ä¢ Not recommended in children <3 years except for severe conditions
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IM (preferred for acute), IV (off-label but common), PO
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 5 mg/mL ampoule (IM/IV), 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20 mg tablets
‚Ä¢ For IV: Dilute in 50 mL NS or D5W, infuse over 30 minutes
‚Ä¢ For IM: Deep injection, massage site
‚Ä¢ Lactate vs decanoate: Lactate for acute, decanoate for depot (every 4 weeks)
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Extrapyramidal symptoms (dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism)
‚Ä¢ Sedation (less than phenothiazines)
‚Ä¢ Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation)
‚Ä¢ Orthostatic hypotension
‚Ä¢ Weight gain (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ Hyperprolactinemia (galactorrhea, sexual dysfunction)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) - hyperthermia, rigidity, autonomic instability
‚Ä¢ Tardive dyskinesia (chronic use)
‚Ä¢ QT prolongation ‚Üí torsades de pointes
‚Ä¢ Seizures (lower threshold)
‚Ä¢ Agranulocytosis (rare)
‚Ä¢ Severe dystonic reactions (especially in young males)
‚Ä¢ Withdrawal dyskinesias
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Other CNS depressants: Additive sedation
‚Ä¢ QT-prolonging drugs: Increased arrhythmia risk (amiodarone, sotalol, TCAs)
‚Ä¢ Anticholinergics: May reduce extrapyramidal symptoms but worsen confusion
‚Ä¢ Levodopa, dopamine agonists: Antagonizes effect
‚Ä¢ Enzyme inducers (carbamazepine, phenytoin): Reduced levels
‚Ä¢ Enzyme inhibitors (fluoxetine): Increased levels
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "5 and 2" protocol: Haloperidol 5 mg IM + Lorazepam 2 mg IM for acute agitation
‚Ä¢ QT monitoring: Check ECG before and after high doses (>10 mg)
‚Ä¢ Extrapyramidal symptoms: Treat with benztropine 1-2 mg IM/IV or diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV
‚Ä¢ NMS treatment: Stop drug, supportive care, dantrolene or bromocriptine
‚Ä¢ Elderly with dementia: Increased mortality risk - use lowest effective dose
‚Ä¢ IV use: More extrapyramidal side effects than IM
‚Ä¢ Half-life: 12-36 hours (long) - accumulates with repeated dosing
‚Ä¢ Delirium: Avoid in hypoactive delirium, may worsen confusion

9 Core Drug 8: Naloxone

NALOXONE - OPIOID ANTAGONIST FOR OVERDOSE

Drug class: Competitive opioid receptor antagonist
Mechanism of action: ‚Ä¢ Competitive antagonist at mu, kappa, and delta opioid receptors
‚Ä¢ Displaces opioids from receptors with high affinity
‚Ä¢ Reverses respiratory depression, sedation, hypotension
‚Ä¢ No opioid agonist activity (pure antagonist)
‚Ä¢ Rapid onset but short duration (shorter than most opioids)
‚Ä¢ Also reverses opioid-induced pruritus, urinary retention
Indications: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose (respiratory depression, coma)
‚Ä¢ Reversal of opioid effects post-operatively
‚Ä¢ Diagnostic: Suspected opioid overdose (therapeutic trial)
‚Ä¢ Opioid-induced pruritus (low dose)
‚Ä¢ Opioid-induced constipation (peripheral acting formulations)
‚Ä¢ Neonatal respiratory depression from maternal opioids
Contraindications: ‚Ä¢ Hypersensitivity to naloxone
‚Ä¢ Note: No absolute contraindications in opioid overdose (life-saving)
‚Ä¢ Relative: Physical dependence on opioids (precipitates withdrawal)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac patients (may cause pulmonary edema, arrhythmias)
‚Ä¢ Neonates of opioid-dependent mothers (use with caution)
Adult dose: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose: 0.4-2 mg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min (max 10 mg)
‚Ä¢ Post-op reversal: 0.1-0.2 mg IV every 2-3 min to desired effect
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 2/3 of initial effective dose per hour (e.g., 0.4 mg/hr)
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: 2 mg (1 mg per nostril) - pre-hospital use
‚Ä¢ Auto-injector: 0.4 mg IM (Evzio¬Æ) or 2 mg IM (higher dose formulations)
Pediatric dose: ‚Ä¢ Opioid overdose: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min (max 2 mg)
‚Ä¢ Neonates: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/SC, repeat every 2-3 min
‚Ä¢ Infusion: 0.0025-0.16 mg/kg/hr after initial bolus
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: 2 mg (same as adult - absorption varies)
Route & preparation: ‚Ä¢ Routes: IV (fastest), IM, SC, intranasal, endotracheal
‚Ä¢ Preparation: 0.4 mg/mL, 1 mg/mL ampoules
‚Ä¢ For IV: Give undiluted, rapid push for overdose, slow titration for post-op
‚Ä¢ For infusion: Mix 2 mg in 500 mL D5W or NS = 4 mcg/mL
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: Use mucosal atomizer device or prefilled device (Narcan¬Æ)
‚Ä¢ Endotracheal: 2-2.5 times IV dose diluted in 5-10 mL NS
Side effects: ‚Ä¢ Precipitated opioid withdrawal (agitation, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
‚Ä¢ Tachycardia, hypertension
‚Ä¢ Nausea, vomiting
‚Ä¢ Sweating, tremors
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (rare, especially in cardiac patients)
Adverse effects / ADR: ‚Ä¢ Acute opioid withdrawal syndrome (dangerous in opioid-dependent patients)
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema (non-cardiogenic)
‚Ä¢ Ventricular arrhythmias
‚Ä¢ Seizures (rare)
‚Ä¢ Cardiac arrest (rare, usually in patients with underlying heart disease)
‚Ä¢ Hypertension ‚Üí cerebral hemorrhage (especially in hypertensive patients)
Drug interactions: ‚Ä¢ Opioid agonists: Antagonizes effects (obvious)
‚Ä¢ Opioid partial agonists (buprenorphine): May require higher doses
‚Ä¢ Clonidine: May precipitate hypertension when reversing opioids
‚Ä¢ Flumazenil: Used together in mixed overdoses
‚Ä¢ Essentially no pharmacodynamic interactions (pure antagonist)
Emergency pearls: ‚Ä¢ "Start low, go slow" in opioid-dependent patients to avoid severe withdrawal
‚Ä¢ Duration: 30-90 minutes (shorter than most opioids) ‚Üí RE-DOSE OFTEN
‚Ä¢ Long-acting opioids (methadone, fentanyl patches): May need infusion for 24+ hours
‚Ä¢ Observe period: At least 4-6 hours after last dose (longer for long-acting opioids)
‚Ä¢ Buprenorphine overdose: May require high doses (2-10 mg) due to high receptor affinity
‚Ä¢ Intranasal: Takes 2-5 minutes to work vs 30-60 seconds IV
‚Ä¢ Pulmonary edema: Treat with oxygen, diuretics, PEEP; usually resolves in 24-48 hours
‚Ä¢ Harm reduction: Provide take-home naloxone to patients at risk of overdose
üíä OPIOID OVERDOSE TRIAD: 1. Respiratory depression (<12 breaths/min) 2. Pinpoint pupils 3. Decreased consciousness ‚Üí TREAT WITH NALOXONE

10 Condition-Specific Management

10.1 Status Epilepticus Management Protocol

üïí STATUS EPILEPTICUS TIMELINE:

0-5 MINUTES (STABILIZATION):
‚Ä¢ ABCDE, oxygen, IV access x2
‚Ä¢ Check glucose, treat if <4 mmol/L
‚Ä¢ Thiamine 100mg IV if at risk of deficiency
‚Ä¢ Consider toxicology screen

5-20 MINUTES (BENZODIAZEPINES):
‚Ä¢ Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4mg) OR
‚Ä¢ Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg IM (if no IV) OR
‚Ä¢ Diazepam 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 10mg)
‚Ä¢ Repeat once in 5-10 minutes if ongoing

20-40 MINUTES (ANTIEPILEPTICS):
‚Ä¢ Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg (max 150 mg PE/min) OR
‚Ä¢ Valproate 40 mg/kg (max 6 mg/kg/min) OR
‚Ä¢ Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg (max 4.5g over 15 min)

>40 MINUTES (REFACTORY):
‚Ä¢ RSI: Propofol 1-2 mg/kg OR Thiopental 4-5 mg/kg
‚Ä¢ Continuous EEG monitoring
‚Ä¢ Midazolam infusion 0.1-0.4 mg/kg/hr OR
‚Ä¢ Propofol infusion 1-15 mg/kg/hr (watch for propofol infusion syndrome)
‚Ä¢ Consider ketamine 1-3 mg/kg load, then 0.5-10 mg/kg/hr

10.2 Raised ICP Management

üß† RAISED ICP >20 mmHg:
1. Head elevation 30¬∞ (if spine cleared)
2. Normocapnia (PaCO‚ÇÇ 35-40 mmHg)
3. Mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg OR Hypertonic saline 3% 250 mL over 30 min
4. Sedation: Propofol 5-50 mcg/kg/min
5. Analgesia: Fentanyl 1-2 mcg/kg/hr
6. Neuromuscular blockade if agitated: Rocuronium 0.6-1.2 mg/kg
7. Barbiturate coma: Thiopental 5-10 mg/kg load, then 3-5 mg/kg/hr
8. Decompressive craniectomy (neurosurgical consult)
